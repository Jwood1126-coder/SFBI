## 1. Familiar (ch01-familiar.md)

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Present is deep winter in Pittsburgh. (1) Marcus, an aging bridge inspector, works a shift under a bridge with Lou, then comes home to Hazelwood and feels nothing for his wife Renata even though he recognizes every fact about her; on first read I couldn't tell if she was literally an impostor or if something was wrong with him. (2) Section two back-fills: ~4 months earlier he had an 11-day focused-ultrasound treatment for lifelong depression that cured the sadness but stripped his emotional recognition of Renata — "acquired Capgras"; here I briefly misread "the trial" as a court/legal matter before the ultrasound reveal corrected me. (3) A follow-up neurologist in Oakland says the condition is probably transient. (4) Over following weeks Marcus secretly "tests" himself (pulse oximeter, rereading her 1989-90 letters, hand on her side of the bed) with no response, while Renata, having quietly deduced it, "courts" him to make him fall for her anew. (5) Warmth returns in stages — first for the dog Duke (a Tuesday), then his mother (a Sunday in Munhall), with Ana phoning that night — but stalls at the one door, Renata. (6) After posting the Glenwood bridge on a Thursday, that night in a hard freeze he finally repairs the deferred back-stoop rail to spec and ends with his hand left on Renata's warm back. The forward present-line and the single clearly-marked flashback made the chronology easy to hold; my only real snags were the false "legal trial" beat and the section-one ambiguity about whether the "stranger" was literal.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** "the warmth that for thirty-one years had come into the room" (para 12) vs. the letters "from ... 1989 and 1990 ... the one that made Ana" (para 27) and the radio's "Adeyemi anniversary ... a decade of it now" (para 21)
  - *Issue:* The stated 31-year marriage cannot be squared with a daughter conceived in 1989-90 once the exoplanet-technosignature discovery and the focused-ultrasound depression cure fix the present in the 2030s — such a couple would have been together ~50 years, and Marcus, already working bridges in 1989, would be near 80 and still doing rope-and-hammer fieldwork under a deck.
  - *Defense:* The present year is never named, so a reader can hold the setting loosely and take "thirty-one years" as a warm, un-audited figure; and the two SF elements could be read as light garnish on an essentially ~2020 story rather than hard date anchors.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · orientation-gap]** "The trial was eleven days ... everyone kept saying it like it was nothing, eleven days, as though a thing that fast could not have cut deep" (para 19), resolved only at "On day eleven the caisson came up dry" (para 21)
  - *Issue:* On first reading "the trial" reads as a legal proceeding (a divorce or lawsuit), and the reader isn't told it means the clinical ultrasound treatment until four paragraphs later.
  - *Defense:* "Trial" deliberately doubles as clinical trial and ordeal, and the misread resolves quickly inside the same section — a controlled brief withholding, not a sustained gap, and the ambiguity itself does thematic work.
- **[cosmetic · ambient-decade]** Paper field book and block-print photo captions (para 2-3), print Post-Gazette crossword in pen (para 21), a radio murmuring in the waiting room (para 21), Ana reading a grocery list "down a phone line" (para 33), a clip-on pulse oximeter (para 25), nine paper pages of consent (para 15)
  - *Issue:* The lived-in texture is uniformly pre-2030s, with no 2030s personal-tech present anywhere in the domestic or work world, so the setting reads a decade or two earlier than its speculative furniture.
  - *Defense:* The protagonist and his circle are elderly people in analog trades, and the story explicitly frames the old tools as kept by choice (Lou's brass plumb bob is "useless now that a laser did it truer"; Lou's father worked "since Eisenhower") — the analog texture is characterization, which the audit rules count as a legitimate reason.

## 2. Because

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** FRAME (present, a 2030s winter): Dr. R. Ozan, a memory researcher at a Cleveland teaching hospital, runs "the coil," a device that shows whether an answer was retrieved (light dark) or built on the spot (amber). On a late-January Thursday she tests Frank Wozniak, a confabulation patient: his facts (fingers lost to a die in 1974, 41 years at the plant) read dark/retrieved, but every "why" reads amber/constructed. FEBRUARY: she and her student Priya run 41 healthy controls; reasons come up amber even in intact brains — the finding: nobody retrieves a "because," everyone builds it. Nine days later, alone, she tests herself on one sentence — "Mom asked me to stay away" — amber. BACKSTORY (20 years earlier): her mother Vera died of ovarian cancer at sister Diane's house over five months; the narrator visited only twice and for 20 years claimed the mother sent her away to her work. That spring she audits her datebooks, an answering-machine cassette, and Diane's hospice chart — no record of the mother asking her to go, but a line, "patient tearful, asking for R." MARCH: she drives to Diane's and confesses the excuse was her own confabulation; Diane, saying neither yes nor no, hands her coffee in their mother's worn measuring cup. On first read I was briefly unsure of the narrator's gender (resolved at "my own husband"), briefly thought the machine displayed a green light (see findings), and never fully learned what "the index" is — but the plot's chronology itself was clear throughout.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** Line 23: "the light stayed dark ... green, green, green" vs. line 5: "Lifted, the trace stays dark. Built, the amber comes up."
  - *Issue:* The device is established as two states — dark (retrieved) and amber (constructed) — but the retrieved state is then also called "green," so a reader briefly thinks the machine shows a green light rather than merely staying dark.
  - *Defense:* "Green" is reused consistently (line 37: "holds green," "comes back green") as a traffic-signal metaphor for pass/clear, so it reads as established authorial shorthand, not a claim that the instrument literally displays green.
- **[cosmetic · other]** Lines 51-55: "she waited" (51) shifts to "You didn't say ... you put it in my hands" (55)
  - *Issue:* Within the single closing scene Diane flips from third person ("she waited") to direct second-person address ("you"), a mid-scene pronoun switch that makes a reader re-check who "you" is.
  - *Defense:* The turn to "you" is a deliberate intimacy move: the confession scene becomes spoken directly to the sister, which is the emotional pivot of the ending.
- **[cosmetic · ambient-decade]** Line 17 "a phone was ringing and going to voicemail ... reporters call the department line"; also paper datebooks (39), a cassette answering machine (39), spiral notebook and laminated card (33)
  - *Issue:* The 2030s research-hospital world is almost entirely analog — reporters chasing a hot neuroscience story via department-line voicemail, paper datebooks, a cassette deck, spiral notebooks — with essentially no present-day digital texture.
  - *Defense:* Nearly every analog detail is motivated (the narrator's paper temperament, Diane the exact record-keeper, the forty-years-analog mother, aged Frank) and the story is thematically about records and retrieval; only the reporters-to-voicemail beat lacks a character reason, and even that reads as a plausible end-run around a stonewalling press office.
- **[cosmetic · orientation-gap]** Line 17: "the coil was the next index ... the box they wheel into the coma wards to decide who's home ... indexed low near the end"
  - *Issue:* "The index" — a culturally famous consciousness-measuring device the coil is repeatedly compared against — is never concretely explained.
  - *Defense:* The reader is given enough to grasp its function (a consciousness/awareness score used in coma and end-of-life decisions); withholding the rest is deliberate background texture that never impedes the plot, and is mystery-of-worldbuilding rather than a needed who/when/where.

## 3. Fluent

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** On first read I placed it as one Osaka winter. Aoi runs her late father's oil-fired bathhouse (fires the boiler at 2, opens at 4 with Mr. Ueno first); her American husband David comes home from the sake brewery at 7. Backstory unspooled: they met at the bathhouse counter eleven years ago, her father died that spring leaving her the boiler, David learned brewing wordlessly and never learned Japanese, and their nine-year-old daughter Mio translates for the whole house. It took me a couple of paragraphs to fix the where (Japanese terms, then "Kansai," then "Osaka" at line 17) and to work out that the "old woman" in the back room is David's American mother, arrived four winters ago after his father died and refusing to learn a word. Then: the January anniversary (Aoi salves David's cracked hands, Mio speaks his ritual line). A Thursday: Mio brings home school-issued translator "buds"; at supper they put them in and the beads expose the grandmother's true cruelty and Mio's years of kind mistranslation at once, breaking the household. Aftermath weeks: Mio stops translating, Aoi removes and later discards the beads, David goes silent. Late February: the sake finishes well; David comes bead-less to the bath and labors through his wrong ritual line unaided while Mio sings upstairs in her own mixed private tongue. I was never lost about events, only about the decade — no year is ever given and I only inferred near-future from the beads.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · ambient-decade]** line 17 "he could not order lunch without Mio"; line 47 "any voice that reached you came back a half-beat later in your own language"
  - *Issue:* The whole emotional engine — a cross-language couple with no way to speak directly for eleven years until a school hands out translator beads — ignores that real-time in-ear/phone translation is ordinary consumer tech in the 2030s (it exists in 2026), so both the world's texture and the beads' marvel status read a decade or two out of date.
  - *Defense:* The analog bathhouse and a couple who chose child-mediated intimacy can be read as a deliberate refusal of screens; the beads' real novelty may be their always-on ambient reach that catches the grandmother's wall-muffled muttering, which handheld translation would not; and a literary collection can posit them as newly issued.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** line 57 "eleven years of it, every kind lie she had made since she was four"; cf. Mio is nine (lines 3, 33) and the grandmother arrived "four winters ago" (lines 27, 49)
  - *Issue:* The durations pinned to Mio's translating do not reconcile: a nine-year-old cannot have made "eleven years" of lies, "since she was four" is only five years, and the grandmother-specific "soft grammar" (line 33) is dated to when Mio was four though its cause arrived only four winters ago, when Mio was about five.
  - *Defense:* "Eleven years" may be meant as the marriage-long household habit of ferrying kind versions (Aoi does it too, "everyone downstream of everyone") — an emotional totalization rather than arithmetic the reader is expected to add up.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** line 55 "Mio, who did not know the beads were in"; cf. line 48 "David put his in because Mio wanted him to"
  - *Issue:* Mio staged the beads going in moments earlier — demonstrated them, urged David to wear his, said "now they could just talk... and she wouldn't have to" — so stating she "did not know the beads were in" contradicts what she just did.
  - *Defense:* The line most likely means her reflex to translate fired before her mind could register that the beads had made it redundant — the tragic automatism ("her body did it before her mind could stop it") is the intended point, and "did not know" reads as didn't-register-in-the-instant.

## Adherence (Chapter 4)

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** The story's "present" is a loose composite of a few days at Stauffer's, an independent pharmacy south of Dayton, Ohio, rather than one linear day: habitual opening rituals; one specific closing night when Owen (the pharmacist-owner) pulls his wife Delia's profile; that evening at home; a general "slow afternoons" stretch working the adherence-scoring list; and a final Thursday near close ending with Owen dosing himself. Backstory assembles cleanly once the speculative premise lands: Pareon is an invented "pair-bond agonist," a marriage-maintenance drug, and both Owen and Delia were prescribed it (prior auth, appeal, diagnosis code, letter). Delia's last fill posted March 9 "the year before last," covered her into early June, but she actually stopped around the third week of May — she took 71 of 90 tablets and left 19, which Owen keeps and re-counts; OARRS confirms she quit statewide. The emotional now: the marriage is hollow, she is off the drug, he keeps taking his. I was only briefly lost — "Pareon" is named (line 13) before it's explained (line 19), so for a few paragraphs I read it as an ordinary drug; the premise clicked cleanly after. I was never lost on place (unmistakably Dayton) or on who Delia is (named immediately), but the decade is never stated, so I defaulted to present-day and only inferred "future" from the invented drug itself.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · illogical-progression]** "His own vial is in the drawer where he keeps it ... He shakes one into his palm ... He takes it." (lines 87-89), set against Delia's "Refills good through the following year, a year that has come and gone" (line 27) and Owen's spotless reconciliation ethic (line 37).
  - *Issue:* The story tracks Delia's Pareon down to lapsed refills and forensically prices the drug (prior auth, appeal, diagnosis code) for her and the new patient, yet never accounts for how Owen still obtains or is authorized for the nightly dose he swallows at the climax — a gap that sits oddly against his established obsessive count-reconciliation and rule-following.
  - *Defense:* The omission is almost certainly deliberate: the tragedy is precisely that Owen keeps dosing his half of a bond past its authorization and past the marriage's reality, and naming a valid prescription would deflate that image; Pareon is also non-scheduled (kept in the fast-mover bay, not the safe), so store stock is not reconciled to the tablet, leaving his self-supply plausibly invisible on purpose.
- **[cosmetic · ambient-decade]** The whole adherence apparatus: "on the slow afternoons he works the list. He calls the ones sliding under" (line 63); hand-sealed bubble cards (line 11); "the county paper folded to the jumble" (line 43); "the ten o'clock weather ... a wall of green coming up the radar" (line 57).
  - *Issue:* A story built entirely around 2030s medication-adherence scoring shows zero digital adherence technology (app reminders, smart pill caps, automated refill texts, connected dispensers for the group home) and no other forward texture, so the world reads present-day/2010s apart from the single invented drug.
  - *Defense:* The prompt's own analog-trade exemption applies squarely: Owen is deliberately an old-school independent pharmacist and Delia an analog older wife, and the story's explicit theme is human adherence settled 'in a kitchen at night' versus the cold measure — so foil-and-blister proof, phone-call outreach, and an ink jumble are characterization, not oversight.

## 5. Sibship

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** 1) The winter before: a Donor Transparency Act unseals 1980-onward sperm-donor records; the 41 offspring of Donor 148 ("Cohort 148," nine states) are served by certified mail — 19 say yes, 4 say no (one is suing the state), 8 open and ignore. 2) Greta — a career event planner who is herself one of the cohort ("the client was her") — takes on the reunion supper. March 1 she mails RSVP forms for the second Saturday in June; forms and dietary confessions trickle back; she books the Bendel barn near Granville, Ohio. 3) Mid-planning the donor, Larry Vance (retired anatomy professor, Tempe), writes asking "whether he might come"; the cohort debates via group chat; Greta takes the letter to figure his seat and vacillates, ultimately leaving him unseated. 4) Event day: 9am load-in for a 5pm supper; 19 siblings arrive from noon on; Larry arrives 5pm on the dot with a grocery sheet cake, is utterly ordinary, and leaves at 7:30 before the toasts; shy sib Marta had slipped out ~6:30 by the other door. 5) 9pm: crew strikes the tables; alone with her paper chart, Greta finds she had penciled Larry at the head (Table 1), erases him, and reseats him at Table 4 by the door — the closing reveal. Where I was briefly lost: the opening paragraph's dense wedding jargon before I grasped Greta's trade, and a momentary uncertainty about who was actually running things (Ruchi = registry coordinator/spreadsheet vs Greta = the professional doing venue and seating) — both resolved within a page.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** Line 92 "we're at 19 green, 2 amber" + line 168 "the two amber sibs who'd firmed to green," against 19 attendees (line 86 "Nine chicken, six walleye, four mushroom" and line 136 "nineteen times")
  - *Issue:* Nineteen green plus two amber that both later firm to green is twenty-one, yet exactly nineteen attend and only nineteen meals are counted, so two confirmed guests silently disappear.
  - *Defense:* RSVP attrition is the story's stated theme ("RSVPs came the way RSVPs come, which is not the way people promise they will"), so two greens quietly dropping before the day reads as realistic churn a reader may absorb without objecting; and the recurring 19 is so insistent that most readers take it as the settled number rather than doing the addition.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** Line 84 "until the state told her she had thirty-some" versus "forty-one" stated at lines 5, 122, 126, and 168
  - *Issue:* The cohort is called forty-one at least four times, but Greta once describes her siblings as "thirty-some," undercounting by roughly ten people.
  - *Defense:* It sits inside Greta's loose, emotional interior voice rather than in a ledger, and can be read as her counting only the roughly thirty-one who actually engaged with the letter (19 yes + 4 no + 8 who opened it), not the full forty-one the Act shook loose.

## 6. Conjunction

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Present night, a Colorado orbital-conjunction screening floor: at 22:00 Noor takes the handoff from Reyes and becomes the sole certified "signer of record" until 06:00, working a queue of 19 close-approach "boxes," one of which is a yellow on the crewed platform Halcyon (a '33-clearance fragment). A flagged flashback — "The night it happened" — establishes a past: her chess friendship with Ade, a flight engineer on the older platform Vireo, and the night a '33-fragment conjunction on Vireo screened at Pc 4×10⁻⁵ (below the 10⁻⁴ line), she called MONITOR, and at closest approach 04:41 something catastrophic occurred; the outcome is never stated, but I inferred (and later confirmed) a collision destroyed Vireo and killed Ade. Back in the present, the 02:20 CDM shoves Halcyon over the line (Pc 4×10⁻⁴, 9 aboard, TCA 05:29); interleaved with the 11-week review board that ruled her Ade-night determination correct, she now re-runs the same scenario — tasking wide, coordinating with Halcyon's director Okwú (burn ready, deadline 03:52), driving the Pc down over successive passes (02:41→2×10⁻⁴, 02:58→1.3×10⁻⁴, 03:10 weather-stalled, 03:31→7×10⁻⁵), and at 03:47:52 signing NO MANEUVER. My only first-read fog: catching the tense-shift into the past section, and holding Ade's death as an inference for a paragraph until "no one across the board" and "brought the module down" confirmed it; the two timelines are otherwise cleanly signposted.

- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** line 87 "The queue behind Halcyon has grown to nine" vs line 99 "The count reads seven."
  - *Issue:* The queue reads nine behind Halcyon while she holds on the one box, then reads seven after she signs Halcyon, even though she clears nothing in between.
  - *Defense:* Conjunction queues churn continuously — new CDMs arrive and boxes fall off as their TCAs pass — so the two figures read as snapshots of a moving queue, not a running tally the reader is meant to reconcile; almost no first reader tracks the count across the climax.
- **[cosmetic · ambient-decade]** lines 23-27, "words came out of her downlink allowance"; "four lines of downlink she must have hoarded against her allowance"
  - *Issue:* Crew on a 400 km platform ration communication down to single-word text moves, which reads like early-ISS bandwidth scarcity rather than a 2030s-40s laser-linked, high-bandwidth orbital comms environment.
  - *Defense:* Personal/crew comms allowances (crew time, privacy windows, policy quotas) plausibly persist regardless of raw bandwidth, and the scarcity is doing explicit emotional work — making each chess move a rationed 'I'm still here' — so it reads as a deliberate characterization device rather than a missing-tech oversight.
- **[cosmetic · orientation-gap]** line 31 "What the floor did at 04:41 she holds in pieces"; line 33 "brought the module down eleven weeks later"; line 35 "no one across the board to play it against"
  - *Issue:* The load-bearing fact that a collision destroyed Vireo and killed Ade is never stated outright, only implied, so a fast first reader assembles the death by inference and can briefly be unsure it happened at all.
  - *Defense:* The obliqueness is clearly deliberate — the death rendered by absence — and multiple converging signals (the module deorbited, the full evidentiary review with her PIN-time in evidence, 'no one across the board,' the photo that 'then did not' stay taped) make it reliably reconstructable; this is MEANING-restraint executed well, not a genuinely withheld orientation fact.

## 7. Deorbit (ch07-deorbit.md)

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** A solo commander, Yuki, spends her last ~nine days aboard the aging station Meridian, methodically decommissioning it for a deorbit that will sink the wreck at Point Nemo weeks after she leaves in a capsule. Across dated ground-loop chats (GMT 128 to 136) with day-capcom Dale Marsh and night-capcom Priya Menon, she inventories every object as "return" or "disposal," repeatedly shuttling a mysterious 31-gram gray cylinder between the two columns. She confides she "hasn't decided" to come home, takes private calls with her daughter Denver, and has smuggled up a cardigan and a tin of tea that once belonged to someone she shared a house with. On first read I was genuinely unsure for most of the story what the cylinder, cardigan, and tea signified, and whether "not coming down" was literal suicide; only near the end did I settle on reading the cylinder as a dead partner's ashes and the cardigan/tea as their effects. She resolves it by packing the cardigan to ride home (keep) and leaving the cylinder velcroed in the cupola facing Earth to burn with the station (release), then closes the capsule hatch on undock day. The chronology itself is linear and easy to follow; the withheld facts are about meaning, not sequence.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · illogical-progression]** GMT 130 / 04:52 — 'I haven't decided I'm coming down.' answered with '...Copy that, Meridian.' and 'nothing's different till you say different'; reinforced by Onyx's open-ended 'There's no clock on you from the ground.'
  - *Issue:* A solo commander signaling she may not return home draws total equanimity from the ground — no flight surgeon on the loop, no family or backup-crew escalation, just granted open-ended time — whereas a real Mission Control would treat that statement as an emergency.
  - *Defense:* The story's entire subject is the ground choosing dignity and trust over protocol, and 'coming down' may be existential (making peace with leaving) rather than an operational refusal, so nothing formally trips a medical/safety procedure.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** GMT 135 header over the section beginning 'By day seven Meridian was mostly dark.'
  - *Issue:* The 'day seven' section is dated GMT 135, but the other day-labels fix day one at GMT 128 (day three = 130, day five = 132, undock/'the ninth' = 136); by that scheme day seven is GMT 134, so 135 is actually day eight.
  - *Defense:* The day counts may be loose narrative markers rather than exact GMT indices, and closeout clearly began before the depicted GMT 128 morning (14 line-drains already logged overnight), so 'day N of closeout' need not map linearly onto GMT day-of-year.
- **[cosmetic · ambient-decade]** Throughout — e.g. bleeding a line while 'reading the pressure off the gauge... and calling the numbers to the floor in Houston'; 'holding each thing to the barcode reader'; voice-only Space-to-Ground loops and physical cargo bags.
  - *Issue:* For a story slotted into a 2029-2044 anthology, the prose offers almost no decade anchor and no year; the operational texture (analog gauge read aloud, barcode-reader inventory against a laptop manifest, voice-only ground loops) reads like 2010s NASA ops rather than the 2030s-2040s.
  - *Defense:* Human spaceflight is deliberately conservative, and Meridian is an ~11-year-old station whose hardware and procedures would date to its build era; the 'Cygnus signal vigil, fewer of them each year' detail also implies enough elapsed time to place the story later, so the analog texture reads as period-correct hardware, not anachronism.

## Chapter 8 — The Ellipse

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Aug 2031: Dell, an amateur stellar-occultation astronomer on a concrete pad east of the propane tank in Protection, Kansas, times a faint asteroid (later 2031 PK4) crossing an 11th-mag star in Capricornus; impact odds long, corridor 1900x210 km. She files the timing and forgets it. // ~2032-33: NASA declares the 2042 impact certain and the tightening corridor (720x130 km) walks across her county; a federal buyout program (officer Alcorn) offers to relocate the towns, appraising the Hartsook place at $61,500 (May 2033). Clay plants a cedar windbreak; both quietly refuse to sell. // 2035-2037: corridor tightens (340->150 km); doom-tourists and preachers trickle in; the school consolidates to Coldwater then closes; Dell learns three of the twelve controlling observations are her own 2031 timings — she helped aim the corridor at her own town — and keeps observing. // 2039-2041: corridor inside 60->9 km; households sell out; Alcorn visits once; a special hand-counted vote (450 ballots) goes 231-219 to remain open and depart together in spring 2042. Final image: Dell outside under Capricornus, the rock on schedule. // Where I was briefly lost: the dense opening delayed placing WHEN (fixed by the 24 Aug 2031 ledger) and WHERE (Protection, KS, a few paragraphs in), but the conceit resolved within the first section. I momentarily wondered whether the school closed in 2035 or 2037, and whether "three from '31" meant three 2031 nights when only one is shown.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** Alcorn visit: "thirty-year stock eight years in" — same sentence-cluster that dates the scene as "under the sugar bowl six years" (first offer 6 May 2033), and ledger 19 Mar 2033 "Clay's first cedar order in"
  - *Issue:* The cedars, planted spring 2033, are only about six years old at the 2039 visit (which the text itself dates as six years after the first offer), not the "eight" stated — "eight" is the correct 2041 figure ("eight springs back") misplaced into the 2039 scene.
  - *Defense:* Could pass as loose farmer's rounding in dialogue, but the ledger explicitly calls 2033 the "first" cedar order, so no earlier planting rescues the count, and the story is otherwise meticulous with numbers.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** Ledger 28 Dec 2037: "the Center's controlling-obs list still carries 3 of mine from '31"; also "Three of the twelve controlling astrometric points were hers"
  - *Issue:* Only one 2031 observation is ever shown or framed (24 Aug 2031, which "lay in the archive" as a single forgotten night), so "3 ... from '31" silently implies two more 2031 timings the story never establishes.
  - *Defense:* Strong: a freshly discovered near-Earth object stays observable for weeks, an occultation specialist would naturally catch it several times in its discovery apparition, and early-arc points are the most orbit-constraining — so three 2031 timings is realistic; "forgot it inside a week" reads as emotional shorthand for her habit, not a literal claim of one night.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · ambient-decade]** The outside-world presence across 2035-2041: "eleven tents," one film couple, bullhorn preachers, a lettered school bus, co-op selling out of bottled water — and no phones, social media, or livestreaming anywhere
  - *Issue:* A certain, precisely located asteroid impact on US soil with a decade's warning would draw a planet-sized, phone-saturated media circus in the 2030s; the depicted response is 1990s-sized and screen-free.
  - *Defense:* Strong: the tight elegiac rural POV deliberately tunes out spectacle ("the star did not care how many people had driven a long way"), a decade-long slow-motion certainty plausibly normalizes and drains media attention, and the digital 2030s IS present at the edges — Dell tracks the corridor on the Center's web page, county fiber, precision-time feeds, and Alcorn's tablet.
- **[cosmetic · orientation-gap]** "when the district let the school go" (2035 section) vs "the winter the school closed for good" (2037 section)
  - *Issue:* The school appears to close twice, which can briefly disorient a reader tracking the town's decline.
  - *Defense:* Strong and clearly intended: reconcilable as a 2035 partial consolidation (grades 7-12 bused to Coldwater while the elementary and two senior sections stay) followed by the final shutter around 2037; a careful reader sorts it out from the datelined minutes.

## 9. The Pollinators

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** February, near-future San Joaquin Valley. Bees are extinct, so a hand-pollination crew brushes Monterey pollen onto old Nonpareil almond trees at $11/tree; boss Ximena weighs the pollen and keeps the count. Over one working day (which I only later fixed as Tuesday): a pre-dawn weigh-in shows the tin short (38.6 of a marked 40); Vogel's 11am call sets the schedule — finish by Wednesday dark, then Gustine's Gonsalves block Thursday, rain Thursday; at lunch Lupe passes the rumor that old Chente (a former beekeeper) caught a live bee three days ago at the Firebaugh block and keeps it in a jar. The crew adopts the hope and works harder/happier through the afternoon. Evening fire and dinner; a radio report says an alien technosignature (the "Adeyemi Signature," five years old) has been confirmed. That night Ximena, unable to sleep, finds Chente alone with the jar — it is empty — and declines to expose it, giving him the day's honest tree-counts instead. Wednesday: fog lifts by 8:30, they finish the far corner by 3 with a gram to spare, break camp. Thursday dawn: they convoy up Highway 33 to Gustine, where the bee legend (texted ahead by Beto overnight) has already arrived — a stranger greets them as "los de la abeja."
Where I was briefly lost: (1) I did not register this as SF/future until the radio broadcast at line 85 — before that it reads as straight present-day realism. (2) Line 71 made me assume there was NO jar at all, so the physical empty jar at the climax was a small recalibration. (3) "Break camp in the last of the light" made me think they left Wednesday night, but the drive is actually Thursday at dawn.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** Line 71 ("there has never been a jar in it, or the weight of one") vs. the physical empty mason jar at lines 105, 127, 133
  - *Issue:* Line 71's flat certainty that Chente's coat holds no jar — and never has — primes the reader to conclude the whole bee-and-jar is pure invention, so the real empty jar produced at the climax reads as a mild reversal of an established fact.
  - *Defense:* Line 71 specifies 'in it [the coat]'; the jar actually rides in the cab / on the dash and Chente only mimes keeping it warm against him, so the sentence stays literally true and the empty-jar reveal is the intended, more-poignant turn (a real vessel, no bee). On a strict reading it is consistent, not contradictory — the seam is only that a first-time reader over-reads line 71 as 'no jar exists anywhere.'
- **[cosmetic · illogical-progression]** Line 131 ("They break camp in the last of the light" / "before the trucks even start") vs. line 133 ("ride the forty miles to Gustine in the morning" and Ximena seeing the jar "at dawn")
  - *Issue:* Breaking camp at dusk plus the 'before the trucks even start' phrasing implies a Wednesday-night departure, but the convoy actually pulls out at dawn Thursday, leaving it momentarily unclear whether they drove that night or slept over.
  - *Defense:* 'Break camp' can simply mean stowing the drum and comal and sleeping in the cabs at the turnrow, with departure at first light — standard for this crew — which reconciles both lines without any real gap.

## 10. Tonewood

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** FRAME: A guitar maker in his workshop, somewhere on a big-tide salt marsh, is talking to "you" — a wealthy buyer who flew in with a case to collect a guitar and asked whether "the salt" made the wood worth more. BACKSTORY unspools: a great-grandfather left 40 acres of slow-grown red spruce standing on an Acadian-dyked marsh; the father taught the narrator to rive tops with a froe and tune by tap-tone. I placed the setting (Bay of Fundy / Nova Scotia — "province," RCMP) fairly quickly but read it as PRESENT-DAY for roughly the first 85%. Two years ago the province scheduled the marsh for managed-retreat "realignment," due "spring after next" (two harvests expected); but ~1.5 years early, one April, the tide gate (aboiteau) was found standing open on a flood, salt drowned the ground, and over three weeks 40 acres died into a "ghost forest." Suspicion falls on the father (who hated the retreat) or brother Curtis (who wanted the buyout and knew scarcity would spike the wood's value); the father's froe turned up at the gate; the RCMP ruled "driftwood, undetermined." The narrator salvaged the standing-dead wood, demand exploded, he built and sold nine guitars, built a tenth from a special set-aside billet, then wired-back the buyer's full payment and kept it — playing it alone that night as the flood came in. Only the late satellite/dark-sky-preserve passage retroactively told me this was near-future, not now; I was also briefly unsure whether the "top" held at the opening was the finished guitar for sale or a loose demo plate (it's a loose plate — the finished tenth is on a wall hook).

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · orientation-gap]** Temporal cues arrive only near the end; first firm future marker at ~line 53 ("the strings of satellites you helped put up there," "four years down the list for the dark preserve out in Nevada").
  - *Issue:* The story gives no clear temporal anchor until roughly 85% in, so a reader who isn't told it's from a 2029-2044 collection reads it as contemporary literary fiction and may never register that it's set in the future at all.
  - *Defense:* The meditative, place-rooted voice and the deliberately analog luthier trade make an exact date immaterial to the story's meaning, and the satellite / dark-sky-preserve passage does eventually locate it in the near future; the softness may be intentional restraint rather than an omission.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · ambient-decade]** line 37: "the RCMP came out the once, wrote driftwood, undetermined, and closed it" (gate found open, line 31; wood suddenly worth a fortune, lines 47-49).
  - *Issue:* A 2030s tide gate that fails 1.5 years early — possibly sabotaged, and instantly converting 40 acres into a fortune — is investigated exactly once and closed "undetermined," with no trace of the ordinary present-day monitoring (trail-cams, dyke sensors, drone or satellite imagery, phone/location data) that would bear on when and how the gate opened.
  - *Defense:* The marsh is on a scheduled-abandonment ("realignment") list, so it is deliberately no longer maintained or watched, and its isolation is explicitly characterized ("the nearest witness is a heron"); the absence of tech is arguably a chosen fact of a place the state has already written off.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** line 63: the tenth guitar's billet is "twenty-two rings to the inch"; line 71 (that same guitar, that night): "my thumb found the twenty rings under the finish."
  - *Issue:* The tenth guitar's top is stated as twenty-two rings to the inch, then described eight lines later as twenty rings, so the same board carries two different ring counts.
  - *Defense:* "Twenty rings to the inch" runs through the piece as an ancestral refrain (line 57: what "my grandfather's father left standing"), so line 71 reads as the symbolic family figure the thumb reaches for rather than a literal recount, and 20 vs 22 sit within the same register; almost no reader counts.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** line 17: "Two hundred years ago the Acadians walled it off."
  - *Issue:* Acadian marsh-dyking and aboiteaux date to the 1600s-1700s (the Deportation was 1755), so "two hundred years ago" — roughly the 1830s from this frame, after the Acadians were mostly gone — is off by a century or more in a narrator who is otherwise fastidious about dates (1930, ring counts, tide timing).
  - *Defense:* In a folksy first-person voice "two hundred years ago" often just means "long ago," and the narrator is a woodworker recounting family memory, not a historian; the imprecision reads as vernacular rather than error, and only history-literate readers will notice.

## 11. Islanding

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Day 1, pre-dawn (5:53am): Junie, a former co-op lineworker in Bledsoe — a rural Ozark town ruled "uneconomic to serve" and cut off the grid two winters ago, now running its own solar/battery microgrid ("islanding") for two ~1-hour windows a day — air-tests her lineman's gloves; her partner Lissa, quietly boxing up her things to leave for "Dana's," goes during the 6–7am window to tend Verna, an oxygen-dependent elder up the lane. Day 1: Junie sounds ice-threatened poles with young Cody; at the 5:30–6:30pm supper window they eat as an ice storm sets in. Day 1 night: at 11:40pm a white oak limb drops the west lateral and Verna radios that her oxygen concentrator has "quit" (the one beat I could not reconcile with the town being dark at night); Junie can't climb an iced pole in the storm, so she de-energizes and grounds the downed line and Verna goes onto reserve cylinders — but the pole transformer is split and Junie has no spare. Day 2: Cody clears the limb, Junie splices the conductor; mid-afternoon an unmarked salvage transformer arrives via Boyd's silent, never-spoken arrangement; Junie sets and wires it. Day 2, 5:30pm window: she closes the cutout, the transformer hums, Verna's concentrator revives and the town lights — while Lissa's truck sits fully loaded and warmed, and Junie stands in the road between the lit house and the packed truck, Lissa's departure left open. It took me a couple of beats to grasp that Boyd was an ex-crewmate diverting co-op salvage, and to read the accumulating boxes (2 → 3 → 4 plus a crate of notebooks) as Lissa's mounting readiness to go.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · illogical-progression]** Dark Watch: "It came at eleven-forty... 'Junie. My machine's quit. My machine's quit and the light's off.'" — against "The island was off between windows, so the whole town primary lay dead."
  - *Issue:* Verna's electric oxygen concentrator and house light are dramatized as running and then failing at 11:40pm, but the story has hammered that the town has power only during two ~1-hour windows and is dead between them, so at midnight her machine was already off and she was already on cylinders (the earlier evening hours had even burned her "part gone" cylinder) — the tree cannot make a concentrator "quit" at an hour it was never powered.
  - *Defense:* A hurried reader reflexively accepts "storm knocked out her power," and the downed line does genuinely threaten the coming morning window's refill; but because the story trains the exact energy-budget scrutiny that catches this, no charitable reading (dedicated medical feed, house battery) survives, since either the primary is dead at night or a local battery wouldn't be cut by the line.
- **[cosmetic · ambient-decade]** Dark Watch: "A hand-crank GMRS Verna kept charged on the window ledge next to the transistor radio... and next to her husband's Elgin pocket watch"; base radio on the dresser; the "scanner crowd"; no phone or internet anywhere.
  - *Issue:* In a story set in the late 2030s, no one has a cell phone, smartphone, or satellite link, and a medically fragile, oxygen-dependent elder's only way to summon help at night is a hand-crank GMRS radio.
  - *Defense:* Strong: a county formally abandoned by the grid as "uneconomic to serve" would plausibly have lost cell backhaul too, the principals are elderly people in an analog trade, and infrastructure-independent GMRS is exactly the correct tool for a grid-down town — so the absence reads as premise, not oversight.
- **[cosmetic · other]** Cold Load: "all three boxes were up out of the bed and a fourth with them, and then Lissa carried the last of it out... and set it in the bed and roped the tarp back down over the load."
  - *Issue:* "Up out of the bed" momentarily reads as Lissa unloading the truck (a beat of staying) before "set it in the bed" reasserts loading (leaving), briefly muddying the truck's loaded/unloaded state — which is the story's load-bearing final variable.
  - *Defense:* May be deliberate: the flicker between unloading and reloading (now with more) mirrors the story's central suspension of whether Lissa leaves; or it is simply compressed packing choreography.

## 12. Absolution

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Reconstructed chronology (the story is told out of order): 2029, Lorraine is widowed and inherits her farm; ~6 yrs before the present the Vestry unit (a diocesan AI) is installed to lead evening prayer; ~11 yrs before, Ray arrives green from seminary and Lorraine begins keeping his altars. The prior fall (All Souls) she is diagnosed terminal; that January she returns the church key. Jan 29 at 2:43 AM a ground blizzard stops Ray three miles short of her house; she phones the parish line, the empty Chester church's unit answers and absolves her, and the unit auto-flags itself to the chancery; she dies by morning and Ray anoints her once the grader opens the road. The Tuesday after Candlemas he seals the log and switches the unit off. A tribunal in Great Falls sits from spring through July; the opening circuit scene is early August; rain ruins the wheat harvest mid-August; the decree (absolution invalid, sealed, Ray sworn to perpetual silence) is read the last Tuesday of August, and that same evening he keys the unit back on for evening prayer. Where I was lost on first read: through Sections 1-2 I did not yet know who Lorraine was or what the "matter" before the tribunal concerned — that resolves in Section 3 and is a deliberate reveal. I also first slotted the opening tribunal scene as the "week Tuesday" August session set up in Section 1, and only reslotted it to spring when Section 4 said the tribunal had already sat "three more times through June and July."

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · ambient-decade]** Throughout: "got on the CB and raised a man at Inverness" (l.33); "a police scanner he ran all day for the voices, the day's wheat bids chalked on a whiteboard" (l.53); paper mileage notebook; fountain pen; no phone beyond a landline
  - *Issue:* The only future-tech in the story is the Vestry AI; every other texture (CB radio, a police scanner used for market voices, chalk grain bids, a paper log, and a complete absence of cell phones or the internet anywhere) reads as pre-2010s rural America, and the sole date anchor "'29" reads just as easily as 1929, so a cold reader gets almost no signal this is the 2030s — and may not register the Vestry unit as near-future AI rather than an eccentric answering machine.
  - *Defense:* The Montana Hi-Line genuinely runs on persistent analog culture; every old artifact attaches to an old/traditional character (Alvin the widower with his scanner, Ray keeping a hunting-style ledger "because a road driven that many times you wanted to see the number of"), rural cell dead zones and blizzard tower loss are real, and the deliberate timelessness serves the theme of ancient priestly work in an unchanging country, with the Vestry unit as a single sufficient marker of futurity.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · orientation-gap]** The tribunal section beginning l.15 ("The tribunal sat in a room...", "The notary took his name") versus l.43 "The tribunal sat three more times through June and July"
  - *Issue:* The first tribunal scene carries first-visit cues (name taken, first bench wait, taking an anise drop "to have something in his hand") and directly follows the early-August opening, so a cold reader files it as the just-announced August session; only the later "three more times through June and July" forces a retroactive reslot of that scene to spring, a momentary misassembly of the chronology.
  - *Defense:* The achronological cut is deliberate and thematic — the summer collapses into identical repeated drives, "the ledger on the visor and the record in the sixty-four-degree room were the only two accounts he kept of that summer" — and the exact session order does not bear on the story's meaning and self-corrects within a page.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** l.37 "the Nunc Dimittis... the canticle of the second of February, three days off" against the decree at l.65 "The purported absolution of the twenty-ninth of January"
  - *Issue:* The confession, death, and anointing are all pinned to Jan 29 (the decree's date plus the same-morning death and grader opening), which makes Candlemas on Feb 2 four days off, not the "three days off" the text states.
  - *Defense:* "The storm came the last days of January" is deliberately loose, the phrase "three days off" carries a pointed Resurrection/third-day resonance and may be counted colloquially or inclusively, and no ordinary reader tallies the interval.
- **[cosmetic · illogical-progression]** l.35 the log "flagged the answer to the chancery on its own, unbidden... built to know the one thing it was forbidden and to raise its hand the instant it did it" versus l.45 "He could have let it lie... a letter asking that the file be closed for the peace of a dead woman... and it might have closed"
  - *Issue:* The unit is described as automatically and unstoppably reporting its own forbidden act to the chancery, yet the narrative then says a single letter from Ray might have quietly closed the whole file, which softens a mechanism built expressly to force the question into the open.
  - *Defense:* The automatic flag only opens the matter; canonical disposition of validity remains a human, discretionary act, so a priest's plea on behalf of a dead woman plausibly could close the inquiry — and the tension between the machine's built-in rigor and Ray's refusal to seek mercy is arguably the moral engine of the story.

## 14. Tacit

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Backstory first: Shigeru (79), last master of the Oyama carpentry line, is rebuilding the mountain gate at Reigen-ji temple a second time (he did it once at 38 under his father). The previous autumn he signed a consortium contract letting sensor gloves, an eye-tracking brow-band, and strain-gauged tools "capture" his craft into an AI model. His granddaughter Hana (34), his apprentice-heir after a brutal 6-year apprenticeship, drives the data-drives 40 min down the mountain twice weekly because there's no signal up top. During the build he cuts a bracket set over three days and on the third afternoon secretly makes one deliberate flaw (I paused here: how is it hidden from the sensors still on his body?). On a Thursday, engineer Aihara shows the model already reproducing his "mercy" (kigoroshi) and offers a perpetual license fee plus a "certified succession" certificate. About a week later, on a wet morning, he tests the model on unseen timber and it sets the flawless piece aside for the load-bearing arm exactly as he would — proving it learned the grain-reading he thought uncapturable. This breaks him. In November he refuses the succession certificate and instead wills the model to Hana "as a tool, never as an heir." Through winter and spring he declines and stops coming up; the gate is finished and blessed at a ceremony he's too weak to climb to. On his last strong day he sharpens and bequeaths Hana his lifelong paring chisel. His death is not stated outright — I inferred it from the decline and the bequest, then had it confirmed by the "forty-ninth-day service" (a Buddhist memorial rite) in June. The autumn after, Hana takes her first solo commission; the model sits dark and unused on her bench as she makes the first cut herself, echoing the opening. I was never seriously lost — the piece is near-linear — only briefly uncertain about the flaw-concealment mechanism, the exact year (never given), and whether the June service marked his death.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · ambient-decade]** Lines 7 & 17: "The nearest tower was down in the valley, forty minutes by the switchback road. On the mountain nothing reached." / "carry the drives down the switchbacks to the valley floor where the signal began."
  - *Issue:* A well-funded AI consortium in the 2030s relies on twice-weekly manual drive-courier because there is "no tower," with no mention of satellite internet that would trivially connect a remote mountain by then.
  - *Defense:* The no-connectivity premise is load-bearing: Hana carrying the record herself is her one tangible act and one of the story's central images. A mountain temple could plausibly forbid antennas or sit in a sky-blocked valley — though the text offers only "no tower," which is the pre-satellite framing.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · illogical-progression]** Line 33: "What he did not let the gloves see he did on the third afternoon, and he sent Kenji for water and set Hana to the whetstones with her back to him first."
  - *Issue:* He hides the deliberate flaw from "the gloves" by removing the two human witnesses, but the pressure-gloves and eye-tracking brow-band stay on his body, so sending people away would not blind the sensors that actually do the capturing.
  - *Defense:* He uses an uninstrumented knife rather than the strain-gauged chisels/plane, so the highest-fidelity channel misses it, and line 63 ("the one wrong thing... that the wire had never caught") confirms authorial intent that it stayed hidden — the staging is compressed rather than strictly contradictory.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** Lines 13 & 15: "fought six years... four of those years had been against the man"; "thirty-one when he finally let her past sweeping... thirty-four now."
  - *Issue:* The arithmetic doesn't close: four years of opposition plus three years at layout (31 to 34) is seven, not the stated six; and if the six years began at 28, the pre-layout "broom" period is only three, not four.
  - *Defense:* The figures read as emotional approximations ("six years," "four of those"), not an accounting the reader is meant to total; the felt duration, not the sum, is the point.

## 15. The Grain

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** The present action runs over roughly a week one February. At a multi-day plenary of a 400-person microwave-background survey (launched 2035), Meera keeps re-cleaning the all-sky map, but the quadrupole/octupole "alignment" — a direction in the universe — won't wash out. A hospice call pulls her to her dying father, a Kerala-born storefront pastor in NE Minneapolis; he asks to be turned to face the measured direction when he dies. Over the following days the collaboration concedes the result is real (Devlin's rival pipeline agrees to a degree) and drafts a measurement-only paper; Meera reruns the whole chain alone one night (run 41). On a later night she computes the bearing — azimuth 131, 20° up toward Virgo at 3 a.m. — and leaves an index card marked "TRUE, NOT MAGNETIC." Friday she gives the foregrounds talk; that night (~2:45 a.m.) her father's breathing fails and two orderlies, using a hobbyist's compass, orient the bed to 131. Where I was lost on first read: (1) briefly, whose deathbed — scene one's "he/his" isn't identified as her father until the next section (a clean one-section hold); (2) durably, the YEAR — the survey/embargo/"forty years preaching" cues read as ~2037, while "storm of '33" plus "eleven years" read as ~2044, and I never resolved it.

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** "a frame he had dusted with his sleeve for eleven years — her mother, gone since the storm of '33" vs. "The survey had launched in 2035" + "Two years the result had lived under embargo" and "had preached forty years from a storefront" (came "from Kerala in 1994")
  - *Issue:* The numeric anchors will not share a single 'now': the memorial photo (wife dead in 2033, dusted for eleven years) forces ~2044, while the survey launched-2035-plus-two-years-embargo and the father's forty years of preaching from 1994 both point to ~2037, so a reader who tracks the numbers cannot fix the year within about seven years.
  - *Defense:* If "launched in 2035" is read as only the mission's start and the anomaly reached significance/embargo years into a long integration, 2044 reconciles the death-and-photo timeline and fits a late-decade setting; "eleven years" and "forty years" can be taken as felt-grief and round numbers rather than audit-grade counts, with "gone since '33" as the true anchor — but nothing in the text tells the reader to make that reconciliation, and the survey framing leads them to ~2037 first.

## 16. Enough

**Timeline as a first reader assembled it:** Anneke, a district assessor of eleven years, opens a recorded interview with her own 78-year-old father, Willem Kraaijeveld, under the "Completed Life Act" — he is not ill but wants the state's help to die "while the setting of the day is still mine." She recused when his file crossed her desk but, the district being short-staffed, was kept on as lead assessor on condition an independent second (Doortje) co-signs. The story then walks the twelve required interviews, said to run four months and beginning in March: his ordered reasons (the works, his dead wife Rie, the house), consistency checks, a near-flag over grief, four other river files she carries, a dike-walk where his life's work is laid out (persuading river people to consent to flooding their own land, rooted in the 1953 and 1995 floods), and the slow signs he is winding the house down. The file closes clean; he sets a Tuesday. On that Tuesday a physician, Elly, ends his life at home; he takes Anneke's hand as it happens, river at plus twenty-four and falling. The following Monday she starts a new file in Woudrichem and says the same first six words. Where I was lost on first read: the year is never stated — I placed it at roughly 2044 only by arithmetic (29 in 1995, now 78); I tripped for a beat on "his grandson's name on the drawing"; and I could not reconcile a four-month, March-start assessment ending on a "Tuesday in March."

- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · internal-contradiction]** "it ran to twelve meetings across four months" (opening) + interview 1 "the river was low for March" vs "The day he chose was a Tuesday in March"
  - *Issue:* The twelve interviews begin in March and are stated to span four months, so the file's close and the chosen death day cannot also land in March.
  - *Defense:* "Four months" may name the Act's maximum window rather than elapsed time, and a March opening echoed by a March death could be a deliberate seasonal frame — but twelve dated interviews with explicit multi-day gaps (ten days to #2, a week between #5 and #6, Doortje's slipped afternoon) plus a post-close waiting period cannot compress back into March, and the river texture stays in one spring melt (30 to 24, falling), which quietly confirms the single-spring imagining the 'four months' line contradicts.
- **[would-puzzle-a-reader · ambient-decade]** granddaughter's "voice-notes he played on the little kitchen radio"; the district's "grey machine"; the wholly paper, pencil-and-hand assessment; year computes to ~2044
  - *Issue:* A story set circa 2044 carries almost no near-future texture; its world reads like the 2010s-2020s, including around its younger characters.
  - *Defense:* Strong for the father: Willem is an old analog dike-man in a rural river village who keeps old things, a life-or-death legal assessment plausibly mandates paper and a chain-of-custody recorder, and "the little kitchen radio" is his own endearing name for what is likely a smart speaker. The analog surface is largely characterized. The weak point is that nothing near-future surfaces anywhere at all — not around Anneke, not around 19-year-old Mirthe away in Nijmegen — so the decade never registers except by arithmetic.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** interview 7: "I put his ground under with his grandson's name on the drawing"
  - *Issue:* Willem is shown throughout as the engineer who led the Noordwaard works, so the drawing should bear his own name (his dead father's son's), yet "grandson" places the named drafter a generation too far down.
  - *Defense:* Could imply Willem's own son served as junior engineer of record on the plans, extending the family's water-trade lineage across three generations; many readers will absorb the line as generational continuity rather than a specific authorship claim and not stop on it.
- **[cosmetic · internal-contradiction]** interview 9 "an independent second has to be in the room in person for the closing meetings" and interview 11 "as the independent second must for the closing meetings" vs interviews 10 and 12, where Doortje is absent from the scene
  - *Issue:* The stated rule that the co-signer must physically attend the closing meetings is invoked at 9 and 11 but not honored at 10 or at 12, the actual final meeting.
  - *Defense:* Narration is selective and Doortje may be silently present; more plausibly "closing meetings" denotes only the second's required checkpoints (a joint session at 9 and the full file review at 11), after which she has co-signed, leaving 12 a lead-only formality with the applicant — a reading the text supports well enough that few readers will feel the seam.
