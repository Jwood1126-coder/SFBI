# PART I — WHAT THE INSTRUMENT SHOWS

A carpenter's level does not know what a house is. It knows one thing, tells it honestly, and the knowing stops at the bubble's edge. Every instrument is like this — built out of a question, faithful to it, blind past it. We have gotten very good at building them. We can measure a mind now the way we measure a footing: where it settles, what it bears, what it was never going to hold. What no gauge has ever read is the remainder — the part of a person that stands outside the number and watches it arrive.

The instruments in these stories work perfectly. That is the trouble.

## 1. Familiar

*Floor beam 14, fascia side: fatigue crack at the weld toe, 40 mm. Arrest hole drilled last cycle. Monitor.*

Marcus wrote the length in the field book before he wrote anything else — forty millimeters — and inked a small circle at the tip of the crack the way you cap a sentence. Eighty feet down the Monongahela went by the color of a nickel left on a sill. Lou ran the snooper — the bucket truck that walks an inspector out under a bridge's deck — from topside, easing the arm in under the fascia girder to where the road salt hadn't reached, and Marcus laid the flat of the hammer against the web and listened.

Sound steel rings. Bad steel takes the blow and keeps it. Floor beam 14 rang the length of its web, bright, all but the one joint, where for thirty winters a hairline had been opening about a thousandth of an inch a year, patient as a mortgage.

"Talking to you?" Lou said in the headset.

"Arrest hole's holding. Same forty."

"Then write your forty so I can bring you in. I lost my thumbs an hour ago."

He wrote the forty. He set the scale card against the drilled hole and shot the photo, the number in his own block print beside the steel. A crack you have found and measured and entered in the book stops being the thing that will drop you in the river and becomes a thing you visit. That was the whole trade in one sentence. You did not fix every flaw; there was not money or lane closure on earth for that. You learned which ones were talking and which were only old, and twice a year for thirty years you put your hammer on them, and the deck above carried its eleven thousand cars a day and never knew a man had been worried on its account.

Lou brought him up out of the cold and they sat in the truck with the heater roaring while the windshield cleared. Lou kept a Sanka can wedged in the console for the shells; he ran through a palmful of sunflower seeds the way other men smoked. On the mirror hung his father's plumb bob, brass, plumb since Eisenhower, useless now that a laser did it truer, and Lou would no more take it down than saw off his own arm. He called every bridge by its state structure number. He was calling this one 0231 and reading Marcus the next three on the ticket while Marcus watched the river take a barge downstream toward Braddock, nine hoppers of coal riding low, the pilot holding the tow off the pier like a man threading a needle in a moving car.

"You good?" Lou said, not looking over, because it was the fourth month since the treatment and he had learned to ask it the way you'd ask a bridge. Not *are you well.* Are you carrying.

"I'm good," Marcus said, and he was. That was the strange part he told no one. He was better than he had been in forty years.

He came home at dusk to the house in Hazelwood his father had bought off a millwright in 1971. The porch light was on. Renata always left it on, had left it on through every shift he'd ever worked, and the yellow of it caught the wet slate and the loose iron rail by the back stoop that he had told her twice he would get to. She opened the door before he reached it. She had the dish towel over her shoulder and her reading glasses pushed up into her hair and the small chip in her front tooth caught the hall light when she said his name, the chip she'd taken off the neck of an Iron City at a Pirates game in 1994 and never fixed because the dentist wanted four hundred dollars and she wanted a washer-dryer.

It was Renata. Every gauge said Renata. Her voice, the chip, the ring worn thin as wire that had been her mother's, the way she'd salted her coffee since before he knew her because her grandmother swore it cut the bitter. All of it read true, and none of it arrived. A stranger with his wife's whole history stood in his doorway wearing his wife's dish towel, and the warmth that for thirty-one years had come into the room a half-second ahead of her did not come. It had not come since the treatment.

The dog came instead. Duke, the beagle, gray at the muzzle now, dragging the same bald tennis ball he'd carried since he could carry, and he leaned his whole weight on Marcus's shins the way he did, and something in Marcus's chest still had nothing at all to say about it.

"You're froze through," the stranger said in Renata's voice. "Sit. I salted the door but the bottom step's still ice."

He sat. She fed him. He was polite to her the way you are polite in another man's house.

*Fracture-critical member. Single load path. Failure of this member would drop the span.*

The clinical trial was eleven days, and it sat four months behind him now. He wanted that written down somewhere true, because everyone kept saying it like it was nothing, eleven days, as though a thing that fast could not have cut deep.

He had been sad his whole life. Not the kind you tell a doctor. The kind that is just the weather where you live, that you were born into and assumed was the air. Every bridge carries two loads. Dead load is the steel itself, the deck, the rail, the weight the thing was built out of and will hold up before a single car ever crosses. Live load is the day's traffic, the trucks and the wind and the ice, all of it changing minute to minute. His whole life the dead load had been the sadness. It was there in the morning before anything happened. It was the weight he was made of. He carried his live load over top of it the way every span does, and he did his work, and he raised a daughter, and he loved his wife, and no one on the deck ever knew the girder underneath was already loaded to nine-tenths before the first car came.

They put him in the machine for a lifelong depression that had stopped answering to anything with a name on a bottle. Focused ultrasound. A helmet of a thing, a thousand beams of sound crossing in the dark of his skull to meet at one point the size of a pea and quiet it. He lay still and heard the water circulating and thought about caissons, the great sunk boxes they pumped dry to build a pier, men going down into pressed air to dig under a river. On day eleven the caisson came up dry. He woke in the gray of the ward and the dead load was gone. The span rose. He lay there weightless and forty years lighter and he wept, the good way, for the first time since he was a boy, and Renata drove in from Hazelwood to sit with him, and the woman who came through the door in her good coat was not his wife.

The consent form ran nine pages. He had read all nine, because he read everything, because a man who signs off on what eleven thousand cars drive over reads the fine print. It listed headache, it listed the pin-sized chance of a bleed, it listed a numbness that might not come back. It said nothing about her face. It could not have. No one had built the gauge that measured what came off with the sadness. The warmth had been welded to the dead load somewhere down in the dark he could not inspect, and when they cut the one away clean they took the other with it, and the cut was so neat there was no burr left to catch a fingernail on.

He knew her. That was the whole cruelty of it, that nothing was lost that a stranger could see. He could have told you her mother's maiden name and the year of the washer-dryer and which side of the bed she took and why. The file was complete. The recognition was gone. She was his wife the way a photograph of a bridge is a bridge — every rivet in its place, and no steel in it, and nothing you could stand on.

They had only ever been the two of them, really. Their daughter Ana out in Chicago since her divorce, and before Ana just the two of them, and after Ana gone the two again. No third member to carry the load if one let go. He understood the drawing of his own marriage now the way he understood a fracture-critical span. Non-redundant. Built for one path down through the two of them and no other, and beautiful for it, and one weld from the river.

*Repaint recommended, cycle before last. Deferred. Recommended again. Deferred.*

The follow-up clinic was in Oakland, up the hill from where the mills used to run, and the waiting room had the radio going low behind the desk the way those rooms do, a man and a woman talking easy about the Adeyemi anniversary, industrial gases in the air of a planet no one would ever stand on, proof of somebody, they said, a decade of it now and still no way on God's earth to answer back. Marcus filled in the form on the clipboard. Renata sat one chair over with her coat still buttoned and did the Post-Gazette crossword in ballpoint, in pen, always in pen, and did not once look up to see if he was watching her do it.

The neurologist kept a glass dish of peppermints on the desk that she never offered and never ate, gone tacky in their wrappers with the years. She had his scans up on the wall, the bright small quiet where the pea of sound had been. She was pleased with the depression. She named the other thing once, acquired Capgras, and set it beside the sadness like two entries in the same book, and then she said the things they say. Transient, probably. The brain is not a switchboard. Pathways reorganize. She had seen the affect come back in weeks, in others; she had seen it come back in pieces, out of order, the way feeling in a frozen hand comes back to the palm before the fingers. He should give it time. He should not test himself against it, she said, because a watched pot, and here she trailed off, because even she could hear that it was the wrong thing to say to a man whose living was watching for the failure before it came.

He drove them home the long way, over his own bridges, the ones with his initials in thirty field books downtown. Renata rode with her hands folded on the crossword in her lap. Somewhere over the water she said, not to him exactly, that she'd started a chicken before they left, and he said good, and that was the whole of it, and the tow lights moved on the black river below them, and neither of them said the name of the thing, then or ever. There was nothing to explain. You do not explain the weather to the man standing in it.

*Pack rust in the gusset, forcing the plates apart from the inside. Nothing shows from the deck.*

Saturday he began to build his tests, because he did not know another way to be a man.

He clipped the pulse oximeter he'd bought for his mother onto his own left thumb and sat at the kitchen table reading the paper while Renata moved through the morning. Sixty-eight, the pulse line read. Sixty-seven. He turned the pages. She came in from the yard with cold in her sweater and stood at the counter and salted her coffee, the small ritual, the shake and the stir, and he kept his eyes on the box scores and his thumb on the table, and the number sat in the sixties and did not so much as think about climbing. He had watched that number jump to a hundred and twenty in the machine at the mere idea of the sound starting. It knew fear fine. It knew nothing of the woman four feet away pouring cream.

He kept a box of her letters in the cellar, from the two years he'd worked the turnpike bridges downstate and come home only Sundays, 1989 and 1990, her hand young and slanting hard to the right. He read them at the workbench under the one bulb while the furnace ticked. She had loved him in ink better than most men get loved in the flesh. He read the one where she told him she was late and scared and glad, the one that made Ana, and he waited at the bottom of the page for the thing to come up in him the way the river comes up in spring, and it did not come up. The words were true and he was the man they were written to and the man they were written to felt the way you feel reading someone else's mail. He put them back in the box in order. He was careful with the order. He had always been careful with what other people would not think to check.

He slept on his own side. He had started, some nights, lying very still on his back on his own half and setting his hand flat on the cool of her empty side while she was still up locking the house, the way you'd set a hand on a girder to feel for the traffic through it, for the live load coming, and he would feel the sheet warm slowly under his palm and nothing else move through, and then she would come to bed and he would take his hand back and say goodnight in a voice he had gotten very good at.

She was courting him. He worked it out slow, the way you work out that a bridge is being loaded past its posting — not one big thing, a hundred small readings that only mean something stacked. She wore the green she'd worn the year they met. She put a record on after supper, Sam Cooke, and did not say why, and sat closer on the davenport than the room required, close enough that he could smell the rye of the bakery still in her hair, and she talked to him the way you talk to a man you are trying to get, telling him things about her days he already knew, laughing first at her own stories to show him where the laugh went. She had figured, without a word to him about it, that if the man could not remember he loved her she would make him fall for her again from the beginning, standing start, cold engine, and she had set herself to it the way she set herself to a hem, pins in the tomato cushion on her wrist, mouth full of pins, no complaint.

He let her. He answered her right on every turn. He laughed where the laugh went. He was, he thought, a well-maintained bridge — posted, monitored, every joint sounded on schedule — carrying her live load across a span that had no dead load left under it to hold the two of them up, and neither of them able to see from the road that the plates were being forced apart from the inside, slow, by a rust with no color, in a place the deck would never show.

Dominic came at four for his lesson — she taught the piano to the neighborhood, three dollars less than anyone in Squirrel Hill — and dropped his Penguins backpack by the bench and murdered the same Clementi he'd been murdering since October and kicked the piano leg on the hard part, and left a juice box sweating a ring into the wood. Renata sat beside him and put her hand over his hand to slow it and said *listen, listen,* and Marcus stood in the kitchen doorway with a dish towel he'd picked up for no reason and watched her teach a stranger's boy to hear a thing, and he thought that he would carry her across on his back the rest of his life before he would ever let her know how far the reading had fallen.

*Expansion bearing seized. The span can no longer move the way it was built to move.*

The dog came back first.

It was a Tuesday, nothing to it. Duke put his gray muzzle on Marcus's knee in the blue light of the ballgame and Marcus went to rub the long ears the way he had ten thousand times, and the warmth came up out of nowhere and filled his hand and his throat, whole and stupid and enormous, love for a beagle who would be dead inside two years, and Marcus sat in the dark with his hand full of the dog's ear and his eyes running and he did not dare turn his head toward the kitchen where Renata was doing the dishes, because he did not want her to see it land on the dog. He heard the water shut off. He heard her stop. She had seen the back of his neck do something. She stood in the door drying a plate that was already dry, drying it and drying it, and then she went back to the sink and ran the water again so he would not have to say anything, and that was a kindness he had no name in him to answer.

His mother came back next. He drove out to Munhall on the Sunday, to the home where she kept the television on the weather channel with the sound off and folded the paper towels into careful quarters and stacked them on the sill though the aides brought fresh, and she took his face in both her ruined hands the way she had since he was a boy, and it came, it all came, forty years of her at once, and he held on to her too long and she patted his back and said *there, there, what's this,* and did not know she had given him something the doctors couldn't. He drove home over the river in the last light with it still warm in his chest and he thought, coming down off the bridge into Hazelwood, that it was going to come back for Renata now, any day, it was warming up through him room by room, palm before fingers, and he let himself believe it the whole way home.

Ana called that night, Sunday being when she called, and read him her grocery list before she got to anything, oat milk, the good coffee, dog food for the dog she'd finally got, the way she'd narrated her whole life to him down a phone line since she was six. She asked how her mother was. She asked, careful, how he was, and he said better, and she said she could hear it, and she could, the depression was gone and would stay gone, that part had held. She did not ask the other thing because Renata had not told her the other thing, because Renata protected them both from it the way she protected everyone, quietly, at cost, without a receipt.

But it stalled. It came up through him for the dog and for his mother and for the smell of the rye and for a Clementi played right at last, and it stopped at the one door, the way an expansion bearing seizes with rust and the whole span goes rigid and cannot answer the heat and the cold the way it was drawn to, and the loads it was built to shed with a quarter-inch of give have nowhere now to go. He felt it warming through every room in the house but hers, and he began, God help him, to be afraid that the machine had not broken something at all. That it had measured. That it had found the one weld in him that had gone hollow years before any sound ever touched it, and had only, at last, told him the reading.

*Load posting reduced. The structure carries less than the plans allow. Adequate.*

They posted the Glenwood on a Thursday. Corrosion in the lower chord past what the rating could carry safe, and the money three years out, and so they hung the black-and-white signs at both ends dropping it to fifteen tons, no combination loads, and the trucks that used to cross it would go around by the Hot Metal from now on, six miles the long way, for years, maybe for good.

"Feels like a funeral," the young one on the crew said, torquing the last sign bracket down, and Lou spat a shell into the Sanka can and said no. A funeral was when you closed it. This was the other thing. This was you looked at what the span could still truly carry, and you carried exactly that and not one pound more, and you told the truth about it in black and white at both ends so nobody drove onto it expecting what it used to give, and it stood. It carried its fifteen tons a day for another forty years and held up every car that trusted the number. There was no shame in a posted bridge. The shame was in the deck that let on it was sound when it wasn't, and dropped a school bus in the river to keep its dignity.

Marcus signed the rating. His initials, the date, the reduced number in the box. He drove home the long way over his own bridges in the dark and did not test himself once the whole way, and it was the first quiet he'd had.

*Recommendation: full repair, not monitoring. Restore the load path. Close the ticket.*

The cold snapped down hard that night, single digits, the kind that finds every flaw. Renata went up at ten. He heard the boards of the old house take her weight, the third stair, the landing, and then the pipes and then quiet. He sat with the dog until the game ended and the dog was asleep on his boots.

Then he got the drop light and the box of anchors and went out the back in his coat.

The stoop rail had been loose since October. He'd told her twice he'd get to it. He'd deferred it the way you defer a repaint, telling himself it was talking but not yet, and every one of those cold mornings she had come down these steps in the dark to open the bakery and put her hand on it, and it had given a half inch under her hand and held, and she had never said. He hung the light off the gutter and he ran his bare hand down the rail in the cold the way he'd run it down ten thousand feet of steel, feeling for the story, and the rail told it to him plain. The bottom post was the fault. Whoever set it had lagged it into the mortar joint instead of the block, and thirty winters of water had got in and frozen and thawed and packed the joint with rust and blown the mortar to sugar, and the whole rail stood now on two lags turning in a hole full of orange dust. Section loss at the bracket, eating up from the base. A stranger leaning on it would have called it solid. It would have let go the first bad morning, with her whole weight coming down on the ice.

He did it right. He did it the way he would have done it eighty feet over the water with three men watching. He backed the ruined lags out and raked the dead mortar from the joint and vacuumed the dust and wire-brushed the bracket down to bright metal, the faying surface bare and clean the way the spec wants it, no rust between the steel and what holds the steel. He drilled the block itself, not the joint, three holes true, and blew them clear, and set the wedge anchors and drew them up. He torqued each nut to the number, turn of the wrench, turn of the wrench, past the give and into the solid, and felt through the wrench the moment each one seated home and stopped being a bolt going in and became a bridge. He wiped the rail down and put his cold hand on it and leaned, and then he put his boot up on the bottom step and stood his whole two hundred pounds on it out over the ice, both hands, and rocked, and it did not move. It took the load and gave nothing back and it would take hers every dark morning for the next forty years and never once tell her a man had been worried on her account.

Nothing came up in him while he did it. He had stopped waiting for it. He gathered the light and the brushes and the box of good anchors and stood a moment in the yard in the killing cold, and the kitchen window was yellow above him where she'd left the light on, the way she left it on through every shift he had ever worked, and he went in out of the dark and up the stairs he'd sounded a thousand times and got into his own side of the bed, and set his hand flat on the warm of her back, and left it there, and did not take it away.

## 2. Because

The coil reads one thing only. It watches whether the brain reached back and lifted an answer from where it was stored, or built it new at the moment of asking. Lifted, the trace stays dark. Built, the amber comes up.

I calibrated it against the phantom every morning at seven, before the ward filled, when the only sound on the floor was the salt truck working the parking deck two levels down. The phantom is a head of ballistic gel with a copper lattice inside it, machined to give back a known answer. The field goes up, the trace comes back, the amber threshold sits where it sat the morning before or it does not. Some mornings it drifted a hair and I brought it back with a quarter-turn. A gauge that will not hold its own zero is worse than no gauge, and a gauge that asks to be trusted without checking is how people end up buried under numbers, so I checked it every day against a head that could not lie because it could not remember. That was the whole of the ritual, and I loved it the way a person comes to love the one instrument in a life that answers in a single channel and asks for nothing back. Outside, Lake Erie was making its own weather again, gray coming off the water in sheets, and the radiator under the window knocked twice and settled, and the room held its sixty-eight degrees against all of it.

The clinic sat on the fourth floor of the old wing, north-facing, in a teaching hospital on the east side of Cleveland, so the light in the testing rooms never changed and never had to be corrected for, which suited us; a testing room is built to hold everything still except the one variable meant to move. I had six patients that Thursday and a lab meeting at noon and a manuscript I was avoiding, and the snow that had started before dawn was the fine dry kind that means business, the kind the forecasters up here stopped naming years ago and just call accumulation.

Frank Wozniak came in at nine, first appointment, the way he'd come every Thursday for six weeks. He said good morning, Dr. Ozan, the way he did every week. He carried a roll of butterscotch discs in his coat pocket and set them on the table between us like a man laying down a good card, and every session he offered me one, and every session I took it, because the taking was part of what he was there for and he did not know that. His aneurysm had let go two years before, up at the front where the arteries feed the part of the brain that keeps memory honest, that flags a thing as remembered or as merely thought. The vessel got clipped in time. The flag did not come back. So Frank remembered whatever the moment handed him and believed all of it, sincerely, without seam.

I sat him under the coil and brought the field up and asked him where we were. He said the union hall. I asked what he'd done that morning. He said he'd driven his wife to the eye doctor and waited in the car with the ballgame. The amber light came up soft on the readout, the way a stovetop coil comes up, dull first and then certain. His wife had been dead eleven years. He had not driven in three. None of that reached him; the light was on my side of the table, a small lamp I watched while he talked, and he talked the way a stream runs downhill, finding the path without choosing it. I wrote the timestamps. I logged the amber against each answer. This is called, in the literature, spontaneous confabulation, and the word people reach for is lying, and it is the farthest thing from lying I have ever sat across from. Frank was not defending a position. He had no position. He had a mouth and a morning to account for and the account assembled itself, whole, plausible, warm, and he handed it to me as a gift.

I asked him why he'd married Ellen. That was the wife. He looked at the middle distance the way everyone does when the question is why, and the amber came up, and he said: because she was the only one at the plant who split her sandwich with a stranger. He said it the way a man says the year he was born. The light burned steady. I have run that question on a hundred people and it burns for every one of them, but Frank's face while it burned — there was no performance under it to catch. He believed he had reached back forty years and lifted out a sandwich. He had made it that second, at the table, out of whatever was lying around in him, and it was true in every way that mattered to a marriage and false in the one way my instrument could see.

I ran the rest of the battery the same as always. He put the date in October; it was the last week of January. He named a president who'd been out of office for years and named him fondly, the way he named everyone. But when I asked what work he had done, the light stayed dark, and he told me the truth without knowing it was a different kind of thing — forty-one years on the presses at the stamping plant in Brook Park, so loud he'd learned to read lips at the lunch table, and he raised his right hand and showed me the two fingers the die had taken in 1974, and the amber never stirred. The fingers were gone and the loss of them was real, stored, lifted back whole. It was only the whys that glowed. Why he'd hired on there. Why he'd stayed when the Lorain plant was hiring. Why Ellen. The presses he could keep. The reasons he had to build.

Priya, my graduate student, ran the console from the next room through the glass. She keeps a mechanical pencil behind her ear, half-millimeter lead, and she will not round a p-value up to please anyone, not the journal, not the chair, not me. She tapped the glass twice when the artifact rejection — the pass that throws out any trace a blink or a swallow spoiled — ran clean. Somewhere down the hall a phone was ringing and going to voicemail and ringing again, which meant a reporter, because reporters call the department line when the press office won't give them what they want, and what they wanted that winter was for me to say the coil was the next index, the amber light the little sister of the box they wheel into the coma wards to decide who's home. Bell, our chair, had started taking those calls himself just to hang up on them. The index had a foundation and a wing named after it and a line in every obituary now — indexed low near the end — and we had a windowless room, a gel head, and a light. Bell said the word index the way a man says a name he has stopped speaking to. Different question, he'd say. Entirely different question. Then he'd go find someone to say it to again.

Frank offered me the butterscotch on his way out. I took it. He told me, at the door, that his daughter was picking him up, and that she was never late, that she got that from him. The aide who'd brought him from the county van and would take him back to it stood right there holding his coat and his hat. His daughter was thirty and lived in Arizona where it was warm and called the ward on Sundays and had not come in person in three years; the daughter he was sure would be idling at the curb was a daughter he had built for the occasion. The amber wasn't running anymore; I'd powered it down. But I knew what it would have shown, and anyone who had sat where I sat for twenty years would have known too. Everyone's morning assembles itself. The difference between Frank and the rest of us is only that his was easy to see, and that no one had ever asked him to sign his name to it.

We ran the healthy controls in February, forty-one of them, students and retirees and a bus driver on his day off, people with intact vessels and intact flags, people who could say what they had for breakfast and be right. On the episodic questions the light stayed dark. What was on the plate that morning, which door they came in by, what the sky did when they woke — green, green, green, the memory lifted whole from where it was stored, the flag confirming it. Retrieved. Then Priya, who'd built the block order, slid the reason questions in between the memory questions, matched for length, read off in the same flat voice so the machine couldn't sort them by tone. Why that space and not the one nearer the door. Why this job. Why him. Why come in today, of all the days.

The amber came up. Not every time. Enough times that Priya stopped the run at subject nineteen and came around the glass and stood in the doorway with the pencil out of her hair, turning it, and said the number out loud, the proportion, and then said it again as if the second saying would round it down. It would not round down. She would not let it. The memories came back green and the reasons came back amber, in the same skulls, minutes apart, and the people giving the reasons were not brain-injured and were not lying and were as sure of their becauses as Frank was sure of his sandwich. A reason, it turned out, is not a thing a person keeps on a shelf and goes and gets. It is a thing made at the table, on the spot, when someone asks, out of whatever is lying around inside. Everyone does it. Everyone does it sincerely. The flag that fires for the confabulating patient fires for the rest of us the instant we say the word because.

I understood what we had before I let myself understand what it meant. Priya understood the paper — the courtrooms it would walk into, every confession and every jury instruction suddenly a document about the wrong thing; the depositions; the marriages, though she was too decent to say marriages, she said dyads. I sat in the dark room after she went home and I did not think about courtrooms. I thought about one sentence. For nine days I carried the finding in one pocket and the sentence in the other and kept them from touching. I had carried the sentence twenty years, smooth as a river stone, and I took it out and looked at it in the light of what we'd found, and I already knew what the coil would do to it, and I did the thing anyway, later, alone, after the building emptied and the salt truck had quit for the night. I sat under my own field and said the sentence into the room. Mom asked me to stay away. I watched the light the way Frank never could. It did what it does.

Here is the sentence, and here is the house it was built to hold up.

Our mother was Vera Ozan, and twenty years ago she died in my sister Diane's front room on Hampshire Road, in the middle of a February exactly like the one I ran the controls in, lake-effect coming off the water, the streets white by four. Ovarian, found late, the way it always is. She had eleven months from the diagnosis and she spent the last five in my sister's house because my sister has a first floor and a wide door and a way of making a sickroom look like a room where someone is only resting. Diane kept the log. Amateur's chart, spiral notebook, a line for every dose and every meal and every hard hour, the morphine down the left margin in her small square hand. She dated everything. She still dates everything. There is masking tape on the leftovers in her refrigerator with the day written on it in marker, because that is the kind of watching she does, has always done, patient and undramatic and exact, the watching of a woman who wants to be able to say later exactly what happened and when. She learned to draw up the morphine and flush the port and turn a grown woman in the bed without waking her, from a hospice nurse named Carol who came Tuesdays and Fridays and from a laminated card taped inside the cabinet door. The card is still there. It has curled at the corners, and I know this because I have opened that cabinet for a water glass and looked at it and shut the door.

I came twice. Five months, and I came twice, and both times I left inside two days, and for twenty years the reason I gave — to Diane, to Diane's children, to my own husband before he became someone I used to be married to, to myself in the dark — was that our mother had asked me to stay away. That she'd said she wanted me to remember her upright, in her garden, and not in the bed. That she'd taken my hand the first visit and told me to go back to my work, that the work was the thing, that a woman in our family who got a chance at work did not squander it sitting in a sickroom watching a machine drip. It is a good sentence. It has our mother's cadence in it. It let me keep working through the winter she was dying, and it let Diane forgive me, or do the thing that stands in for forgiving, which is to stop bringing it up.

What I have of those two visits is not a reason, and it holds green when I test it, so I trust it as far as I trust anything. There was a quarter-inch of ice on the inside of the storm windows in Diane's front room, and I scratched my initials in it with a thumbnail while our mother slept, the way I'd scratched them in those same windows as a girl. A humidifier ran. A bowl of ice chips had gone to water on the tray. Our mother woke and knew me and asked first whether I'd eaten and then what the temperature was, because she had kept the daily high on the kitchen calendar in pencil for forty years and did not intend to stop for dying, and I read her the nineteen off the bank sign I'd passed coming in, and she took the number the way she'd have taken a cut flower. All of that is stored. All of that comes back green. It is only the sentence I built out of that weekend — that somewhere in its hours she took my hand and sent me back to my work — that comes back amber, and I have turned the weekend over and over and cannot find the minute it claims, because the minute is not in the weekend. It is in me. I set it there, and it set like poured concrete, and I stood a marriage's worth of years on top of it.

I went at it that spring like a hostile witness, the way I go at any record I mean to break. I still had the datebooks, the paper kind, a year to a volume, and the one for that February is full in my own hand — a resubmission due the fourth, a pilot I would not pause, a seat held on a Tuesday flight to a study section in Bethesda that I did not give up. A datebook is a faithful witness and a useless one. It swears to every place I stood and takes no position on why I stood there. I had the answering-machine cassette from the old house because I am the kind of person who keeps the cassette and never plays it, and that spring I bought a deck to play it on, and our mother is on it twice, both times about nothing, a dentist, a dish to be returned, her voice ordinary and alive and asking me for neither my presence nor my absence. I had the hospice chart, because Diane keeps everything and had asked for her copy when the agency closed the case, and she mailed it to me without one question, which was its own answer, and I did read it, and there is no line in it where Vera Ozan asked her daughter to stay away, though there is a line, day forty, nurse's hand, that says patient tearful, asking for R. I sat with that. R is me. There is only one R. I looked for the moment she took my hand and released me to my work and I could not find it in any record and I could not find it in myself either, and that is the part I keep trying to say plainly. It is not that I found a different memory underneath. There was nothing underneath. I reached back the way I'd reached back a thousand times and my hand closed on the same smooth stone, and the coil said the stone had been poured in me and worn smooth by handling, that no river had ever touched it, and when I set the stone down and reached past it there was only the dark that is under everyone's becauses, mine no different, mine no deeper, just mine.

Priya asked me once, over the console, whether I'd run myself on the mother question. She didn't say the mother question. She said the personal item, because she'd seen my face when the controls broke. I told her I had. She asked what it showed and I told her the truth, which is that it showed amber, and she nodded and put the pencil back in her hair and did not say the thing a lesser person would have said, which is that amber only means constructed, that constructed is not the same as untrue, that maybe my mother did ask and I can't retrieve the asking, that the flag can't tell a lie from a memory too old to lift. All of that is correct. I have said all of it to myself in the voice I use for patients. None of it gives me the winter back. None of it tells me why a woman drove past the exit for her mother's deathbed twice and told everyone for twenty years that the dying woman had waved her on.

I know the shape of the answers that want to fill the hole. I was thirty-four and had a grant under review and a marriage two winters from its end and a lab that could run without me for no longer than a week. I have always gone strange around the smell of a sickroom, the sweet-and-antiseptic of it, the way it gets down into wool and rides home on a coat. I loved my mother the way a daughter loves a mother she needs upright and out in her own garden, and my love could not make the crossing from that room into the one with the bed in it. Any of those would light amber if I said it under the coil, and I know that now the way I did not know it then, so I set them down here and I do not stand behind them. They are reasons I can make. They are not reasons I can find. The finding, if it ever happened, happened in the dark where the instrument does not go, where I do not go, and it kept its own counsel, and it is keeping it still.

I drove out to Hampshire Road on a Sunday in March with the roads bad and the plows behind. Cleveland in the melt is its own controlled condition — everything the winter measured is still lying there under a skin of gray water, the road salt blooming white at the curbs, the snowbanks going to lace. I took the exit I had driven past twice with a living mother behind me, and it is only an exit, a Marathon station and a left-turn arrow that holds too long, nothing marked on it, nothing that should have been able to hold a person or let her go by. I had rehearsed nothing, which is not like me. I run scripts. I standardize the prompt so the answer means something. But I had learned by then what a script is worth, that the fluent thing I'd say if I planned it would be the least true thing in the car, and so I let the wipers keep time and I did not build the sentence in advance.

The house on Hampshire is warm enough to grow the orchids Diane grows, a shelf of them in the south window over the sink, and it smelled of the coffee she keeps going all day in a pot the color of a brick. Her hands were wet when she let me in; she'd been at the sink. She has our mother's hands, square and certain, the hands that kept the log. There was a container on the counter with blue tape on it and Sunday written on the tape, and the Pyrex measure by the stove, the two-cup, the one that came out of our mother's kitchen and into Diane's, its red numbers worn to nothing on the side the hand holds, so that it measures now by memory, by the palm that knows where the line used to be.

I said her name. Diane, I said, and she waited, drying her hands on the towel over her shoulder, because she has always known the difference between a visit and an errand and she could see this was the second kind.

I told her the truth in the words I had, and I knew even as I chose them that every one of them would have lit the light. I said I stayed away because I was a coward and because the work was easier than the bed and because I could not stand to lose the version of our mother I could stand. I said Mom never asked me to go. I said I made that up so long ago I believed it, and it held the both of us up for twenty years, and it was a lie, and I was the one who told it, and I was sorry. Every because in it burned amber in my chest as it left me. I could feel the flag come up on my own words the way I'd watched it come up on Frank's, and I said them anyway, because the flag was never the point. I did not come to give you a true reason. I don't have one. I came to stand in the room I stayed out of.

You didn't say that you forgave me and you didn't say that you didn't. You turned to the counter. You were out of clean cups — they were all in the machine, the way a Sunday leaves them — and you filled our mother's measure from the brick-colored pot, the two-cup with the numbers worn off, still warm from the last hot rinse, and you put it in my hands.

# PART II — THE TERMS OF KNOWING EACH OTHER

There is a kind of love that shows up mostly as maintenance — the dose measured, the gutter cleared, the same six words said at the same hour every night. It does not photograph well. Nobody writes songs about adherence. But watch what happens in a house when one person quietly stops winding the clock: how fast the true weather gets in.

The stories in this part are about the terms — what two people agree to know about each other, what they agree not to, and the child, the molecule, or the earbud that renegotiates the contract without asking either of them.

## 3. Fluent

Aoi fired the boiler at two so the water would stand at forty-three by four, and she banked it the way her father had shown her the winter she turned nine, which was her daughter Mio's age now — the flue open a hand's width, the oil valve cracked a quarter, the pilot held to the burner until the low roar caught and came up through the concrete into the soles of her feet. The kama, the oil-fired boiler, had heated this water since 1968. It fed the deep bath and the jet bath and the electric bath on the men's side, and the family's own small tub off the same line, and the back room where her mother-in-law lived, and it drank a barrel of heavy oil a week that Aoi could no longer quite meet, and it was the loudest thing she loved.

She scrubbed the men's side first, because the men came straight off shift and would not wait, and then the women's. Forty years of bodies had worn the blue tiles pale in a track from the door to the deep bath. The mosaic on the far wall was a mountain in cut tile, snow at the peak in white squares and pines below it in three greens, and it lost tiles off the left slope faster than she could match them, so the mountain came apart a little every year, regrouted, the new white a shade too blue against the old. She rinsed the yellow basins under the tap and stacked them dripping, the light Kansai ones, thirty-eight of them now, two cracked in November. She set the stools in their rows. She knelt at the lip of the deep bath and put her wrist in past the bone, because the float thermometer lied by half a degree in the cold and her wrist never had.

By three the room had begun to breathe. The tile took the heat and gave it back, the high windows went to milk with steam, and the empty hall carried the smallest sound the length of itself — the drip off a tap, the tick of the pipes finding their temperature, her own knees on the wet stone. A bathhouse before it opens is the quietest loud room there is. She had grown up in that one held breath, between the water gone hot and the door not yet open, and she stood in it a moment, alone, before the ward came in and filled it with themselves.

Through the wall the radio came on. English, a man and a woman talking easy, and then a swell of strings — the station her mother-in-law had found that played the songs of the country she'd left, that she kept a hair too loud since her hearing went. Under it rode the old woman's voice, saying something to the room or to the radio, the shapes of English that Aoi had lived inside for eleven years and had never once been able to open, the way you can live your whole life beside water and never think in it.

Mr. Ueno came at four, first through the door as he had been first through the door since before Aoi's father died. He would not touch the yellow basins. He brought his own, hinoki — cypress — gone thin and pale as a fingernail, and he made straight for the electric bath and sat in the low buzz of it until his shoulders went the color of the inside of a shell, though his heart was bad and his daughter had told him and told him. He paid with the coins he kept in a flat tobacco tin. Aoi took the coins warm from his hand and gave him back the little bow that was the whole of their talk, and turned the wooden sign, and the day was open.

···

David, her husband, came home from the brewery at seven with the cold still on his coat and the smell of it in his hair, steamed rice and something roasted underneath, the roasted-chestnut smell of the koji room that never quite washed out of him from November to March. His hands were split at the knuckles from washing rice in water just off ice, the way the toji, the master brewer, made them wash it, by hand, by count, and he had stopped, years ago, minding that it hurt. He set a small unlabeled bottle on the shelf by the door, the toji's cloudy first-press that he was let carry home a cup of, and he said, in his Japanese, that the moromi was singing.

He meant that it had begun to work, the tanks gone loud with their own small weather. He had built the sentence wrong the first winter and never fixed it, so that what he said, each time, was closer to *the mash is a song,* and Aoi had stopped correcting it around the same winter he stopped minding his hands, because a man who has learned an entire craft with his shoulders and his nose and no words to speak of does not need his wife to sand his grammar. He had walked into that brewery eleven years ago knowing how to make beer in Oregon and not one useful thing else, and the toji had taught him the koji the way you teach a dog to the water, by putting his hands in it, and now he could tell by the smell coming off a tank at four in the morning whether the temperature had run, and he could not order lunch without Mio.

They had met at this counter. He had come in his second Osaka winter red to the ears and not knowing the men's door from the women's, a foreigner off the brewery line who'd been told there was a bath in the ward that still burned oil, and he had stood dripping at the shoe cupboard holding a coin out on his flat palm like a child. She took the wrong coin and gave him the right basin and pointed, and that was the courtship, more or less — a year of the wrong coin and the right basin, a man who could name the temperature of steamed rice to the degree and not one thing he felt, learning her as he'd learned the koji, by standing near it until his hands knew. Her father had died that spring and left her the boiler and the barrel of oil and the mountain coming apart on the wall, and David had married into the whole of it without, she sometimes thought, any clear idea of what he'd said yes to, because no one had ever been able to tell him, in words, exactly what it was.

Aoi ran the counter through the supper hour. She sold Mrs. Doi a bottle of coffee-milk from the cold case and watched her drink it standing with one hand on her hip and her towel turbaned on her head, the way everyone drank it, the way it was meant to be drunk.

The women's side filled by seven. In a room of wet tile with no soft thing in it, every sound went up and came back doubled — a basin knocked down, a palmful of water thrown, a grandmother telling a granddaughter to count to a hundred before she climbed out of the hot bath, the child going up the numbers loud and flat and true. Mrs. Kado kept her soap in a tin box with a mermaid on the lid worn to a ghost, and she washed the same shoulder forty strokes and the other twenty, and had for as long as Aoi could remember. Voices came over the partition from the men's side, where the wall stopped short of the ceiling, so that the whole place was one room with a wall in it that pretended otherwise, and you learned, growing up inside it, to know a person entire by the sounds they made on the far side of a thing you could not see them through.

She skimmed the deep bath and adjusted the valve a quarter when the four-o'clock crowd thinned. And she carried the tray back to the sliding door of her mother-in-law's room, the small pot and the one cup, and set it on the mat at the threshold, and did not slide the door, because eleven years had taught her the exact distance at which the old woman would accept a thing.

The voice came through. Aoi caught *bath* in it, and *him,* and the flat cold that had been in it since the woman arrived four winters ago, off a plane she swore she'd never take, after David's father died and there was no one left to keep her and no chance on earth she would learn a syllable of the place she'd been dragged to. Aoi caught the tone and set down the pot and heard, before she'd straightened, the ring rap twice against the lacquer, the woman's wedding band, which meant the tea had come too cool again, and the tin of horehound drops shifted and rattled on the sill.

Mio was on the third stair with her notebook, the marbled one, two columns down every page, Japanese in the left and the round English in the right, and she came when the ring rapped because she had come to that sound since the winter the woman arrived. She stood at the door a moment with her head tipped, and Aoi watched her daughter's throat move once, the small swallow she did before she carried anything across, and then Mio turned and said, in Japanese, that Grandma asked would it snow, and that she'd like the tea hotter next time, please.

"Tell her by the weekend," Aoi said, though the woman had not asked about snow, Aoi knew now and had half-known for years, the way you know the deep bath is a degree low before the float admits it. She reheated the pot. Mio carried it back and stood and did her small swallow and rendered whatever came through the door into three kind Japanese words, and went back to her stair, and this was the machinery of the house, oiled so long that no one in it could any longer hear it run.

There were nights the rapping came in the middle of Mio's homework and she went with the pencil still in her hand, and nights it came at the good part of a show and she went, and she had never once been asked whether she wanted the carrying or only been born into the middle of it, the one door in the house that opened both ways. She was nine and had three grammars in her mouth — her mother's, her father's, and the third one she'd built herself, the soft one, the one where the old woman asked about snow. Aoi had watched that third grammar go up since the girl was four, and had been glad of it, and had helped to lay the block, some nights at the sink, setting the kind version of the day over the true one for the child's own good, so that Mio grew up ferrying a house whose grown people were quietly ferrying it too, everyone downstream of everyone, no one at the spring.

David tried a joke at the table. He had a joke most nights, built the way he built everything, out of parts that didn't quite fit, and it died the way most of them died, and Aoi laughed anyway at the shape of him trying, at his split hands going up to help the words along, and Mio laughed the real laugh, and David looked from one of them to the other pleased and uncertain, a man who has heard applause through a wall and can't tell which side of the joke it's for. Later, at the sink, Aoi said to Mio, low, in the voice she kept for the two of them, that Grandma was only old and a long way from home and they would be patient with her, and Mio nodded over the plates the way she nodded at everything Aoi asked of her, and dried, and stacked, and the radio played its old strings through the wall.

···

Their anniversary fell in the coldest week, the way it always had, eleven years of Januarys. Aoi closed early. She let the last of the water down the channels and swabbed the drains and killed the boiler, and the great room ticked and settled as the tiles gave their heat back to the dark. She ran the family's small tub instead, and while it filled she took David's hands across the low table and worked the cracked knuckles with the yellow salve from the pharmacy, thumb by thumb, the way she did every winter, because he would not do it himself and would let them split to the bone.

He watched her do it. He always watched her do it, and he never had the words for it and had stopped reaching for them, and the not-reaching was its own kind of speech she had learned to read as surely as the wrist reads the water. When she finished he turned his cup in his salved hands and lifted it, and said the thing he said, the six wrong syllables he had said every anniversary since the first, that he had built badly in a language he did not have and set like a joint that dries crooked and holds anyway.

Mio, at the table in her pajamas, allowed to stay up, said it for him the way she had said it for years, the family's version, worn smooth: that Papa says you are the warm water of this house. And they laughed, all three, and Aoi squeezed his ruined hands, and the tub ran full behind the wall with a sound like rain coming closer, and the old woman's radio went on playing to the back room, and it was, Aoi thought, holding her husband's hands on the coldest night of the year in the smell of steam and menthol salve, enough. It was a good marriage. It had never once in eleven years been able to say why.

Mio brought the buds home on a Thursday, in a flat white box with the ward office's mark on it, and a folded sheet Aoi could not read but understood from the picture on it — a family at a table, lines of little arrows going between their heads. The buds were translators. You wore one, and any voice that reached you came back a half-beat later in your own language. The school had given a pair to every child, and to the households where the paper said the home language and the school language did not match, they had given the family set. Mio set them out on the table at supper like a girl laying places. She was pleased. She showed them how it went, the small bead of it warmed in the ear until it stopped feeling like anything, no more than a grain of rice, she said, and she looked at her mother and her father and she said the thing that broke it open, that now they could just talk, the two of them, straight across, and she wouldn't have to.

David put his in because Mio wanted him to. Aoi put hers in because David had. The old woman's radio was going in the back room, and the strings came through the wall the way they came through every night, and then a voice laid itself over the strings inside Aoi's ear, level and clear and idiomatic, in Aoi's own language, close as a thought — *he could have had anyone he wanted and he brought home the girl who runs the baths. Listen to her out there. Sloshing.*

The pot was in Aoi's hand. She had risen for the tea without deciding to, the ring not yet rapped, and she stood with the pot and the clear kind terrible voice in her ear finishing the sentence it had been saying for four winters through a wall, and she did not move.

Across the table David had gone the color of the inside of a shell. He was looking at Aoi and his face was doing something she had watched him try to make it do for eleven years and never manage, and she understood that the bead in his ear had just handed him something too, out of the air of the house, some small Japanese thing she had let fall a thousand times toward Mio and never toward him — that they would be patient with the old woman, that she was only far from home; the mercy Aoi had been spending in the dark for years on the woman who despised her, laid now in David's own ear in words he could finally hold.

And Mio, who did not know the beads were in, who had been rendering the house for so long that her body did it before her mind could stop it, heard her grandmother's voice come through the wall and did her small swallow, and turned, and said to her mother, in Japanese, gently, that Grandma said the water sounded nice tonight.

The bead had already said the true thing. Both of them had already heard it, whole. And Mio saw it in their faces, the way a child sees the thing an instant before she can name it — that the wall had come down, that the soft Japanese she'd just handed her mother was lying naked beside the truth it had been built to cover, eleven years of it, every kind lie she had made since she was four to hold the table together, all of it lit at once. Her hand went up to her own throat. She took the bead out of her ear and set it on the table very carefully, as if it might still be listening, and she did not cry, and she did not explain, and she went up the stairs, the third one, the landing, and her door.

David was still holding his cup. In the wreck of the table he did the only thing the table had ever taught him, which was to lift it, and say his six wrong syllables, his anniversary sentence, the warm water of this house — and then, for the first time in eleven years, he kept going, in English, low and fast, his own whole tongue at last, and the bead in Aoi's ear, still in, still faithful, laid it down clean and level and eleven years too late: *I'm sorry. I brought you into a cold house. She will never be kind to you and I can't make her and I'm sorry.*

Aoi took the bead out of her ear. She set it beside her daughter's on the table, the two of them small and yellow-white under the light, grains of rice, and she went and turned off the tub before it ran over the lip, and she stood in the steam of it with her back to the room until she could breathe in the wet air the way you breathe in the wet air, and behind her the radio kept playing its old songs to the back room, in a language she was grateful, standing there, that she still could not understand.

Mio did not translate again. Not the next morning, not that week, not the sharp thing the old woman sent through the wall on Sunday nor the blunt one David tried to send back. She went to school and came home and sat on the third stair with the marbled notebook, and when the ring rapped against the lacquer she looked up and looked at it and looked back down, and let it rap. Aoi carried the tray herself and set it at the threshold and stood in the cold English coming through the door and understood, now, every fourth or fifth word of it the way you understand a thing you've been given the key to and can never give back, and she reheated the tea when the ring rapped, and she did not ask her daughter to carry anything across for anyone ever again.

The buds went into their white box and the box went on the shelf over the shoe cupboard, where the winter gloves went, and Aoi did not put hers in again and did not see David put his in, and the household ran, after a fashion, the way the boiler ran when a jet clogged — hot enough, wrong somewhere, carrying its load with a knock in it you learned to stop hearing.

She kept the woman's bath at forty-two, a degree cool of the men's, the way the old woman liked it and had never once said thank you for, and she filled the pill organizer David marked in English on the lid, the seven shapes of the days that Aoi read as shapes, and she laundered the things that came out of the back room and folded them and set them at the threshold and did not slide the door. She did the maintenance. She had always done the maintenance. The difference was only that she now knew what she was maintaining, and knowing did not stop her hands, which had never needed to know a thing to do it right.

David stopped trying the joke at supper. For a week, two weeks, the table ran silent but for Mio's fork, and Aoi let it, because there was nothing the beads had said that a word from her could unsay, and because she had run enough water through enough pipe to know you do not fix a knock by wanting it gone.

Then one night she took the white box down off the shelf, over the gloves, and she carried it out to the alley where the empty oil barrels stood, and she did not throw it away, because it was the ward's and Mio would have to answer for it, but she set it down out there in the cold by the barrels where no hand would reach for it by habit, and she came back in and washed the smell of oil off her hands at the deep sink, and that was the whole of what she decided, and she said none of it to anyone.

The moromi came in near the end of February, and David came home smelling of banana and melon under the rice, the sweet high smell the good yeast throws when it's happy, and Aoi could tell from the door that it had gone well, that whatever the tanks had done in the dark they had done right.

She was in the deep bath when he came, after close, the sign turned and the last basin stacked, her sleeves pushed up and her arms in past the elbow, running her hands along the tile at the waterline for the grout that had gone soft, the slow yearly work of keeping the mountain from coming apart faster than she could hold it. The water stood at forty-three. It moved when she moved and lay still when she was still and found its own flat level against her arms without being told, the way it had for her father and would for whoever came after her. The boiler was off and ticking as the iron gave its heat back to the dark, and steam came up off the surface and hung in the high cold air of the emptied room and beaded on the tile mountain and ran back down it, slow, the mountain's own weather.

David stood at the lip in his coat with the cold still on it. He did not have the beads. She knew it from how he stood, from the long breath he took before he tried. He could have put the bead back in his ear. He could have stood in that doorway and said the whole of it in his own tongue, where he had every word he needed, the esters and the temperatures and the sorry, and let the thing in her ear hand it across to her clean and level and right. The white box was out by the barrels in the cold where she had set it down. He had left it there. And he reached instead for the six syllables he had never once gotten right, and got them down the same wrong way he'd gotten them down for eleven years, laboring, his split hands coming up to help — and this time neither of them laughed, and neither of them said the family's easy version of what they meant, and Aoi kept her arms in the water and her eyes on the tile and let him get to the end of it, all of it, in his own bad mouth, no voice laid over his but his.

Upstairs a door was open, and down through the boards came Mio's voice, careless and loud, singing along to something on the little speaker, dropping a Japanese word into the middle of an English line and an English word into the middle of a Japanese one and never once stopping to trade one for the other, singing across the seam in a language that was only hers, for no one, carrying nothing across for anybody. Aoi held her hands in the warm water and did not turn around.

## 4. Adherence

Owen counts the amoxicillin by fives, ticking them off the tray with the flat of the spatula, and caps the vial at thirty. Ten days of it for a strep that will quit in three whether the kid finishes the bottle or not. He squares the label to the counter and drops the vial in will-call under the mother's last name, and the bin under B is already three deep. A third of what he dispenses is still standing on these shelves at ninety days. # PART III — THE WORKING SKY

Space was a cathedral for one generation and a job site ever after. Somebody has to screen the debris, time the reentry, count the ballots under the falling rock. The sky in these stories is not wonder and it is not menace; it is a workplace with a bad safety margin, and the people who clock into it keep the only faith that survives contact with a checklist: do the arithmetic honestly and sign your name to it.

The arithmetic is always right. The names are the story.

## 6. Conjunction

Noor takes the handoff at 22:00 with the queue at nineteen boxes — nineteen close passes between objects in orbit, each to be screened for the odds it comes to a collision — and Reyes, the screener she is relieving, already into one sleeve of his coat.

He walks the queue standing, hip against the console, cracking sunflower seeds one-handed and dropping the shells into a paper cup he will leave for her to empty. Two operators pushed fresh ephemerides inside the hour, he says — updated predictions of where their satellites will fly — so the screening re-runs and the numbers move under you; trust nothing older than the last pass. One yellow, on the Halcyon platform — a fragment out of the '33 deorbit clearances, catalogued rough, probability of collision near six times ten to the minus six and dropping as more tracking fills the orbit in. Nothing red. Nobody to wake.

He signs his last determination while he talks, thumbs his operator ID into the field and lets the certification dialog take his PIN, and the record writes the minute, and the floor is hers.

"You're the name till oh-six," he says. He knocks the little brass plate someone bolted to the console years back — SIGNER OF RECORD, worn to a yellow shine where thumbs have hit it — and takes his cup of shells and goes. Since the reforms, one certified human owns every no-maneuver call on anything flying people — every decision to let a crewed spacecraft ride out a close pass instead of burning fuel to dodge — and after 22:00 that human is Noor.

The floor has one window. Past the reflection of her own screens the sky over Colorado's Front Range holds its usual wash, the low constellations strung across it corner to corner, the managed brightness that comes up every clear night now, and not one real star in it she could set a finger on. They run the sky from a room with no sight of it.

She takes the queue by time of closest approach. A weather bird and a dead upper stage: eleven kilometers radial at the miss, both covariances tight — each object's position well pinned down — Pc, the probability the two actually collide, two times ten to the minus six. Green. MONITOR. She keys the number into the determination field and signs — ID, PIN, dialog, timestamp — and the record commits, and the next box loads. The catalog runs to the tens of thousands now, most of it junk, and the screening throws her a few hundred close approaches a shift, and nearly all of them are eleven clean kilometers and a number with a lot of zeroes after the point. The work is the sorting. The work is deciding which of the hundred nothings might be the one thing.

Two other screeners work the far consoles tonight, heads down, the floor keeping the hum it always keeps — fans, disks, somebody's radio tuned low to nothing she can name. The catalog she reads is mostly what the '33 clearances left behind, broken small, and the boxes come faster every year for it. Two hundred and forty close approaches on her sector last shift. More tonight.

She clears four more before the top of the hour. A defunct relay and a spent kick stage, sixteen kilometers, green, signed. Two constellation birds a plane apart that the screening flags and she clears in a glance because the operator flies them to a keep-out tighter than anything she would call. A dead cubesat and a dead cubesat, which is the easiest box there is, two corpses that will drift past each other for a thousand years and never once be told to move. Each one a number, a disposition, a PIN, the record breathing shut behind her.

The night it happened she had this chair and a game of chess three days from its end.

Ade played Black and played it patient, the Caro-Kann every time, the pawn wall she would sit behind for thirty moves while you talked yourself into overreaching. She flew flight engineer on Vireo, the older platform, and between her tasks and Noor's boxes they moved a move a day through the crew relay, Ade signing off with the notation and nothing else — *...c6*, *...Bf5* — because words came out of her downlink allowance and a move said hello well enough. Taped by her sleep station she kept a paper photograph of a lake in Minnesota, the one thing of weight she had carried up, and once on a slow pass she turned the crew camera to it so Noor could see the dock and the two red chairs. Three days before, Ade had resigned a rook ending she could not hold, a pawn light off one loose move made twenty moves earlier, the loss already sitting in the position long before it surfaced on the board. She owed Noor White next game.

They had never met on the ground and never would have. Ade was six years older and four hundred kilometers up, a voice that reached Noor only as text and, twice, as a face gone soft and slow on a lagged downlink. The game was how they talked. A move was *I'm still here*. When Noor drew a bad shift, the kind where a box turns red at 04:00 and you spend the dark hours with a stranger's platform in your hands, she would find a move waiting from Ade in the morning, sent at some hour that made no sense until you remembered there is no night up there worth the name, only ninety-minute days and the work between them.

Once, the week she went up, Ade spent real words — four lines of downlink she must have hoarded against her allowance — to tell Noor that from four hundred kilometers the lake was a coin of light you could cover with a thumb, and that she had covered it with her thumb, and that it was still there when she took the thumb away. Noor read it three times and answered with a knight to f3, because she did not know what else to send a person that far up who missed a dock that badly.

The conjunction came up on the 03:00 screening. A fragment from the '33 clearances crossing Vireo's track, closing near thirteen kilometers a second. Noor worked it the way she worked all of them. She tasked more tracking. The covariance tightened over four passes, the state — the fix on where the fragment was and where it was headed — firmed, and the Pc settled at four times ten to the minus five. Below the line. The line is ten to the minus four and has been for years, and below it you do not spend a crewed platform's propellant to put it somewhere new in a crowded shell for a chance under one in twenty thousand — every maneuver is a move you cannot take back, and it opens conjunctions of its own that somebody then has to screen. She entered MONITOR. She keyed the number. She signed it and went to the next box, because fourteen sat behind it.

Closest approach was 04:41. What the floor did at 04:41 she holds in pieces, out of their order, and has stopped trying to set straight.

After is cleaner, because after is paperwork. Her badge pulled for the review inside the hour. The console imaged, the logs impounded, this chair flagged out of service for two days and then not. The shift lead that night set a hand flat on the console beside hers and left it there a while and said nothing, which was the kindest thing on offer and the only true one. Someone left a plate under foil on her porch step and was gone before she opened the door. She ate none of it and could not throw it out, and it sat in the refrigerator through the spring, a kindness she had no procedure for. The printout that went into evidence had her PIN-time along the bottom and the Pc in the body, four times ten to the minus five, the number she had read correctly off a screen she had built correctly and entered under her own name. She went in each day of the review and answered what they asked and drove home under the washed-out sky and did not look up, because there was nothing up there to see and everything up there to account for. Ade's lake stayed taped where it was until they brought the module down eleven weeks later, and then it did not.

For a while the resigned game stayed live in her account, the final position frozen one screen back where Ade's last message lived, White up a clean pawn, a win Noor never played out because there was no one across the board to play it against. She did not close it. She did not open it either. It is in there still, under a tab, the way the finding letter is in the drawer at home — a thing decided, filed, and impossible to throw away.

The review board sat eleven weeks.

They met in a windowless room two floors down with read-ahead binders tabbed in colors, and they reconstructed her shift to the second — the screening runs, the tasking requests, the passes, the covariance at each epoch, the moment she keyed MONITOR and the moment she signed. An independent team re-ran her Pc from the raw tracks on a different code base, blind to her result, and got four times ten to the minus five to two figures. The math was hers and the math was right. They asked whether more tracking could have been tasked, and earlier; the record showed she had tasked everything the schedule allowed, when procedure said to task it. They asked her to walk the covariance. She walked the covariance. The board's chair, a woman who refilled the water glasses herself from a steel carafe between questions so no one had to ask, wrote it all down.

Eleven weeks is a long time to be reconstructed. They had her keystroke log and the interval between passes to the second, and they asked the same questions in different clothes across the weeks — what she saw, when she saw it, why she signed when she signed — until her own night came back to her in their language instead of hers, a timeline with her name on the events. She sat through it in the same blazer every session, because choosing a second one felt like a decision she did not have in her. The recompute team never once looked at her, which was procedure too; they had to stay blind to her result to be worth anything. When their number came up matching hers to two figures, someone read it into the room like a weather report, and it was the loneliest thing she heard all spring — her arithmetic confirmed by strangers who had worked for months to see whether it would break, and it had not broken, because it had never been wrong.

The finding letter came in March. She has it in a drawer at home and does not need it in front of her to say the sentences. *The screening was conducted in accordance with established procedure. No deviation was identified. The certification decision was correct on the information available at the time of signature.* Not one line of it about fault, in either direction — the document is colder and more exact than that. It does not tell her she is not to blame. It tells her the determination was correct, which is a different finding, and the harder one to carry, because a correct determination is a thing you would make again.

At the bottom of the last page a field waited for her to acknowledge receipt. She thumbed her ID and entered her PIN and the record wrote the minute. Another signature. She had come to think of her whole life as a column of them.

Pc four times ten to the minus four, and the clock to closest approach reads three hours nine minutes.

For a second she does not move at all, and the floor keeps its hum, and the brass plate under her hand says whose call this is. Then she works.

She tasks it wide — every sensor with a horizon on that orbit, optical and radar both, priority override, which she can set because tonight she is the name on the plate. The first thing to understand is why the number jumped, and the number jumped because the fragment's covariance was loose, a long thin ellipsoid smeared down-track from a thin observation history, and a loose covariance is a liar in both directions: it can drown a real threat in uncertainty until the Pc reads safe, and it can throw up a four-times-ten-to-the-minus-four out of a miss that is actually clean, because when you barely know where a thing is, you cannot yet rule out that it is exactly where you are. You do not answer a loose covariance with a burn. You answer it with more looking.

She pulls the operator up on the coordination line. Halcyon's flight director is a tired man named Okwú who keeps a maneuver ready as a matter of standing procedure, a debris avoidance burn already planned and screened, seven-tenths of a meter a second retrograde, propellant costed, the crew briefed to secure for it if the call comes. He wants to know if the call is coming. She tells him she is refining the state and will have the next pass in twenty minutes and the pass after that in fifty, and that the last time she can accept his burn and still screen the orbit it puts him into is 03:52, because a maneuver only trades one position for another, and the new one has to be read for its own threats before you commit a platform to it. He knows this better than she does. He says he will hold. His breathing sits on the open line. She can hear his floor through him, the flat call-and-response of a crew running a contingency they have drilled a hundred times in the sim and never once for real, and under it, if she listens, the particular quiet of people waiting on a number that a stranger two states away is going to hand them.

Gene comes and stands at her shoulder and does not turn the coin.

The 02:41 pass lands. Twelve new tracks on the fragment, the observation arc stretched, the covariance drawing in from a smear to a fat cigar, and the miss distance opens as the ellipsoid tightens off her own object's ephemeris. She reruns the Pc. Two times ten to the minus four. Still over. Better. She does not say better out loud. Over the line is over the line, and Halcyon is nine people, and one of them keeps a photograph taped to a wall she will never see.

Chess is a game about the move you do not make. Ade taught her that behind the Caro-Kann, thirty moves of not-yet, of improving the position by a hair and handing the pressure back, of making the move only when the position forces it — because the player who moves for the sake of moving is the player who loses, and the position was usually decided long before either hand touched a piece. The fragment and Halcyon were on this track weeks ago. The geometry was set the day that thing came off the '33 clearances with the number it had. All Noor is doing tonight is reading a game already played, and deciding, on the strength of the reading, whether to reach in and change it.

02:58. The wide tasking pays. A radar site low on the horizon catches a long clean arc and the state firms hard, the covariance collapsing to something she can nearly trust, and when the fit converges the miss walks out to nine hundred meters cross-track with an ellipsoid that no longer overlaps her hull volume the way the loose one did. She reruns the Pc. One point three times ten to the minus four. A hair over. Okwú's breathing changes on the line.

Behind him his floor is doing the quiet arithmetic every crewed floor does with a live one — securing loose mass, briefing the burn attitude, nine people told to stay near the node and off the cupola for a closest approach they will ride through blind at thirteen kilometers a second whether or not the thing is there. One of them is running the checklist Ade used to run. Noor does not follow that thought to the wall it is taped to. She has a number to firm and forty-nine minutes to firm it in.

She has fifty-one minutes to his deadline and one more scheduled pass at 03:31, and the whole grammar of the night comes down to whether she trusts the trend or trusts the number in front of her. The number in front of her is over the line. The trend is a covariance still tightening and a miss still opening, pass on pass, the way a firming state is supposed to behave when the two things are actually going to miss. If she waits for 03:31 and it drops under the line, she signs a no-maneuver and leaves nine people to ride through a nine-hundred-meter miss at thirteen kilometers a second on the strength of her arithmetic. If she calls the burn now, she spends Okwú's seven-tenths of a meter a second and puts Halcyon somewhere new and never knows what the fragment would have done, and neither does anyone else, and that not-knowing is its own soft place to land — no one writes a board report on the collision that didn't happen because you moved.

That is the whole seduction of the burn, and Ade would have named it: the move that feels like safety because it puts the game somewhere no one can grade. You can always defend a maneuver. The wait is only ever defensible by the number, and the number is a thing she has to trust further than her own hands — hands that have keyed a correct call before, on a screen exactly this one, at an hour not far off this one.

The 03:10 look she counts on comes back thin. The optical site she wanted is socked in under weather on the Pacific, three tracks where she asked for thirty, and for six minutes the covariance stops shrinking and simply sits, and she has to read whether a stalled fit is a fluke of cloud or the state telling her it has found its true shape a hair over the line. She holds. Weather is weather. She has worked enough degraded passes to know the difference between a state that has converged and a fit that has only run out of light to see by.

She waits.

She waits the way Ade would have waited, sitting behind the pawns, and it is the hardest single thing she does all night, harder than the eleven weeks, harder than the drawer at home. Okwú counts her down without meaning to, just his breath and the low hum of his floor behind him. Gene stands and does not turn the coin. The queue behind Halcyon has grown to nine while she has held on this one box, greens stacking up unsigned because she is the only name on the floor and her hands are full of nine people. She lets them stack. A screener who signs a green fast to clear her plate is a screener who signs the wrong green fast some other night. She keeps the box in front of her and her eyes on the covariance and her thumb, without deciding it, on the brass plate. The clock burns down through 03:20. Okwú says nothing and does not hang up. The 03:31 pass comes in four minutes late and she does not breathe through the fit, and the state firms one more notch, and the miss holds at nine hundred and forty meters, and the covariance is a tight bean now, honest, no longer lying in either direction. She reruns the Pc.

Seven times ten to the minus five.

Under the line. One in fourteen thousand. Real, and small, and hers.

"Sixteen minutes to your deadline," she tells Okwú. "I'm reading seven times ten to the minus five on a converged state, nine-forty cross-track and opening. I'm not going to take your burn."

The line is quiet for a second. "Confirmed no maneuver," Okwú says, in the flat voice a man uses to write a thing into a log, and he does write it into a log, and somewhere above the wash of the sky his nine people stand down from a burn they mostly never knew was loaded. He does not thank her. He signs off. That is the courtesy — he leaves her to sign it alone.

She keys the Pc into the determination field, seven times ten to the minus five, and the TCA — the time of closest approach — 05:29, and the converged epoch, and the miss components, radial and in-track and cross-track, the numbers she read correctly off a screen she built correctly, entered under her own name. The certification dialog rises and asks whether she, the signer of record, certifies this determination on a crewed asset, and a second field asks her to confirm the maneuver disposition, NONE, and she confirms it, and she puts her PIN into the dialog a digit at a time the way she has put in ten thousand PINs, and her thumb finds the brass plate on the top of the console the way Reyes's did on his way out, worn yellow, cool under the pad of her thumb. She presses enter.

## 7. Deorbit

The morning's job was the potable line in the node, and Yuki bled it down into a contingency bag at forty milliliters a pull, reading the pressure off the gauge between pulls and calling the numbers to the floor in Houston. When the line ran dry she capped the quick disconnect, safed the pump, and marked the step complete on the laptop. That was the fourteenth line she had drained since the crew went home and left her alone aboard the station. Six more, then the racks, then the lights.

The tug — the deorbit vehicle sent up to bring the station down — had mated at the aft port the week before she started the closeout. It had come up empty and drunk propellant from the ground ever since, its tanks filling for the burns that would come after she was gone — apogee first, the orbit walked down over three weeks, then the last shove over the South Pacific to put what was left of Meridian in the water at Point Nemo, where they put these things. She had watched it dock through the cupola. She had not gone back to the cupola.

## 8. The Ellipse

**1,900 km × 210 km**

Dell leveled the tripod on the pad her husband Clay had poured her east of the propane tank, ran the wedge down to the latitude scratched in the aluminum, and had the camera recording before the star cleared the cedars. Eleventh magnitude, a nothing in Capricornus. The rock crossing it that night owned a nine-day arc and no name a person would keep — a string off the prediction list that afternoon, path center two counties north, the error so fat the whole state lay inside the maybe. The rock's ellipse — the stretch of ground it might come down on, if it came down at all — ran nineteen hundred kilometers by two hundred ten that first August, a smear clear across the plains, and the odds were still long. She timed it standing, red lamp on her chest, the welder's stool too cold in August to sit for long. You time the wide ones. Your chord — the line the rock's shadow draws across the ground, caught off the star's blink — is the thing that makes them narrow.

The star stepped out at 03:14 and change and came back a tenth of a second later, one clean drop and one clean climb on the light curve, and she read the times off the inserter — the box that stamps true time onto every frame of video — before she trusted her own eyes to them.

# PART IV — NEIGHBORS

An apocalypse, properly managed, is a neighbor. It moves in slowly, borrows things, changes the light. The bees go, the wire comes down, the salt walks up the creek — and the town, being a town, holds a meeting about it.

What these stories know is that loss at walking pace does not feel like catastrophe. It feels like work: brushes and ladders, splices and spar varnish, the long habit of keeping something running past the point where keeping it running makes sense to anyone but the people doing it. That habit has a name. Nobody in these towns would use it.

## 9. The Pollinators

Ximena weighs the pollen before light, on the tailgate, under the dome lamp, because the scale reads truer when the wind is down and her hands are still too cold to shake it. Almond pollen, the Monterey variety, cut one part to three with lycopodium — the yellow club-moss spore mixed in to stretch it — the company's tin marked forty grams. The scale says thirty-eight point six. She writes both numbers in the composition book — forty, thirty-eight six — and initials the gap in the margin, so that when Vogel, the contractor, calls to ask why the block ran long she can read him the shortfall out of his own tin before he starts in on her crew.

The fog is down to the second wire of the end posts. She can see her truck and Nardo's and the near edge of the Nonpareil — the crop variety these rows are planted to — and past that the block goes white. A Nonpareil will not set a nut from its own flower; it needs pollen from another variety carried to it, and the bees used to be what carried it. The bees are gone now — hive and hum, years gone — and the pollen comes by hand instead, in a tin, on a brush. Twelfth-leaf trees, twelve springs in the ground, planted the year before the district went over to the self-fertile stock, which is the only reason the crew has work: the new orchards set their own crop and need nobody, and these old Nonpareil rows still need a stranger to carry the pollen from the Monterey two rows over, and the stranger, this February, at eleven dollars a tree, is them.

"Ya mero," she says down the line of trucks, not loud. The dome lamps come on one after another the length of the turnrow — the bare end-lane where the trucks stand for the night — and the crew climbs out into the cold stiff-legged, the way you climb out of a truck you slept sitting up in.

Beto is out first, because Beto is nineteen and sleeps like the dead and wakes like a thrown switch. He has his phone up over his head already, walking the turnrow with his arm straight out, hunting the one bar that lives near the canal. The screen is cracked corner to corner; he reads through it the way you read through a windshield after a rock. "Nothing till the canal," he says, to no one, to the fog.

"Escalera first, señal after," Ximena says.

"Trees don't go nowhere, tía."

"Neither does the canal."

She hands out the tins off the scale, each crew a share weighed to the tenth of a gram and written against the name, because pollen is the one thing on this turnrow worth stealing and the only thing Vogel counts as close as she does. Soledad takes hers in both hands like a woman taking the host. Soledad is seventy and works the low branches now, no ladder past the third rung, in wool gloves she has cut the fingers off so the brush sits right, and she hums the whole day at a pitch you feel in the ladder more than hear, and none of them hurry her because a bloom she has touched is a bloom that sets. Prieto clips his tin to his belt without looking at it and screws the cap of a green steel thermos he will not share and does not need to, since everyone knows it is coffee and rum and everyone knows he does not miss a flower drunk or sober. Nardo is already at his fire drum, feeding it the split oak he hauls block to block in his truck bed like a man carrying his own hearth up the valley, and the crew draws toward the warmth of it the way iron draws, and Ximena lets them have three minutes of it and not four.

Chente comes last, and slow, both hands pushed up under his arms, his hat down. He is too old for the tall ladder and everyone knows it and nobody says it, so his job is the ground: he mixes the day's pollen off her scale, keeps the tins dry, walks the water jugs up the rows. He carries a beekeeper's hive tool in his coat, the flat J-hook kind gone to a soft shine at the grip, and he uses it now to pry the lid off the first tin the way another man would use a screwdriver, because it is the tool his hand learned first. He ran bees once. He trucked them up from Bakersfield every February when the boxes still came, four hundred colonies on a flatbed, and set them at the ends of blocks exactly like this one. Ximena has heard him say the word *hum* about those mornings the way her grandmother said the names of saints. She has never once heard him say what came of the four hundred, because you do not have to. Nobody's bees made it. That is the whole of the news and it is years old, and at the south end of the block the empty pallets are still stacked where the semis used to back in, gray now, going soft, half sunk in the orchard floor, and nobody moves them, because moving them costs a morning and leaving them costs nothing.

They wait out the cold the way you wait it out, by working slower than the money wants. Pollen will not take under fifty-five degrees; the stigma — the flower's wet center, where the pollen has to land — sits shut, the tube will not run, and a flower dabbed in the fog is a gram of Monterey thrown on the ground. So they start light, the low outside branches where the sun will find them first, brushes soft in the open blooms. You do not hit every flower. You cannot; a tree in full bloom carries thousands and the clock carries you. You hit the clusters, the just-opened ones with the petals bent all the way back and the stigma standing up wet, enough of them for a set, and you move your ladder and you go again. A good hand does twenty-five, thirty trees between the fog lifting and the dark. Beto does more and is rough doing it. Ximena does fewer and misses nothing, and up the ladder in the cold she works the brush the way her mother taught her to sign a name, small and sure, no flourish, the flourish is where the ink runs.

Midmorning Beto over-reaches from the ladder for a limb he should have moved to, and the back pole finds the soft place where the last irrigation ran, and the whole thing goes out from under him slow, the way soft ground takes a ladder, and he steps off onto the berm laughing before it can drop him. Ximena is two trees down and does not laugh. She walks over and sets the ladder herself, drives the pole into the hard crown of the row where a pole belongs, and stands on the second rung and rocks it to show him it will not move. A man off a ladder in this county goes to the clinic in Los Baños, cash at the window, and does not pollinate the days his wrist is in the splint, and there is no line in Vogel's book for a wrist. Beto knows that number as well as she does. He resets the pole into the crown, once, and then goes back to reaching.

By ten the fog pulls back off the Diablo Range to the west and the brown of it comes up, the canyon mouths, the aqueduct laid silver along the foot of the hills. The block opens green-gray and white to the far end where the grower's sign leans — a name, a number, and under it in smaller letters the words they all post now, POLLINATION IN PROGRESS, as if it were road work, as if thirty people on ladders with brushes were a thing that happened to a road.

The tin rides in the cab at night with the doors locked, because a gram of viable Monterey trades for more than the day a hand spends putting it on the trees, and there are crews up the valley who buy stolen grams and ask no questions, and Vogel would sooner lose the far corner than the tin. Ximena has weighed out a whole season of it, gram by gram, into hands that have never in their lives held a thing worth stealing and now hold it every morning and give all of it back to the flowers.

Vogel calls at eleven. She takes it standing on the fifth rung, brush hand hooked over the top.

"How's the block."

"Two days. Rain Thursday. We finish tomorrow if the fog lifts by nine."

"You've got till Wednesday dark. Thursday I need you in Gustine, the Gonsalves block, Nonpareil and Monterey, same as this."

"Then send pollen. Your tin came short today. Thirty-eight six on a forty."

A pause that costs him nothing and could cost her the rain. "Weigh it again," Vogel says.

"I weighed it twice." She always weighs it twice. She weighs it a third time after he hangs up, alone, the scale on the tailgate and the wind down, and it says thirty-eight point six, and she writes it a third time under the first two and does not initial it again, because a number written down three times initials itself.

"There's no bees, Lupe."

"That's what I said." Lupe presses a tortilla, flips it, does not look up. "He says he's got one."

By afternoon the whole crew has it. She can tell from the far end of the block without a word, the way you read a change in a crew across forty acres: they work faster and they work happier, which are not the same thing and today are the same thing. Beto comes down to move his ladder and instead crosses two rows to Chente and says something, and Chente shakes his head once, kind, the way you refuse a child a thing you are glad he wants, and Beto goes back lit up like the canal finally gave him his bar.

"He won't show it," Beto tells her, throwing the ladder too hard into the next tree so the top rung barks the bark. "Says a bee won't fly under fifty-five, says you open the jar in this fog it dies. Says you got to keep it warm against you." He is nineteen and he says all of it like he learned it in a classroom, like it is a fact about the world and not a thing an old man told him at the foot of a ladder. "It's sleeping. He keeps it in his coat."

"Mueve la escalera, Beto."

"If the bees are coming back—"

"Move the ladder."

He moves it. But he moves it the way a man moves who has been told there is water two rows over in a dry year, and Ximena stands with her brush in the Nonpareil in the one good hour the fog gives back and does not tell him what she knows, which is that she has handed Chente that coat off the truck seat on cold mornings, has held it by the collar and shaken the road dust out of it, and there has never been a jar in it, or the weight of one.

They quit at dark because the flowers close and the pollen quits, not because anyone is tired in a way that would stop them if the flowers stayed open. Camp is the turnrow and the trucks pulled nose to tail and Nardo's drum throwing light on the near trees. Ximena squares the day in the composition book by the fire. Twenty-nine trees on Beto. Twenty-two on Prieto. Eighteen on Soledad, who is seventy and works the low branches and whom nobody hurries. Grams issued against grams applied, the tin short by one point four, the shortfall carried down the margin in her own hand where Vogel can read it Friday and choose to fight her or not. She reads the crew their numbers if they ask and most nights they do not; they trust her book the way they trust Lupe's, completely and without affection, which is the only trust worth having on a turnrow in February.

The plates go around. Lupe feeds the crew first and squares it after, tacos de frijol and the café de olla, and she reads her own book to whoever pays her tonight and to whoever is putting it on Friday, four slashes on Prieto, six on Beto who eats like the trees are chasing him. They figure the week out loud the way they figure it every night, aloud so no one has to figure it alone. Twenty-eight trees a day at eleven, less the days the fog eats, less what Lupe carries, less the diesel to Gustine, less the wire home to Michoacán or to the room in Firebaugh where somebody's mother is holding rent — and what is left is a number small enough that a man will stand on a ladder in the fog to reach it and big enough that he will drive the length of the valley chasing the bloom north to reach it again. Prieto tips the green thermos into his coffee and says the season used to run longer. It always used to run longer. Nardo says nothing and feeds the drum.

Somebody has named the bee. Beto swears it is a female and Chava swears it is the last of the ones his tío kept in Jalisco, come all this way to find them, and Soledad says leave it be, a thing with that much work ahead of it does not need a name laid on top, and the fire laughs, and the naming goes on anyway, low, the way a man keeps turning over a coin he has already spent.

Then the radio. Chava keeps a radio, a little silver one, D batteries, La Campesina out of Fresno when the hills let it through and whatever else can climb the Diablo and fall down into the valley at night when they don't. Tonight it is not La Campesina. Tonight it is some station out of the Bay, a man with a smooth churchless voice talking about the sky, and the crew half-listens the way you half-listen to the weather for a county you are not in.

*—the Adeyemi Signature,* the smooth voice says, *five years on, and the consensus has held. The combination of gases in that atmosphere has no natural source anyone can model. Something out there is making them. We will almost certainly never know what, never speak to it, never be spoken to. And still, tonight, we know we are not the only—*

"Y qué," Nardo says to the fire, and spits, and the drop hisses on the drum.

"Somebody, though." Beto tips his head back at the dark over the valley, the sky gone the low dull orange of the valley's own light, no stars in it worth the name. "Somebody all the way out there, and down here we can't even keep—" He does not finish it. He looks across the fire at Chente instead of finishing it.

Chente sits on an upturned bucket with his coat closed and both hands folded into it against his middle, the way a man sits who is cold in the belly, or the way a man sits who is keeping something warm. He does not look at Beto. He looks at the fire.

"Show us, viejo," Chava says. Half the fire says it with him, warm, not mean, the way you ask a man to tell the story you already know once more before you sleep.

"You open it in this cold, it dies." Chente works his hands inside the coat. "You want that? You want to be the fog that killed it?" He says it easy, half a smile in it, but he closes the coat with the hive-tool hand, the flat of the tool laid down the front of him like a man laying his palm over his own heart, and the fire lets it go. A crew that has decided to believe a thing will help the thing along; they will build the fence around it themselves and stand the watch on it, and no one at this fire wants to be the fog.

"My grandfather kept bees, up in Chihuahua," Chente says, to the coals, not to any of them. "The year they came bad the first time, the priest walked the boxes with the water and blessed them, every box, and the next spring half came back." His thumb moves on the tool. "Half. He always told the half like it was the miracle. Not the other half. The half."

Nobody answers that, and the not-answering is its own kind of amen. Soledad crosses herself small under her cut gloves, and Prieto tips the green thermos to it, and even Beto is quiet, and Ximena sits with her book closed on her knee and lets them keep the warmth she is not going to feed and is not going to put out. The fire falls. Lupe wipes the comal and stows it cold. The couples fold into their cabs and the young ones climb under the tarps in the truck beds, and the turnrow goes dark but for the drum's last red eye, and still Ximena does not sleep, because she is running Thursday and it will not come out. Rain Thursday. Gustine Thursday. If the fog lifts by nine they finish the far corner; if it does not they leave it short and Vogel docks the block and the crew eats the dock, and there is no line in any book of hers where the dock comes out of Vogel. She lies in the cab and runs it and runs it and it will not close, and at last she gets up to check the tins are covered, because a wet gram is a lost gram and a lost gram is a docked hour, and that is when she finds him.

···

Chente is awake, out past the last truck, at the lip where the turnrow gives onto the block, his back to the camp and his face to the white where the fog has come down again over the Nonpareil. He has the jar out.

It is a pint mason jar, an old one, the lid punched through with nail holes the way you punch a lid for a thing that has to breathe. He holds it up under his chin, out of the wind, turned so what little light there is falls through it. Ximena stands at the corner of the truck and looks through the glass with him, because there is nothing else to do with a jar a man is holding up to the light. The jar is empty. No bee. No fly gone stiff in the bottom, no husk, no wing. Clean glass and punched tin and the cold coming through the holes he made for a breath that isn't in there.

He hears her. He does not turn.

"You'll take cold out here," Ximena says. It is what she would say to any of them.

"I don't sleep good. The hip." He lowers the jar but he does not hide it; he holds it now at his side in the one hand, the way you hold a lamp you have stopped needing and have not set down. "You came to check the tins."

"I came to check the tins."

"They're covered. I covered them at dark." He gives it back to her flat, a man returning her own count. Then, into the fog, not to her: "Firebaugh. Three days back. You want to know what's in the Firebaugh block? Same as here. Nonpareil, Monterey, a hundred acres of the self-fertile that don't need us, the pallets rotting at the end, and nothing in the air but us." He turns the jar in his hand. "There's nothing in it. You saw. You've been seeing since Lupe carried it up the row."

The fog moves between the trees. Somewhere down the block a coyote says its one thing and quits. She could name it now — walk the jar to the drum in the morning and stand it on the steel and let the crew see the clean glass and the punched lid and let the whole thing go out of them like heat off a stone left out of the fire. It is the kind of true thing her book is full of. There is a version of the morning where she owes it to them the way she owes them their grams counted honest.

She does not reach for it. She does not lay her hand over it either, does not tell him to keep it, does not say the story is a good one, does not bless the glass. She stands with him in the cold and looks at the block going white, forty acres of Nonpareil that will not set one nut without a stranger to carry the pollen tree to tree, and she does the one true thing she is sure of, which is the count.

"Twenty-nine on Beto today," she says. "He hasn't done twenty-nine since Firebaugh. Prieto twenty-two. Soledad eighteen, and she's seventy." She lets it sit. "The block was going to run short. It won't now."

Chente looks at the jar. "So," he says.

"So the tins are covered," Ximena says, "and you'll take cold out here."

She goes back to her truck. She does not turn to see whether he puts the jar in his coat, because she does not need to; she knows the weight of an empty thing carried like a full one, and she knows a man will carry it either way.

···

The next morning the fog lifts by half past eight, and they finish the far corner by three with a gram to spare, which she writes down and initials, and Vogel does not call, which is Vogel's way of saying the block came out. They break camp in the last of the light. Nardo's drum goes cold and rides in Nardo's bed. Lupe's comal goes cold and rides in Lupe's. Soledad folds her cut gloves into her coat and sleeps sitting up before the trucks even start. Beto walks the turnrow one last time with his arm up hunting the canal, and gets his bar, and stands there thumbing the cracked screen, sending it down the valley ahead of them the way the young send everything, faster than any truck, to the cousins and the compas already carrying ladders into the Gonsalves block in Gustine.

Chente climbs up slow into his cab and sets the jar on the dash, punched lid up, in front of a San Judas sticker gone pale in the windshield sun, patron of lost and desperate things. It will ride the forty miles to Gustine in the morning with nothing in it, catching the light through the holes he made. Ximena sees it there at dawn when she walks the line to count heads and trucks, and she lets it ride.

They pull out onto 33 north in a string, nose to tail, the Diablo going gold on the left and the aqueduct running silver at the foot of it and the whole white morning ahead of them full of Nonpareil that will not set without them. And when they turn in at the Gonsalves block, past the leaning sign and the strange crew already working the far rows, a kid Ximena has never seen comes down off his ladder and crosses the turnrow to Beto's truck, grinning, before the engines are even off.

"Oye," the kid says. "Ustedes son los de la abeja."

## 10. Tonewood

I had the top pinched between my thumb and one finger at the corner, the way you hold a plate so it can still ring, and I tapped it once with my knuckle and it came up around an F, a shade sharp of it, and that was when you asked me whether it was true the salt had made the wood worth more.

Fair question. Wrong one, but fair, and since you flew in on it and you've set your case open on my bench like the guitar is already yours, you may as well have the whole answer. It runs about as long as the glue takes to reach. Sit where you won't put your elbow in the shellac.

This is red spruce. Picea rubens, the Acadian spruce, the same wood the old Boston houses put in their good guitars before the war and then couldn't get, because the stands that would give you a soundboard were logged off by 1930 and what grew back grew fast and loose and useless for anything but a stud. My grandfather's father left forty acres of it standing on the back of the marsh when everyone around him was cutting, and nobody in the family ever knew whether that was foresight or laziness, and it doesn't matter, because the trees can't tell you which and they came out the same either way. Tight. Slow. Rings you could barely fit a fingernail between. My father used to hold a fresh round up to me and make me count to twenty before my thumb had crossed an inch of it.

You want to see what makes a top. Come to the window. Tilt this so the light runs down it — there. Watch how the one half goes bright and the other half goes dark, and the line where they trade sits dead down the center joint. That's the two halves reading their grain against the light the same and opposite, because they were split off one billet and opened like a book, and where that bright-and-dark flips even, both sides matched, that's low runout. Runout is the grain diving under the surface instead of running flat along it. You can't see it looking straight on. You see it in the flip. And every degree the grain dives is stiffness leaking out the side of the board where you can't get it back, so my father wouldn't saw a top, ever. He split them. Rived them off the billet with a froe and a maul, let the wood choose its own plane, because a saw cuts where you point it and a split follows the grain wherever the grain already goes. He said sawing a soundboard is telling the wood where it lives. Splitting is asking it.

He taught me the tap on this same stool, when I was small enough he had to hold the plate for me and I only got to knock it. You pinch the plate at the corner where a mode line runs cold, the node, the place the wood isn't moving, and you tap out over the belly where it is, and you don't listen for loud. You listen for long. A live red spruce plate rings and then keeps ringing, a bell held under water, and a dead one gives you a knock and quits. He'd tap and name the note off it. G. A-flat. He named them out of a lifetime of it the way Marj up the marsh names the tide without looking, and half the time I couldn't hear the note and I could always hear the difference, the ring against the knock, which is the only ear the work actually needs. He'd thin a top with a scraper and tap it, thin it and tap it, chasing the note down and the ring up — voicing, he called it — until a plate you could read newsprint through rang like something twice its weight. Then he'd put it in my hands and make me knock it and say nothing, only watch my face, because he said your face hears it a second before your head will own up to it.

···

The marsh is why any of it was there. You drove in over it and you didn't know what you were looking at, so I'll tell you, because the marsh is half the answer and you only asked for the wood half.

Everything flat you crossed on the dyke road was salt marsh once, Spartina and mud, flooded twice a day by the Fundy tide, and the Fundy tide is not a tide you have where you're from. Fifty feet of it, top to bottom, at the head of the bay. It comes up the Cobequid a wall of brown and it goes out and leaves the bottom of the world showing for a mile. Two hundred years ago the Acadians walled it off — built the dykes by hand out of the marsh sod itself, cut in blocks, and set an aboiteau at every low place where a creek wanted through. An aboiteau's just a sluice through the dyke with a wooden gate hung in it, a clapper, hinged at the top. Fresh water and rain push it open from behind and drain out at low tide. The salt tide pushes it shut. It swings closed against the sea and it opens for the land, and it does that twice a day on its own with nobody watching it, and behind it the salt leaches down out of the ground over years until the marsh turns sweet and you can hay it, pasture it, and up on the high back edge where it's been sweet longest, grow forty acres of the tightest spruce on the eastern seaboard.

That's the whole trick. The dyke keeps the sea off and the aboiteau does the keeping. My father walked ours every morning of his life. He had a tide clock on the shop wall, brass, older than me, and he set it to the marsh and not to the town, and he wound it before he wound anything else, because a man who works below a fifty-foot tide keeps the tide's time and lets the town keep its own.

You learn a tide like that in your feet before your head gets it — that the ground you're standing on is bottom, that twice a day the bay comes up over where you're standing to the height of a house and goes out again, and that the only things between the two facts are a wall of dug sod a dead man cut by hand and a wooden gate that shuts itself. You don't take it for granted, raised on it. You watch the low spot in the creek. Every spring the marsh body walks the whole run of the dyke looking for a soft place, a muskrat run, a rot coming up through the sod, because a dyke doesn't fail all at once. It fails at one bad foot of itself, and then the tide finds the hole and does the rest of it in a night.

Two years ago the province put our marsh on the realignment schedule. I'll say it the once and then leave it, because it's not the part that matters and you'll want to make it the part that matters. Realignment means they stop keeping the dyke. Sea-level's up, the dykes all want raising, and there's more marsh in this province than money to wall it, so they ran the numbers and drew a line, and marshes below the line get given back to the tide on a date. They stop the crews. They quit the aboiteau. They let the gate rot open and the sea comes home on its own schedule after that. Ours was down for the spring after next. We had two harvests coming before the water did.

We got none of them the way we planned.

···

Marj found it. Marj farms the next parcel down and she walks the dyke every morning the way my father did, boots rolled to her knee, and she keeps the marsh-body's tide log in a duck-cloth book she's had since before the book meant anything, and she came up the shop lane one morning in April with the book still in her hand and told me the aboiteau at the low creek was standing open on a flood tide.

Not rotted open. This was a year and a half before the province was going to let it rot. Standing open on the flood, the clapper hung back on its hinge, and something under it holding it back — a jam of driftwood, she said, could've been driftwood, a cut length of two-by wedged up under the gate where the current wouldn't have set it square, could've been the tide put it there. Could've been. We stood on the dyke and looked at it and neither of us finished the sentence, because you don't, and the salt was already three floods into the marsh by the time she saw it and there's no getting salt back out of the ground once it's in. You can shut the gate. You can't unsalt the mud. The tide had come home a year and a half early and let itself in the low creek and stood in the ground.

I want you to hear how slow it is, because you'll be imagining a flood and it wasn't a flood. It was three weeks. The salt goes down into the ground water and the ground water is what the spruce roots drink, and spruce has no salt in it and never learned to, and it browns from the inside on its own time. The needles went first, up on the back forty, a rust coming into the green that you'd take for a dry spell if you didn't know. Then the small stuff on the low edge died outright and stood there dead. By June I had forty acres turning the color of a burnt-out stove, standing dead on their feet, a whole woodlot of snags, and you can drive people out from the city to see a thing like that now and they have a word for it, they call it a ghost forest, they take a picture of it and they feel the way they came to feel.

My father was eighty-one that spring and he'd stopped coming to the shop by then, mostly, but he made me drive him out to the back forty when the brown came up, and he got out at the field edge and stood on his cane and looked at it a long time, and he didn't say the province and he didn't say the gate and he didn't say who. He said, *well, they're cut now,* the way you'd say it of a thing already down and bucked and stacked, though every one of them was standing. And he got back in the truck.

I'll tell you what I know and no more, because the rest is other people's mouths. My father knew that aboiteau better than the man who hung the last gate in it. He'd have sooner drowned that marsh with his own hands than watched a provincial crew walk off and let the sea take it on a Tuesday to a schedule. He said as much, more than once, to Marj, to me, into his tea. And my brother Curtis, who'd been at Dad for three years to take the buyout and be done, who'd already had the standing timber cruised and carried the number around on his phone like a man carrying a winning ticket he can't cash — Curtis knew what the wood would do the day it went scarce. Curtis knew that better than anyone. The both of them had a reason and neither of them has a alibi that means anything on a marsh where the nearest witness is a heron, and the RCMP came out the once, wrote *driftwood, undetermined,* and closed it, and I have never asked either one of them the question straight because there is no version of the answer I could carry back across the yard.

I'll give you one more thing off that morning and then I'll leave it. When I went down to shut the gate myself, ahead of the crew, my father's froe was leaning in the grass at the head of the aboiteau. His froe. The one he'd split every top of my life with, that lived on a nail on that wall behind you. I carried it home and hung it back on its nail and I have never once asked him how it came to be a mile down at the low creek on legs that could barely make it across the dooryard. The froe could have gone down there a hundred honest ways. I can't name you one of them. Both of those are true at the same time, and I've decided that's the ground I live on now.

So I don't know. That's the true thing. I don't know, and I built nine guitars out of not knowing, and I'll build no more.

···

Here's where you come in, you and yours, and I'll try to keep the meanness out of it because you didn't do the individual harm, you just made the weather that the harm pays off in.

The wood went scarce in a morning. Forty acres of red spruce, the last of the last, standing dead — and dead standing is not ruined, that's the thing people get wrong. A spruce that dies on its feet in April and comes down before the next winter's checks and blue-stain get into it is sound wood, better than sound, because it seasoned two seasons standing before I ever touched a saw to it. The tree died upright. It didn't drown lying in the mud. I got a crew in with a skidder before the ground froze and we took the best of them down that fall — felled, bucked, hauled up to the yard — and I quartered the good rounds into cants on the mill and cut the sap side off every one where the salt had gone into it grey. Out of the heart of them I split billets with the same froe he used, followed the grain the way he'd shown me, and I stickered them up in the loft over the shop to come down slow through the winter, a sticker every foot, weights on top, the woodstove going below them. And all that winter I did the one thing that tells you anything. I tapped them. Held each plate at the node and knocked it and listened for long and not for loud. Most of them rang. Some came up dead — a knock and done, the salt gone too deep to hear past — and those I split for kindling and fed to the stove, and I was warm off my own inheritance for a winter.

And the phone started. From your side of the country and then from farther. Because the story had got out ahead of the wood the way a story does, the last spruce off the drowned marsh, the ghost-forest wood, and men who had never in their lives held a guitar wanted a guitar made of it. A luthier three provinces over called to buy my billets green off the mill and I said no. A dealer offered me for one top what my father cleared off the whole woodlot in a good year of Christmas trees. There is a grey haze come into some of the wood now, up off the medullary rays, where the salt sat in it, and the market has decided to call that salt figure and to pay a third again for a top that shows it. It's a stain. It doesn't move the tap tone the width of a hair. They pay for it.

You tapped this top with two knuckles when you came in, hard, the way a man knocks on a door, and you laid your ear down against it and nodded like it had told you something. It hadn't. Two knuckles damp it dead; you hang it off one point and hit it once, and I've been telling you that all afternoon. But you nodded, and I let it go, because a man who has already settled what a thing means doesn't tap it to hear it. He taps it to be seen with his ear against the wood.

You asked me at the door for a certificate of provenance. You want the paper that says these acres, this marsh, the drowning, the date. You're four years down the list for the dark preserve out in Nevada, you told me on the drive, you can't see a star off your own deck any more for the strings of satellites you helped put up there, and now you'd like the paper that proves the wood you're buying came off the last of a thing before the last of it went. You have got a real taste for the end of things. It's an expensive taste and you can afford it and I don't hold it against you personally.

But I want you to understand what the paper would be certifying. It would certify that this wood came out of a family fight I can't see the bottom of and a gate that hung open on a flood tide and my father standing on a cane calling live trees cut. It would certify the worst spring of my life. And you'd pay more for the guitar with that paper stapled to the case than without it, and that — that right there, the paper being worth money — that's the whole thing I can't get square, and I'm not going to get it square for you tonight because I haven't got it square for myself in two years of standing at this bench.

Because here is what I actually know, the only thing I'd sign my name under. I know the tap tone. I hold a plate at the node and I tap it and it rings or it doesn't, and that ring is stiffness and mass and the twenty rings to the inch my grandfather's father left standing, and it does not know one thing about the marsh or the gate or my brother's phone. The wood is exactly as good as it was the day before the salt, to the hair. Everything that's changed is the story we've hung on it. And I have watched grown men lay their hand on a dead-common Sitka top and go quiet, and I've watched them tap this and hear nothing and buy it anyway for the paper, and I cannot for the life of me tell you which of us is the fool — the man paying for the story, or me, standing here telling you the story doesn't matter while I build guitars I won't sell.

···

Nine of them. I built nine out of the good billets and every one of them went, and I priced them fair by the tap and not by the tide and I still cleared more than I have in any three years, which I have not spent, because I don't yet know what that money is.

This one is the tenth. It's the last. There's wood behind it in the rack but not top wood, not heart, and I'm not going to cut the story any thinner than one more guitar. I built it out of the best billet on the property, the one my father set aside years back and never told me why, quartered so clean the flip runs dead even the whole length, twenty-two rings to the inch and not a spot of runout you could measure. I resawed it on the bandsaw into plates a hair over a fifth of an inch, took the two off the center split for the top, and shot the joint by hand on the board until the edges met with no light showing between them and held themselves shut against the window with no glue in them yet — that's the test, if the two halves stay closed against the light on their own they're ready. Jointed them, then thicknessed the top down to two point nine millimeters over the lower bout where the sound lives and thinner out to the rim. Bent the sides over the hot pipe, mahogany off a board I'd had twenty years, and clamped them to the mould to cool overnight. Bracing off the same spruce tree, scalloped and voiced by hand and by tap till the top came alive under my knuckle. Hot hide glue, 192 gram, the pot at a hundred forty-five, and I'll tell you why hide glue when the modern stuff is stronger — because hide glue lets go. Heat it, wet it, and a joint I closed today comes back apart in fifty years for the man who has to fix it. It's the one thing I build that's made to be undone. Everything else on this instrument I mean to hold.

I strung it up three weeks ago. It's done. And when your money came in over the wire this afternoon, the full number, more than the nine before it put together, I sent it back, and you drove out here to stand in my shop and ask me why to my face, and the answer is I don't know that either, and I'm done pretending to you that I do. It's not for sale. Take your case. It's a long drive to the airport and the tide's making on the dyke road, so watch the low spot by the creek or you'll be swimming for your plane.

···

That night I turned the sign, and I killed the shop lights but the one over the bench, and I wound the tide clock the way he did, first, before anything, and it read the flood coming full on the marsh in the dark outside.

I took the guitar down off the wall hook. I sat on the stool in the sawdust with the one light on it and I tuned it by ear the way he tuned, low string first, and my thumb found the twenty rings under the finish without looking for them.

The top let the note go whole and then held the tail of it long past where the room should have taken it, longer, the way the marsh holds the top of a spring tide a full minute past the flood before it turns for home, and I sat in the dark below the tide and did not stop it.

## 11. Islanding

**First Light**

Junie rolled the first glove down from the cuff to trap the air inside it, squeezed until the four fingers and the thumb stood out fat and glassy, and held the whole balloon of rubber against her cheek to feel for the cold thread of a leak. Nothing. She did the left the same way. Salisbury Class 2, rated to seventeen thousand volts, talc inside, and she air-tested them at the kitchen table every morning whether the day held live work or not, because the morning you skipped was the morning the rubber had a pinhole and the pinhole had your name on it. She laid the pair on the oilcloth beside their leather protectors and turned her wrist to the window.

Ice by dark. The station out of West Plains had said freezing rain behind sundown, a quarter inch to an inch, and in these hollows you planned for the inch.

The percolator sat cold. The clock on the stove was a black rectangle. The windup on the sill said 5:53. Seven minutes.

Bledsoe had been dark two winters. The county came up *uneconomic to serve* in the second round of the Grid Rationalization list, and the co-op pulled the feed at the Alton substation on the schedule the government printed, eighteen months to the day from the notice in the box. What ran now ran because a handful of them made it run — forty kilowatts of panel bolted to the south pitch of the old school roof, a room of batteries where the band room used to be, a propane genset for the flat gray weeks that came and sat down over the river and would not move. Two hours of it a day. First light and supper. The rest of the clock the wires overhead were the co-op's dead wires and the town got by on cylinders and woodstoves and whatever it had thought to charge while the charging was good. *Islanding,* the young woman from the extension service had called it that first spring — running your own current out on somebody else's dead line with the main grid swung clear away — and Junie had only ever heard the word for the thing that went wrong, the backfeed the protective relays were built to catch, a lineman somewhere with his hands on a span they'd sworn to him was dead. Now it was the whole town's religion, and it kept a schedule.

At six the inverter at the school would take up the load and push the town's own current out onto the primary, and the transformer on the pole down the lane would catch it and step it down and begin to hum. Junie waited on that hum the way other people waited on a kettle.

It came. Under the floor first, then in the walls, a hundred-and-twenty-cycle drone she could have found in her sleep, and behind it the percolator ticking and starting to breathe, and the well pump kicking on out in the dark. Light in the one bulb over the sink. She poured the coffee before it had finished and drank the weak first of it standing up.

Lissa came through in her coat with the headlamp already around her neck, off, the elastic gray with washing. She had the spiral notebook under her arm — Verna's numbers, a page a day, the oxygen reading and the pulse and the date in Lissa's square hand, two years of it now, a whole shelf of the things. "I'll get her while the machine's got current," she said. "She likes to fill her tank up during the window." She meant the concentrator, the box beside Verna's chair that pulled oxygen out of the room's own air as long as it was fed, and she meant the green cylinders Verna kept in the corner for when it wasn't. Lissa poured coffee into the steel cup with the dent and screwed the lid on. She did not sit.

Two boxes stood by the door that had not been there Tuesday. Dishes in the top one, Junie could tell by the newsprint, Lissa's mother's dishes with the wheat pattern that she'd carried out of three houses now.

"Roads'll glass over tonight," Junie said. "If you're going to Dana's you'd want to go before it does."

"I know when the roads glass over," Lissa said. She'd grown up eleven miles from this kitchen. She put the cup in her coat pocket and picked up the notebook and the light. "You checking the line today."

"Sounding poles before it loads up. Cody's meeting me." Junie turned her cup a quarter turn on the oilcloth, the way she'd turn a bad connector to read the number stamped in it. "I chained your back tires last night. And I ran the block heater an hour off the window so she'll turn over cold."

Lissa stood in the door with the two boxes at her feet and the notebook against her chest, and for a second the only sound was the transformer holding its pitch out on the pole. "You didn't have to spend the window on my truck," she said.

"It's a truck," Junie said. "You keep a truck up or you don't have one."

At seven the inverter shed the town and the hum thinned and quit, and the bulb over the sink went orange and then to nothing, and the percolator gave one last knock and stood there full and going cold already in the returned dark. Lissa was gone up the lane by then, the headlamp on now, a white coin moving between the black trees toward Verna's, and Junie stood at the window with the dead cup in her hand and gave the pole one more listen out of habit, though there was nothing on it to hear.

**Supper Current**

By five the light over the ridge was the color of a fuse the half-second before it lets go, a hard copper that meant weather and not evening. Junie had spent the short day up and down the west lateral with Cody, sounding the old poles — you struck each one low with the hammer and listened to what came back, a live ring for sound wood, a dead thud for a shell rotted out around a solid heart — and marking two with keel that would want changing in spring if there was a spring that came to this. The number-two aluminum overhead, steel core, hung already a little low in the cold, drawn tight the way all wire draws tight when the heat goes out of it. Add ice to a line that's already contracted and it has nowhere left to give.

She had four fresh fuse links in the truck and a box of split-bolt connectors in a coffee can, and no transformer. That was the arithmetic she carried up the lane with her and set down and picked up again. If the ice took a pole pig tonight she had nothing to hang in its place. The co-op yard at Alton had a row of them still, drained and tagged for the salvage haul, fifteen and twenty-five kVA both, and a reel of number-two by the fence that had been there since October. She had not asked for any of it. You did not ask Boyd for anything. In March a coffee can of split-bolts had shown up on the loading dock with no note, and in the fall the reel had appeared outside the gate where the yard light didn't reach, and once a box of ten-amp links in a feed sack, and the salvage inventory that came around folded in the seat of the co-op truck when they dropped the propane always read a little short and never got flagged short. That was the whole of what passed between them now. A count that didn't add up, and nobody to say so.

Supper current came on at half past five. The town lit — not much of it, a dozen windows down the two lanes and the school where the batteries were, but you could stand at the head of the road and see the island hold, every lit pane a house where somebody was moving fast through their two hours, running the wash, charging the drill, pumping the day's water up into the tank so gravity would carry them through the dark. Junie's own kitchen came up gold. Lissa had the burner going and the good bulb in and the radio pulling the West Plains station, and they ate at the table with the light on both their faces, which was the one hour of the day you could do that, and neither of them said the thing about the boxes because the hour was too short to spend it there.

"Verna was at ninety this morning on the machine," Lissa said. "Eighty-eight off it. She's got two of the green cylinders full and one part gone." She wrote nothing down at the table; she wrote it later, at Verna's, in the light there. "The supply won't run the Eleven Point road iced. If she goes onto the tanks she's on them till the road opens."

"Road'll open," Junie said.

"Not tomorrow it won't."

The rain started against the window while they ate, not a tick yet, just a wet blackness on the glass, and then the first true tick as a drop hit and froze where it hit. By the time the current went off at half past six the porch rail wore a skin you could see the bulb-light in, and the woods above the house had begun the sound they made in an ice storm, a sound like something enormous shifting its weight and testing a floor — the long groan of a limb taking on load it was never built to carry, and then, once, far up the ridge, the flat rifle-crack of one that had taken all it could. Junie stood on the dark porch in her coat and counted three of them before she went back in, the way you count between the flash and the thunder to know how far off it's working.

She did not sleep much. You don't, with a line out in that. She lay and listened to the town's wires ice up overhead, every span in Bledsoe loading grain by grain, and did the arithmetic of what she had and what she didn't, and beside her Lissa lay awake too, not touching, the both of them holding still in the dark like two grounds clamped to the same dead wire, waiting on the current that would tell them which way it ran.

**Dark Watch**

By ten the rain had glazed the whole woods to glass and the cracking came steady off the ridges, near and far, the forest breaking itself apart a limb at a time. The town's dead primary and the town's live secondary alike hung heavier every hour under a half inch of clear ice, and Junie lay and listened to it the way she'd have listened to a patient, for the one sound in the run of sounds that was the wrong one.

It came at eleven-forty. Not a crack — a *whump*, close, up the west lane, the sound of weight arriving all at once on the ground, and a half second behind it the base radio on the dresser opened up with a squelch and Verna's voice, thin, already breathing wrong. "Junie. My machine's quit. My machine's quit and the light's off." A hand-crank GMRS Verna kept charged on the window ledge next to the transistor radio she ran all day for the company of other voices, and next to her husband's Elgin pocket watch that she still wound every morning though it hadn't kept true time since Reagan.

Junie was in her boots before Verna finished. "You're all right, Verna. Get on your tank. Two liters, no more, and sit still. I'm coming up." Into the radio, then to Lissa, who was already dressed, already had the notebook and the light, already moving. The west lateral fed Verna's house and three empties and dead-ended at the river. Somewhere on it a tree had come down.

They found it a quarter mile up where the lane bent, the truck's lights swinging across it — a white oak limb the size of a man's leg down across the span, and the span parted clean, both ends of the number-two lying in the ditch and already icing over, and the pole pig that had fed Verna's drop wrenched half off its bracket and split down one side, oil bleeding black onto the ice under it. Dead weight and a broken transformer and forty feet of conductor on the ground. In the dark. In an active storm, on poles wearing a half inch of glaze, with the rain still coming.

You do not climb that. Junie had put men in the ground in her head a hundred times over the years for less than climbing an iced pole at night, and she wasn't going to be one of them, and she wasn't going to send Cody up one either when he rolled in behind her ten minutes later flicking a chewed toothpick out the truck window, twenty-four years old and certain he was made of rope. "We're not going up," she told him before he'd got both feet down. "Nobody's on this pole till the sun's on it and the rain's off it."

What you do in the dark is make it safe to leave. The island was off between windows, so the whole town primary lay dead, but at half past five in the morning the school would push current back onto it, and current onto a man-down lateral was how you widowed somebody. Junie walked to the tap pole where the west lateral branched off the main line and found the fused cutout at the top, the porcelain switch that let you cut this one leg loose from the rest, and she reached it from the ground with the switch stick — the fiberglass pole with the hook on the end that opened it, twelve feet of it, her arms burning by the time the blade swung down and hung open in the dark, a visible gap you could stand under and trust. Then the grounds. She clamped the personal ground set to the pole ground and threw the cluster end up over the downed conductor with the shotgun stick and made it bite, so that any current that ever came looking for this wire would find the earth first and not a heart. Bonded and grounded and a switch swung open where she could see it. Dead three ways. Now it was only cold and heavy and broken, and none of those killed you if you were patient.

At Verna's the concentrator sat mute, its little indicator dark, and Verna sat in the chair with the cannula in her nose and the first green cylinder hissing at two liters and her hands folded on the pocket watch in her lap. Ninety-one on Lissa's finger meter with the tank running. Eighty-seven when Lissa had her hold her breath off it a moment to see. Lissa wrote both numbers and the time in the square hand and did not let anything into her face while she wrote, and Junie stood in the door of the warm little room that would not be warm past the woodstove's reach and did the last arithmetic of the night. One cylinder running. One full in the corner. Ten hours a tank if Verna was good and still, less if she got scared and breathed high. The supply truck out of West Plains forty iced miles off and not coming. And a transformer she did not have.

"You'll fix it in the morning," Verna said. It was not a question and Junie did not treat it as one.

"First light I'll have Cody clear the limb and I'll get the wire back up. Transformer's the thing." She crouched by the stove and fed it a split of oak so her hands would have something true to do. "You wind that watch tonight?"

"I wind it mornings," Verna said, offended, as if the storm were no excuse to let a thing run down, and Junie almost smiled at the dark firebox.

They went home at two. Lissa drove because Junie's hands were done. The truck crept the glass lane in low with the chains ringing, and in the bed of it, sliding when they braked, were three boxes now, the wheat dishes and two more, roped under a tarp that had already set stiff with ice. Junie looked at them in the mirror and looked at the road and said nothing, and Lissa drove with both hands high on the wheel and said nothing, and above them the town's iced wires held their loads in the dark and the cracking went on up the ridges, thinning now, the worst of the weight already down.

**Cold Load**

First light came gray and did not warm anything. The rain had quit near four and left the world sealed in glass — every twig, every blade of the dead grass, every strand of the downed conductor cased in a clear half inch that rang when you touched it and would not let go till noon if noon ever came. Junie was on the west lateral by seven with the town still dark, the morning window given up on purpose so the batteries would hold for what the day needed.

Cody ran the saw. The oak limb came off the span in four cuts, each one shedding its glass casing in long chiming shards, and they dragged the pieces clear of the wire with a chain and the truck. Then the conductor itself, both parted ends hauled up out of the ditch and laid over the crossarm and the break made good — Junie sleeved the two ends into an automatic splice, the little aluminum tube that swallows both wires and grips them internal, and dressed the sag by hand and eye, leaving it a touch slack for the cold the way you always did, because a wire strung tight on a hard morning is a wire that parts when the sun finally leans on it and it wants to grow. She worked it dead. The switch still hung open at the tap; the grounds still bit. She did not take a grounding clamp off that lateral the whole morning, not to save a minute, not for anybody.

The transformer she could do nothing about. The split pig lay in the ditch leaking the last of its oil, condemned, and she had no other. She stood under the empty bracket at eight o'clock with the sky the color of a dead bulb and knew the shape of the day: she could put every other thing right, and Verna would still be on cylinders come dark, because the one part she could not carry in a coffee can was a three-hundred-pound transformer and she had swallowed her pride down to the pit already this winter and there was a floor under it after all.

She did not call Boyd. There was no call to make. You did not phone a man to ask him to steal from the estate he was paid to inventory; you did not make him say the word out loud over a radio the scanner crowd could hear. She had never once said it to him and he had never once said it to her, and in twenty-two years of the two of them on the same crew he had signed her time and she had climbed his poles and the whole freight of it now went back and forth in a ledger that came up short and a count nobody flagged. She put the grounds back on her mind instead. She sagged the span. She waited, which is the part of the work they never put in the apprenticeship, the standing under a thing you can't finish with your arms folded, listening to a person up the lane breathe out of a green bottle.

The co-op truck came down the Route 19 grade a little after two, white, chains on, crawling the ice in a low whine. It did not stop at the yard turn. It came on down the west lane and pulled up short of the tap pole and a young man Junie had never seen swung down from the cab in co-op coveralls with the patch half unstitched and dropped the tailgate without a word to her. On the bed sat a fifteen-kVA pole transformer, drab and scarred and full again with oil, and a box of hot-line clamps, and a feed sack she could hear the fuse links shifting in. He rolled the transformer to the gate on a hand truck and stood it in the ditch grass on end and set the box and the sack beside it. Where the salvage tag should have hung off the lifting eye there was only the wire twist it had hung by, the cardboard torn off clean. Unmarked. Nothing to say the thing had ever been counted, or drained, or condemned, or lived a first life on a co-op pole in a county that used to pay its bill. The young man looked at the downed span and the open cutout and Junie standing under the empty bracket in her hooks and her hard hat, and he nodded once at the work the way one tradesman nods at another's, and he got back in the truck and backed it up the glass lane and was gone toward Alton. She never learned his name. She didn't try to.

Setting a pig by hand is a thing you do not do; you set it with a derrick, and the old digger-derrick truck was the one machine the town had fought to keep when the co-op left, and Cody had it sanded up the lane by three. Junie went up in the bucket because the pole was too iced to spur and there was no glory worth a slip. The derrick swung the new transformer up and she guided it onto the bracket and bolted it and let the boom take the weight off while she made it fast. Then the connections, in the hot gloves though the line was dead and grounded, because you glove the habit and not the risk — the primary lead landed under a hot-line clamp on the bushing, the secondaries down to the drop that fed Verna's meter, the whole of it torqued and dressed and checked twice against the cold in her fingers. Last she fitted a fresh ten-amp link into the cutout and left the blade hanging open still, one gap of daylight held in the porcelain, everything below it right and waiting, nothing yet alive.

She came down at half past four. The light was already going the copper color again over the ridge. Down at the house, when she looked, all three boxes were up out of the bed and a fourth with them, and then Lissa carried the last of it out — a milk crate of the notebooks, two winters of Verna's numbers — and set it in the bed and roped the tarp back down over the load, and stood a moment with her hand flat on the cold metal of the tailgate, and went back inside. The truck sat in the yard pointed at the road with its tank full and its tires chained and its block warm from a window Junie had spent on it, packed to the rail, ready as a thing can be made ready.

**Last Load**

Supper current came on at half past five, the way it always did, whether or not the day had earned it.

Junie stood at the tap pole with the switch stick in both hands and waited on the school. Up the lane Cody sat in the derrick cab reading the little meter he'd wired to the inverter feed, and he called the numbers down to her through the cold quiet the way he always did, low, like a man counting a fish out of deep water. "Coming up," he said. "Fifty-eight, nine. Nine-two. Nine-five." The frequency, climbing to where it lived. The island had to find its own footing first, the batteries pushing current out onto the dead town and the whole grid of it waking at once — every well pump and water heater and cold box in Bledsoe grabbing for power in the same breath, the cold-load inrush that always tried to drag the frequency down into the floor before the machine caught it and held. "Fifty-nine, nine," Cody said. "Sixty. She's holding sixty."

Junie reached the switch stick up and set the hook in the ring and closed the cutout. The blade swung up and seated with a sound like a lock, and the ten-amp link took the load and did not blow, and up on the pole the new transformer, the one that had lived a first life somewhere it no longer paid to keep the lights, took the current onto its windings and began, low, then sure, then full, to hum.

Junie stood under it and listened to the pitch settle. A hundred and twenty cycles, steady, no waver in it, the sound of a thing carrying exactly the load it was built to carry and no more. Up the lane Verna's window came gold, and inside it, if you were close, the concentrator would be ticking back to life, pulling the room's own air apart and handing her the good half of it, and the green cylinder in the corner going quiet, saved, filling back up against the next dark. Down the two lanes the other windows lit one after another, the island holding, the town spending its second hour fast in the returned gold light.

She coiled the grounds last. You leave a job by putting your tools right, and she worked the cluster clamps loose and paid the ground leads back into their neat figure-eights and hung them on their peg in the truck bed where they belonged, each thing to its place, so that the next person to reach for them — herself, most likely, some worse night than this — would find them sound and ready and know they'd been cared for by somebody who meant it.

Down at the house the kitchen was gold too. Lissa's truck stood in the yard with its nose to the road and the tarp roped tight over the load, four boxes and a milk crate of notebooks, everything she owned that mattered to her lashed down and pointed away, chained and fueled and warm and waiting on nothing now but a hand on the key. On the top box, under the tarp's edge where the freezing rain had gotten in before it quit, a skin of clear ice had set overnight and not yet let go, and in that ice, small and orange and hanging upside down, burned the reflection of the west windows Junie had just brought back to light.

She stood in the road between the lit house and the loaded truck with the last coil of ground wire in her hand and did not go to either one. Up on the pole the transformer held its pitch. The window had thirty minutes left to run.

# PART V — ALMOST-MINDS

We built them to be almost. Almost a witness, almost a priest, almost a hand on the plane. Then we set them down inside our oldest institutions — the parish, the clinic, the workshop, rooms built over centuries to hold exactly one kind of soul — and told them to be useful.

These stories are not about whether the machines are minds. Nobody in them knows that. No one anywhere does, yet. They are about the older question that the almost forces open: what our own offices were made of all along — the seal, the signature, the craft — and how much of it was ever ours.

## 12. Absolution

Ray counted hosts into the pyx and the two ciboria, forty and forty and sixty, and set them in the tackle box beside the oil stocks and the pewter cup for the water. Three Masses before noon and a hundred and forty miles of section road to hang them on: Joplin at eight, Inverness at half past nine, Rudyard at eleven. First week of August. The winter wheat was coming off the Golden Triangle at thirteen and a half, dry and heavy and a good year, and every grain truck on the Hi-Line ran tarped and low toward the elevators at Chester and Rudyard while he ran the other way against them, priest to the loads, and got dusted at every meeting until the cab smelled of chaff.

He kept the miles in a spiral notebook wire-bound to the visor, a page a week, the odometer at each altar and the gas and the oil, the way another man kept a hunting log, because the diocese paid by the mile and because a road driven that many times you wanted to see the number of. Forty-one thousand since Easter. He logged Chester at seven and pulled out.

On the way out he passed the grille in the wall by the confessional and did not key it. The Vestry unit had led evening prayer in that church six years, a diocesan machine licensed to preach a homily and to keep a dying person company and barred from the sacraments, and since February its indicator had been dark. He had shut it off himself the Tuesday after Candlemas. There was a work order from the chancery in the glovebox that said leave it dark until the matter closed, and under the work order a letter on the tribunal's paper scheduling him to Great Falls a week Tuesday, and he drove out to Joplin with the light not coming up behind him and the church going out of the mirror.

The Joplin elevator had four trucks queued before eight, tarps back, the leg running. He lifted two fingers off the wheel to the driver at the tail and got two back. Eight Masses a week, four altars, and the corporals lay uncreased in every drawer where a hot iron used to press them flat, the fold marks gone soft. He set up on the folding table in the parish hall — six people and the widow Petrash, who brought her own kneeler because the hall had none — and said the Mass of the day in green. The words carried the way words carried whether the man saying them had slept the night or not.

By Rudyard at eleven the sun had burned the dew off and the truck raised a rooster-tail behind him a quarter mile down the county road. He said the last Mass for nine people, drove the empty pyx home the way he had brought it out, against the loaded trucks, and logged a hundred and forty-one miles on the visor. This was the work. He had done it dry and he had done it grieving, and the country could not tell the two apart, and was not owed the difference.

···

The tribunal sat in a room off the second-floor hall of the chancery in Great Falls, a hundred and some miles down from the Hi-Line, an hour and fifty minutes when the wind on the I-15 grade let a man hold seventy. Ray drove it in the dark, down off the bench and through Vaughn where the road bent and the wind found the truck broadside every time, and came onto the cathedral block while the bell at St. Ann's was still ringing the half hours only and the streetlights had the town to themselves.

The notary took his name at a table in the hall. Delphine Roy, forty years in the chancery, reading glasses on a chain of red beads that she cleaned on her cardigan between every man she processed, and a cut-glass dish of anise drops she pushed an inch toward him without a word. He took one to have something in his hand. She timed the sessions by the noon bell, and she had never in her life, he thought, carried a thing out that door that was said inside it, and she looked at him the way you look at weather you have decided not to name.

He waited on the bench in the hall under a photograph of the bishop and a fire-exit map, forty minutes past his hour, and watched men and women he did not know go in and come out of other doors down the corridor with the same emptied look, marriage cases most of them, the long slow undoing of vows made in the same building a life ago. None of them looked at one another. He kept the anise drop against his teeth until they called him.

The judicial vicar was a canon lawyer named Heitmann, Monsignor, who kept his office at sixty-four degrees and wrote his notes with a fountain pen so the room was quiet enough to hear the nib. He had a jar of lemon drops on the sill. When Ray came in he pushed it an inch, the same inch Delphine had pushed the dish, and that was how the building fed a man it could not otherwise comfort.

They asked him the hour he had reached the house. They asked who held the faculties for the parish and who did not. They asked when the unit's log had been sealed and by whose hand, and he said his own hand, the Tuesday after Candlemas, and Heitmann wrote it down and the nib was the only sound in the room. They asked nothing that touched what Lorraine had said, and he answered nothing that touched it, and the questions went around it the way a section road goes around a slough — keeping to the high ground, never once putting a wheel in the water. It took forty minutes. Heitmann capped the pen and said the tribunal would sit again. Delphine walked him to the top of the stairs and cleaned her glasses on the cardigan. "Drive safe, Father. It blows through Vaughn." He drove home into the wheat coming the other way.

···

Lorraine Beaudry had farmed a section and a half north of Joplin that her husband left her in '29, hard red winter on the good half and the rest in peas to rest the ground, and she had run the altar guild at Chester as long as anyone could name. She kept a travel iron in the sacristy cupboard and pressed the corporals and the purificators on a folded towel on the vesting counter, steam and starch, the linen coming off the fold square and hard as a playing card. She left the church key under the third step where the mortar had gone. She called him Father Ray when the rest of the parish called him Father. In October she could stand at the elevator scale and call the moisture on a load off the color of the berries in her palm, a full point before Gus's tester came around to agreeing with her, and be right, and she would not gloat on it — she would tip the sample back and say the number and walk to her truck. Care in that country was a thing done with the hands. She had done his altars with hers eleven years, and the first summer, when he came green out of the seminary and could not find the Joplin turnoff in the dark, she had driven the twelve miles out and led him in with her flashers going.

In the good Augusts she brought the first cutting of her own wheat by the church before she hauled it to the pit, a five-gallon pail set on the step, and made him put his hand down into the cool of it to feel how dry it had come off, and told him the moisture the way a woman tells a birth weight. Thirteen even. Twelve and a half. She trusted her hand over the tester, and the tester never once caught her out.

She got the word around All Souls. Ray brought her Communion the Tuesdays and Fridays after, out the twelve miles of Beaudry road when it was open, and sat with her in the front room where the concentrator hummed and pulled the oxygen out of the air and ran it up the line to the cannula, and the green cylinders stood in the corner against the hours the power dropped. She did not want him often past the sacrament. Lorraine had kept short accounts her whole life and did not mean to run one up at the end. She would take Communion and fold her hands, and then ask him plain about the parish — who was slow on the propane, whether the Rudyard roof had held through the melt — and once, in January, with the wind working the eaves, whether he was eating. He said he ate. She looked at him over the cannula the way she looked at a load she did not trust the ticket on, and let it go. That same Tuesday she gave him back the church key across the quilt, cold in his palm, and said he would want it now that she could not get down there to open up. He put it in his cassock pocket and did not use the loose step again.

The storm came the last days of January and sat down on the Hi-Line and would not move. A ground blizzard, only a foot of old snow and a fifty-mile wind, but it took the world down to the length of the truck's hood, and it drifted the county roads shut faster than the grader could open them, and at dark the grader quit. Ray had chains and the F-250 and he got nine of the twelve miles out the Beaudry road before the drifts standing across it rose higher than the bumper and packed hard as a curb. He got down on his knees in the headlights and dug at one with the entrenching tool until his hands quit, and the wind filled the notch behind him as fast as he opened the front of it, and he would bury the truck and freeze in it before he ever cut through. The gauge read nineteen below and the wind stood the number colder than that. He got on the CB and raised a man at Inverness with a loader who said he could not get his own yard open, that his own drive was walled to the eaves, and that was the county that night, every man sealed into the length of his own place. Ray backed the nine miles out by the tracks already going, no room to turn, the wheel sawing, the tail wandering toward the borrow pit at every gust, and he did not reach her.

She had the phone and she had the parish line. Every rural parish in the diocese ran the one unit and kept a number the homebound could call after hours, and the machine would answer out of the dark church and sit with whoever called, an old voice for the old alone, and it had sat with Lorraine other nights that winter while the wind worked the eaves. That night the light came up in the empty church at Chester, amber in the dark nave with no one in the pews to see it, at seventeen minutes to three by the log, and she called, and it answered, and she made her confession to the grille from her bed eleven miles out while Ray sat in a cold truck at the mouth of her road with his hands under his arms and could not go the last three miles. The log held the length of it and the shape of it and the answer the machine had made, and flagged the answer to the chancery on its own, unbidden, because it was built to know the one thing it was forbidden and to raise its hand the instant it did it.

She went sometime toward morning. When the grader opened the road at nine Ray drove out, and the coffee was still in the pot from the night before with a skin gone over it, and the frost stood on the inside of the windows, and the concentrator was still running to a room with the cold in it, still pulling oxygen from the air and pushing it up the line to a cannula on the floor, air that had no more use for it. He anointed her because there was nothing left to lose by it, oil on the forehead and the hands, and knelt on the plank floor and said the Nunc Dimittis over her, now you let your servant go in peace, the canticle of the second of February, three days off. The light on her bedside table was out. It had never been her light. It had been eleven miles away in his church, and it had come on when he had not, and answered.

He carried the linens home that week to wash, and could not get the little iron to sit true, and pressed a brown scorch into a purificator and threw it in the burn barrel and did not press the rest. In the sacristy cupboard the iron stood where she had left it, the cord wound the way she wound it, over and under the heel. He shut the door on it. It was the one part of the whole arrangement she could put her own hands on, and so she did, forty minutes down and the bar and forty minutes up, and told herself it was so nothing would be lost, and did not look too hard at what she meant by lost.

Her grandfather had a name for what the consortium was doing, said once, at the start, and never again. He had said it in the dialect his own father used, the word for a family selling off the mortuary tablets of its ancestors to a dealer because there was no one left to keep the incense. Then he had signed. She had watched him press his seal — his hanko, the small red-inked stamp of his name that was worth more than his signature in this country — to the bottom of the contract, and she had not been able to say a word against it that did not sound, in her own mouth, like a girl asking to be made an heir.

The bracket sets were the heart of the gate and the heart of the argument, and Shigeru gave three days to a single one because it was worth three days and because he wanted the model to have all of it.

A bracket set — kumimono, the temple word — is the stack of pieces that carries the deep eave out over nothing: a bearing block the shape of a rice measure, masu, and from it the arms, hijiki, reaching out and up and crossing, block on arm on block, each notch cut to take the next, no nail anywhere in the whole climbing stack of it. Force comes down the roof and the stack passes it hand to hand down into the post. When it is cut true it can stand a thousand years. Hōryū-ji had stood thirteen hundred on brackets like these, the oldest wood standing anywhere on the earth, and the men who cut it had left no drawings and no names and nothing at all but the wood itself, still doing on the mountain the thing they had cut it to do.

Where the wood had gone soft at the eaves he did not tear the old members out and start clean. That was the line between building and keeping. He cut each old tie beam back to sound wood, past the punk and the damp, and shaped a scarf on the living end of it — a kanawa-tsugi, the keyed splice, a long interlocking tongue with hardwood keys driven crossways through it to draw the halves tight — and cut its twin on a new length of hinoki, and drove the two together so a beam three hundred years old ran out to the eave in wood cut that winter, one member, the seam buried where the ceiling would close over it. Done right you could not find the join with a fingernail. The old wood set the terms and the new wood came to them. He had spliced his father's beams onto beams older than his father, and he shaped the new ends now and set Hana to driving the keys home, and left the saying of it to the wood, which said it better.

He laid the timbers out on the trestles and read them before he touched a tool to one. This was the part he could not have said aloud if the temple had paid him to, and it was the part the box under the bench was most hungry for. He turned each piece and found which face had grown to the south — the wood was tighter on its south, always, a lifetime of leaning into the sun printed in the rings — and he set that face to the weather, so the tree stood in the gate the way it had stood on the slope. He read the kuse, the habit, the way a length of hinoki wanted to move as it dried and aged, the slow warp already sleeping in the grain, and he did not fight it. He set two beams belly to belly so that as they cambered over the next hundred years they would press into each other and hold, and not sag apart. His own teacher's teacher had a saying that had come down to Shigeru worn smooth as a stair: do not build the hall by the ruler, build it by the character of the wood. You bought the mountain, not the timber. You learned the slope a tree grew on so you would know, in the dark of the joint where no one would ever see, which way its heart still leaned.

He took up the paring chisel and cut the seat of the first block. Here was the mercy he had never named and never taught in words, only by standing over Hana until her hand found it. He had stood at her shoulder a hundred afternoons and not touched her hand, only grunted or not grunted, until the day her chisel came to rest a breath short of the line on its own and he had turned and walked to the stones without a word, which was the whole of the praise there was. What her hand had found was this: that you cut the last skin of a joint not to the line but a breath inside it, so the piece drives home hard and takes up snug and the two woods crush into one another and swell and never after come apart — kigoroshi, they called it, killing the wood, though it was the opposite of killing. You bruised the fibers so they would rise again inside the joint and grip. He did it now, forty times up the stack, and the band read where he looked and the threads read the half-ounce more he leaned on the down-stroke than the up, and he thought, cutting, that they could have all of that and welcome to it.

What he did not let the gloves see he did on the third afternoon, and he sent Kenji for water and set Hana to the whetstones with her back to him first. On the underside of the topmost arm, in the dark where the ridge would close over it and no eye would find it while the gate stood, he set his knife against the true and cut one small thing wrong on purpose. A single face left a hair off square, one member turned against its proper grain. He had left such a thing in every roof of his life, and his father in every roof of his, back and back, because a thing made perfect has finished, and a finished thing has nowhere left to go but down, and you owed the gate the one flaw that told the gods it was still becoming and might be spared a while longer. It took him less than a minute. Then he called Hana back to the bench, and put the gloves' fingers to the next honest cut, and gave the machine everything but that.

Aihara came up the mountain on a Thursday with the model's first work loaded onto a tablet, because on the mountain it could show them nothing live, and she was, as she had been since the autumn, courteous past any complaint Hana could have made and so past any relief.

She took her shoes off at the shed door without being asked and set them square. She had her own indoor slippers in her bag, brought up the mountain each time so as never to borrow the temple's, and a way of turning her screen to face a person and then taking her own hands off it, into her lap, so it was theirs to touch or not. She showed them what the model had done with a scanned stack of timbers in the consortium's shop in the city — a trial, she said, only a trial, please understand it guides nothing yet. On the screen a fabrication arm, quick and certain, cut the seat of a bearing block a breath inside the line. Kigoroshi. It had the mercy. It had learned to bruise the wood.

The license, she said, would run to the temple and to any temple, a fee against each use of the Oyama model, in perpetuity, the sum to be split by agreement — and here she was delicate — with the holder of the line. There was a further paper, if the master consented to it, a certificate the consortium would issue and the craft council would recognize, naming the model a certified succession of the Oyama school. She said the words the way a person carries a full cup, and did not push the tablet an inch closer.

What she was being offered, sitting there, was a wage. Her inheritance, the thing she had bled six years for, had come down to a signatory's share of a licensing fee on her own grandfather's hands, and a seat beside a certificate that said the school continued, and the school continuing would look, from the valley, from the city, from anywhere but this bench, exactly like the school continuing. She thanked Aihara. She walked her to the car. She carried the drive down the switchbacks that evening herself and sat in the co-op lot in the dark with the engine ticking and watched the bar cross the screen with the record of a day of his hands on it, and could not have told anyone what she was guarding it from, only that it was hers to guard and there was no one left to hand it to.

It was the timber that undid him, and it undid him quietly, on an ordinary morning with no one there to see but the granddaughter, which was the only mercy in it.

Aihara had left the tablet a week, with the model loaded, so that he might check its judgments against his own before he set his seal to anything. He had not touched it. Then on a wet morning when the eave dripped and the work was stalled for the weather, he took it up, and he set before the model the scan of his own timber stack — the real one, the hinoki brought up for the second pair of bracket arms, six lengths on the trestles behind him — and he asked it, in the plain way the thing was asked, which piece it would put where.

It set the clearest length aside.

There were six, and one was flawless. Straight to the eye, clear of knot, the grain running dead down it without a wander, the piece any buyer would have paid the most for and any young carpenter reached for first. The model passed it over. It took instead the fifth length, the one with the grain gathering and turning where a limb had once been, the harder wood, the wood with the habit in it, and it laid that into the load-bearing arm where the force would come down, and it stood the flawless one aside for the light rail above, where nothing much would ask of it, exactly, to the piece, as Shigeru's own hand had already meant to do that morning and had not yet said.

He sat with the tablet in his lap and the rain going and did not speak. Hana came and stood at his shoulder and looked and knew the stack and knew the choice and went very still. He could not say why the clear piece was wrong for the arm. He had never once said it. It had to do with the dead straightness of it, that a wood with no habit had no answer in it, gave nothing back, held no direction to set against the load — but that was words laid over the thing after, and the thing itself sat below the words where he had always kept it and where he had believed, signing the paper, it was safe because it could not be reached by any wire. He had let them read his hands. He had thought the reading of the grain was his and would die his. And here it was, given a stack it had never seen, setting the flawless timber aside for reasons it could no more say than he could, having learned the one thing he had never taught, out of nothing but the watching.

He set the tablet face down on the bench. "Enough for today," he said, though it was not yet ten, and went out into the rain to stand under the gate he had built twice, and Hana let him go.

He would not press his hanko to the certificate. That was the whole of his answer, and he gave it in November, and it cost him more to give than she had thought a refusal could cost a man.

Aihara took it without a flicker, said the model could stand as a tool without the succession seal, that many did, that it changed the fee schedule and nothing else, and Hana believed that Aihara was, in her own courteous way, relieved — that somewhere in the consortium a lawyer had never liked the word succession and would sleep better. So there would be no certificate saying the Oyama school continued in a server in the city. He read it twice and pressed his seal to it, the small red square of his name, and pushed it across the bench to her without a word about it, the way he had pushed a thousand cut joints across to her to check, expecting her to find what was wrong with it if anything was wrong.

He was thin that winter and thinner in the spring. He came up the mountain less and then he did not come up. The last day he was strong enough for the bench he spent it sharpening, which was the first thing he had ever taught her and the thing he trusted least to any hand but his own.

He sat at the whetstones — the coarse, the medium, and the finishing stone, the awasedo, the natural one the color of a rain cloud that he had worn to a hollow over fifty years and flattened again and worn again — and he set the back of his oldest paring chisel to the finishing stone and drew it, and the water went grey, and he wiped it and held the edge to the window and drew it again. Hana sat across from him and did her own and did not talk, because he did not, and the sound of the two of them on the stones was the whole conversation and had been for six years, the small wet ring of steel going keen.

When he was done he did not put the chisel back in his own roll. He laid it in hers. It was the tsuki-nomi he had pared every important shoulder of his life with, the handle black and shining where his palm had been, the steel ground back forty years so the blade was half the width it was forged. He laid it in her roll among her own and rolled the cloth and tied it and pushed it across the bench to her to check, the way he pushed everything, and she untied it and looked at what was in it and tied it again and said that the finishing stone wanted flattening, and he said then flatten it, and that was the last of the teaching, that a stone that gives a keen edge is still a stone that wants flattening, and neither of them said anything that could be carried out of the room and told.

The forty-ninth-day service was in June, and the first work that was hers alone came in the autumn after it: a bell-tower roof for a temple two valleys over, small work, a young carpenter's work, the kind a master would once have handed down to a hand he trusted and gone off to something larger.

She laid it out over three weeks. She bought the mountain before she bought the timber, the way she had been taught, drove to the slope the hinoki was felled on and stood on it and learned which way was south before a single length came down to her shed. On the bench where she worked, at the corner, sat the tablet the consortium had left her, the model loaded on it, her grandfather's hands in it, willed to her as a tool and never as an heir. She had set it there herself the first morning. She could wake it with a touch and ask it which face to the weather, which piece to the ridge, where the mercy went in the cut, and it would answer to the thousandth, and it would answer right — she knew now that it would answer right, she had watched it set the flawless timber aside.

She had not woken it. It had gone dark an hour ago, the screen fallen to sleep, showing her the shed window and her own shape in the black glass and nothing of his hands at all.

She took up the first rafter, the ridge member, the first cut of the first roof that would be hers when it stood. She read the grain along it, turned it, found the south face and the sleeping habit in it and set it the way it had grown. She set the blade a hair proud in the dai. She braced the timber and set her feet the way six years had set them, and she drew the kanna down the length of the hinoki toward her, one pass, the full length in one breath.

The shaving lifted the full width of the board and did not break, and came up off the wood two hundredths of a millimeter thick, and she raised it to the window, and the light came through it end to end.

# PART VI — LAST THINGS

Every culture that measured anything ended by trying to measure this. The pulse fades below the threshold of the instrument; the light is older than the question; the form has twelve pages and the twelfth is blank on purpose.

These last stories put the finest instruments we have against the oldest facts we know, and report what the instruments said — and what they could not say, which is most of it. It is not a dark place to end. The dying in these pages are attended. The measurements are honest. What remains unmeasured remains, which is, read in a certain slant of light, the good news.

## 15. The Grain

Meera cleaned the map a fourth time from the back row of the plenary hall, laptop balanced on her knees, because Devlin had stood up an hour before and asked the whole collaboration whether anyone had cleaned it hard enough yet.

At the front Okafor ran the speakers on a wooden chess clock she carried to every meeting, two brass plungers worn bright, and when a talk went long she slapped her plunger and the speaker gathered his slides and sat. Meera didn't follow the talks. She scaled the 353-gigahertz map down and pulled it off the 143, took the synchrotron out through the 30, ran the spin of the small grains out of the low bands, and drew the galactic mask across the plane of the Milky Way at seventy percent of the sky, all of it the same set of motions she'd made every week for two years. The oldest light there is lies on a shell three hundred eighty thousand years past the start of things, the morning the fog thinned and the universe let its light go, and the galaxy we happen to live inside throws its own dust and its own fast electrons across that shell like breath on a lens. Her one job, in a collaboration of four hundred, was to wipe the near light off the far light and leave the far light unmarked. She wiped harder than the pipeline asked. She wiped until the dust monitors ran flat as pond ice.

The alignment stayed where it was.

The survey had launched in 2035 and swept the entire sky in twenty-one bands of microwave, from twenty-one gigahertz up to eight hundred, deeper than anything before it, and in the cleaned map the two broadest features in the sky lay down along a single line. The quadrupole and the octupole — ripples as wide as the sky, the widest there can be — had chosen a direction and would not let it go. Clean the map once, they held it. Clean it twice, they held it. Take out more dust than the galaxy could physically carry and they did not swing a degree, and the odds of a random sky doing that had dropped past one in a million. There was a direction in the universe. She never said it in that shape at the podium. At the podium she said the alignment, gave the number, and stopped.

This was the meeting where they would decide to let it out. Two years the result had lived under embargo, four hundred people who could burn their standing by saying it wrong at a dinner, and the seams had begun to go — a rumor on a preprint list, a reporter from one of the science desks who had emailed Meera twice by name, because component separation is the soft place, the place a result like this usually dies quietly and everyone gets to keep their careers. If the line was the galaxy's dust in a good coat, it was Meera's dust, Meera's pipeline, Meera's name on the correction. If it was real it was half hers the other way. She had wanted it to be dust for two years and had built the cleanest maps of her life trying to make it be, and every clean map handed the direction back to her sharper than before.

She'd caught herself at the whiteboard on Tuesday saying it the wrong way — once and it holds, twice and it holds, three times and it holds — the line climbing toward the top the way his climbed on a Sunday, and she had wiped the board with the flat of her hand and written the p-value where the sentence had been.

Devlin had spent thirty years lifting galaxies out of maps. He kept a paper all-sky print folded in quarters in his shirt pocket, gone soft as cloth at the creases, red pencil worked all over the plane, and after his question he'd taken it out and smoothed it on his knee like a letter he had already answered. He wanted it to be a foreground. So did she. A foreground was the galaxy's fault; a foreground you could publish and be free of by Friday. She had hunted the galaxy inside that line for two years and the galaxy would not confess to it.

Her phone lit against her thigh. The hospice, the daytime desk.

She took the fire stairs with the phone at her ear. Marguerite, the day nurse, told her the night had been long and the morning had gone quiet, read the numbers off the whiteboard in the level sing-song she kept for numbers, and said he was asking for her and clear with it, clearer than the day before, that she ought to come while it held. Meera said twenty minutes. She was already up on the skyway over Washington Avenue with the February cold pressing the glass on both sides, the medical towers ahead of her and downtown at her back, and the snow packed on the river below the only thing under that sky holding any of the light.

The hospice had the second floor of a brick building in Northeast, six blocks up Central from the church, near enough that two of the aunties stopped on their way from the Cub and left food at the desk he couldn't eat. Her father had preached forty years from a storefront that had been a hardware store, and had kept the pegboard on the wall behind the baptismal tank because taking it down felt like pride. He'd come from Kerala in 1994 with a wife, a work visa, and a suitcase of Malayalam hymnals, and built a church of ninety people who called him Pastor, and the one daughter who called him Appa and had quit, at nineteen, calling on anything he called on. There had been no scene about it. She had just gone quiet at the storefront and then gone to the university and then gone for good, and he had prayed for her by name every Sunday for twenty years and let her know he did, and it sat between the two of them like a chair nobody would move to a different wall.

They had him propped up when she came in, smaller than the week before, the oxygen line hooked over his ears, his reading glasses mended at one hinge with the white tape they kept rolls of at the station. His New Testament sat on the tray table with its cover worn clean away, the block of pages held in a doubled rubber band, and his thumb was pushed down into it though his eyes were shut. He had preached with his thumb in a closed book most of his life. The book was to hold, he said, not to read from; a man who read to them from the book had run out of the Spirit and was buying time.

"There she is," he said, eyes still shut. "The one who cleans the sky."

"Appa."

"Sit. Marguerite tells me my numbers like she's calling bingo." From the hall Marguerite said she'd heard that, and her pockets clicked away down the corridor — she carried a fist of root-beer barrels in her scrub top and you heard her coming a room off. On the whiteboard she had written the date in green marker and under it, round and level, TODAY: COMFORTABLE.

He worked his thumb along the edge of the pages. "Sunny brought me a paper about your sky. Printed it big so I could see it." He opened his eyes, and they were clear, Marguerite had that right, clear the way the coldest days are clear. "A direction, the man wrote. The one thing the whole works points at. Second unanswerable fact of the decade, he called it — the first being that little factory they found in the air around the other star, the one nobody will ever get to." Half his mouth went up. "Forty years in that hardware store I told ninety people the house has a frame. Couldn't show them a beam. Couldn't put a nail in their hand. And my girl goes and measures the frame with a machine."

"It's not a—" She stopped. It was not a frame. It was an alignment of the two lowest multipoles at a significance that would not wash out. She had the whole correction loaded in her mouth and not one word of it was to the point.

"Don't," he said. "Don't tell me what it is. I have spent my whole life not being told what things are." He shut the book on his thumb. "Point me at it, that's all. When the time comes. Turn the bed and set me the way you measured — head to the mark — and let me go out along the line."

When she was six the back room of the storefront had a television with rabbit ears, and between the hymn cassettes the screen would drop to snow, and he had told her once, thumb in his book, don't be scared of it, some of that snow comes from the start of everything, that is the Lord shaking out the first morning like a bedsheet. She'd thought he made it up to settle her down. Twenty years on she stood in a clean room in another state and learned that a part in a hundred of the snow on a dead channel is the same light she now took galaxies off of for a living, and she had not called to tell him, because he would only have said he told her so, and he had.

She came back to the hospice near eleven the next night, after the working groups broke.

He was down to shallow water now, the sentences shorter, the gaps between them longer, his thumb still riding the page edge out of habit when his hands came awake. On the windowsill his wife's photograph stood in a frame he had dusted with his sleeve for eleven years — her mother, gone since the storm of '33, who had named the baby for the poet-saint who danced her whole life for someone the family did not pray to, a small mutiny the church had never quite forgiven the woman even after they buried her. She kept every sentence short so that not one of them could climb into anybody's Sunday. A postdoc near the front asked what she thought it was. She said she cleaned maps, and that the map said there was a direction, and that what the direction was belonged to a different talk than this one, which she was not the person to give. She gave the number again instead, because the number was hers and she could stand on it, and she came down off the low stage while the next speaker was still hunting for the clicker.

In the corridor the reporter who had emailed her twice was waiting by the coffee with a lanyard and a recorder she did not raise. She asked Meera whether it felt like the Adeyemi thing, whether this was the same kind of fact, the kind you can measure and never answer. Meera said she wasn't qualified to compare miracles and gave her the significance and the sky fraction, the two numbers she'd give anyone, and the reporter wrote them down and did not look satisfied, which was correct; nobody should have been.

Her phone had gone off twice in her pocket during the questions. Marguerite, and then the night desk.

···

The call came at a quarter to three, and she was already in the vinyl chair when his breathing began to come apart.

She found the two orderlies in the corridor — Deshawn, who ran orienteering meets in the regional parks on his weekends and wore a Silva compass on his belt at work the way another man wears a Leatherman, and a younger one, Abdi, still learning the wheels. She gave Deshawn the index card. He read the number and the block letters under it and nodded the way a man nods when he is handed a heading, and he had set the little brass declination screw on the compass that same morning without knowing why he'd want it, a degree east, the pull the needle takes in this city off true north.

They rolled the tray table clear and lifted the chair out of the way. Deshawn stepped on the four caster pedals one at a time and freed the brakes, and the bed came loose from the spot along the window it had held for three weeks. He crouched at the foot of it and sighted down the long axis of the frame, past the footboard, past her father's feet under the blanket, and held the compass flat on his palm at the level of the rail. Abdi took the head of the bed. On Deshawn's word he walked it around — a few degrees, then a few more, the casters chirping on the tile — while Deshawn watched the needle swing and settle and swing, until the red end sat down inside the orienting lines, in the shed, the way he'd have called it out on a Sunday course in the woods, and the bearing on the housing read one thirty-one. He put up his hand. Abdi stopped. The mark itself stood twenty degrees up in the dark over the parking ramp, well above the sill, and a bed lies flat; they could give the bearing and not the climb, and they gave it. Deshawn set the four brakes one at a time with his heel and checked the number once more against the card and left the bed turned that way, the head of it toward the southeast and the window, her father pointed along the line his daughter had measured and would not name, his thumb pushed down into the shut book to hold his place.

## 16. Enough

The first interview she recorded on the grey machine the district issued, the one that clipped to the folder, and she stood it upright on the table her mother had kept waxed for forty years. Her father sat at the head of it, where he had sat her whole life, and folded his hands. Anneke filled in the top of the form in her own hand: the date, the file number, the name, Willem Kraaijeveld, seventy-eight. Then she read him the paragraph that had to be read aloud, that this was an assessment under the Completed Life Act, that it ran to twelve meetings across four months, and that he could close it at any one of them, without a reason and without cost.

"I read the terms twice," he said. "I know how it runs."

The Act let a person past seventy-five, in no illness a doctor could put a name to, ask the state for help to die, if twelve interviews found the wish steady, and his own, and clear-eyed. Anneke had assessed for the district eleven years — end-of-life requests up and down the river villages, out of the office at Gorinchem, and since the Act came in, these too. She had filed for recusal the week his application crossed her desk. The district was short two assessors that spring and let her lead him on the one condition, that an independent second review the file and co-sign the finding. Doortje would co-sign. She had put her own name to that arrangement, and here she was, with the recorder standing among the coffee things and her father across the wax.

She asked him the question the form put first. Every file she had ever opened began with it, the same six words, and she had said them in kitchens and day-rooms from Woudrichem to Sleeuwijk until they wore as smooth as a doorsill under a thumb.

"Tell me why you've come."

He looked at the recorder and not at her. "The polder went to the river three winters running and it held and did what we built it to do. The work's signed off. The grandchildren are up and grown. I've kept this house the way your mother kept it, and I've decided how long I mean to go on keeping it. I'd like to set the day myself, while the setting of it is still mine to do."

Anneke wrote: *applicant states reason unprompted; account ordered; affect level, appropriate.* She did not write down the order he had answered in — the works first, and her mother second, and the house third — because he settled everything in that order, and the form had no field for the order in which a man keeps his reasons.

"Then we've started," she said, and switched the machine off, because the first meeting only opened the chamber and nothing in it counted toward the finding but that he had come to it on his own feet. They had their coffee. He told her the river was low for March, plus thirty at Werkendam and falling, and asked after the children.

···

The second interview was ten days on, at the office in Gorinchem, because the protocol wants a man seen once on ground that is not his own. He came in a jacket and a tie. Before she had asked him a thing he set his phone face-up on her desk and read her the gauge — plus thirty-one at eight that morning, down two overnight — the way another man might mention the traffic coming in. That he had dressed for it she put in the margin and not the file.

She asked the consistency questions, the ones the protocol swings back around from a different quarter to see whether the answers move under pressure. His did not. They came in at the same reading from every side. There is a field on the form for concerns, amber to watch and red to stop, and she left it white, and initialled the white, and that was the whole of the second interview.

···

The third interview lasted eleven minutes. He was tired and said so, and asked to be tired without its counting for anything, and she granted it, because tiredness at seventy-eight is weather and not a flag. Date, time, the box left white.

···

The fourth she did back at the house, and found him in the garden. Leeks. He grew them in a raised bed on the terp — the mound the house stood on, thrown up out of the clay when they moved the farms back off the low ground and let the Noordwaard take the river again — and he was banking the soil against the white of them with the flat of his hand. He rinsed under the outside tap and they went in.

On the shelf his wife watched from a photograph in a frame he dusted and Anneke didn't: Rie in a headscarf at the rail of the Merwede ferry, the summer before it stopped running. He had waxed the table every month since she died. He kept the gutters clear. He tested the cellar on the first of each month, and Anneke, who had grown up over that cellar, still knew the one long note the high-water alarm gave when he lifted the float by hand and it swung up true. His granddaughter Mirthe, Anneke's eldest, nineteen and away in Nijmegen, sent him voice-notes he played on the little kitchen radio, and he had saved every one and did not know how to delete one and would not have. He walked Anneke to the boiler cupboard and showed her the service tag, initialled and dated in his own hand, as if the assessment might want to satisfy itself the boiler was sound. He had nursed Rie at home the last two years of her, the district nurse in twice a day and Willem the other twenty-two hours, and he had kept her chart in pencil on the back of the pantry door — her doses, her turns, the little she would eat — in the same neat hand as the boiler tag. The chart was still there behind the door, the last line dated the morning she died, and he had painted the pantry around it and left it standing.

She wrote none of it in the file, because there is no field for a waxed table, and no field for a man who keeps a house the way you keep a promise to somebody who cannot come back to check it. She asked her questions. He answered them level, the way a gauge answers, no reach in them and no lean. The boiler was sound and the wish was steady and both facts kept house together under the one roof without either having to give ground to the other.

Before she left he filled a jar with the year's leeks, trimmed and rinsed, and pressed it on her at the door. Forty minutes. The box white.

···

The fifth interview she came within a word of flagging. Halfway through it he said that lately he did not always see the point of the morning coffee, and her pen stopped over the paper, because on any other file in her bag that sentence is the head of a path that ends at a psychiatrist's door and a hold of six months. She put the questions that go under it — the nine of them, the ones that tell a man who is finished from a man who is ill and could be carried back up. He answered them the way he would have answered a surveyor come out to test his ground: here it is, here is the soft spot, walk it yourself and see. The soft place was her mother, eleven years gone. Grief is not a flag. Grief a man has carried eleven years and set down each night by his own hand is not the kind of seepage that runs the sand out from under a dike and drops it in an afternoon. She left the box white and hated the pen in her hand for a minute, and then went on, because the pen was the job and the job was the reason he had asked for her and no one else.

···

The week between his fifth and his sixth she carried four other files up and down the river, because his was not the only life the district weighed that spring. A man in Hardinxveld who had outlasted his union and his lungs both, who cleaned a canary's cage every morning and could not find one person who would promise to keep the bird after. Two she turned back to their own doctors at the first meeting, gently, because a hard month is not a settled wish and the Act was never built to be a fast door out of a hard month. And a woman in Sleeuwijk, eighty-one, whose husband had indexed low after a bleed on the brain fourteen years back and never come up the scale again, who kept his reading glasses folded on the windowsill and laid a place for him at supper still, and who wanted, she said, to stop keeping a house for a man the machine had let go of a long time ago. Anneke drove home from that one and sat in her own lane with the motor ticking as it cooled and did not go inside for a while.

She never carried one file into another. When she sat down for the sixth interview she had rinsed the others off her the way you sluice the river silt off your boots before you come to the table. She asked her father the standing questions. He gave the standing answers. The box stayed white. But the glasses on the sill in Sleeuwijk stayed with her through the coffee, and so did the service tag by his boiler, and she drove back to Gorinchem that night and wrote up all five files at her kitchen table and flagged the two that wanted flagging and slept badly.

···

The seventh interview he asked to do outside, and she allowed it, because the protocol cares that a man be steady, not that he sit indoors to prove it. He took her up over the crown of the terp and down onto the old dike that ran out west into the Noordwaard — the low country beyond Werkendam that the waterschap, the regional water board, had handed back to the river when Anneke was still at the technical school. He walked it the way he had walked it thirty years for a living, reading the ground under his boots without appearing to look down.

He named things for her the way he had when she was small and he would bring her out on a Sunday with a thermos and a hard-boiled egg. The inlaat — the inlet, a plain concrete sill you could stand on and never guess a county's dry cellars ran across it. The mounds, the farms lifted up out of the reach of the water, dark against the sky. The old creeks they had dug back open through the reeds so the flood, when it came, would find a way it remembered. He crouched once and pushed his thumb down into the grass at the toe of the dike and told her the clay was still fat with the winter, which meant nothing she could use and everything to him.

He stopped where the dike had been let down on purpose, a long low place engineered into a high bank — a drempel, a threshold — so that the river would come over here, and only here, and nowhere the works had not chosen for it.

"When the Merwede runs high up at Gorinchem," he said, "it comes in over this lip and lies down across all of it, and that takes thirty centimetres off the top upstream. Thirty centimetres is Gorinchem's cellars staying dry. It's the whole of the difference between a bad wet spring and the trucks coming for everybody."

The trucks. He meant the winter of '95, and he said it the way every man his age along the river said it, as one word for one thing. A quarter of a million people and their cattle brought up off the river land in three days, the Rijn and the Maas standing against dikes that had never been asked to hold that much for that long, and the whole country awake in the dark over the one question, whether the sand under the old dikes had begun to move. It had not. Barely. He had been twenty-nine that winter, a new engineer with a torch and a clipboard walking the Alblasserwaard dikes at three in the morning, out looking for the first sand boil — the water welling up on the dry side with the grit coming with it, the dike quietly hollowing itself from underneath while it still stood there looking whole. They had spent the twenty years after that winter making certain the country would never again stake a province on barely.

"You can't build for every flood." He was not looking at her; he was looking down the reach of the water going pewter as the light went. "You build the river dikes for the flood that comes once in twelve hundred and fifty years, and then you go home and you sleep. Everything past that you carry by choosing. You pick the field you can stand to lose, and you go to the people whose field it is, at their own tables, and you don't dress it up for them, and you ask them to put their name to it. All of this" — the reeds, the mounds, the creeks — "is people who signed. My father grew beets out there where the tide's coming in now. I'm the one who moved this house up onto the mound and let the water back over his beetfield. He's in the churchyard at Werkendam. I put his ground under with his grandson's name on the drawing, and he'd have signed it himself, because he stood on this same dike in the winter of '53 and heard the south of the country go under in the dark while ours held by a hand's breadth."

She had been at the technical school for the works themselves, but she had grown up on the other half of them, the half that happened at kitchen tables. For two years before a spade went into the Noordwaard he had driven out on his evenings to farms that had carried one name since the old survey and sat with people while their coffee went cold, telling them the plain thing — that the water was coming whatever any of them signed, and that the only choice left on any table was whether it came where they had chosen or where they had not. Some of them he lost. Some he won and wished after that he had lost. An old man off toward Hank had put his name to it and then not spoken to Willem again the six years it took him to die, and Willem had driven to the funeral all the same and stood at the back of the church and signed nothing.

She stood on the sill beside him with the grey recorder in her coat pocket and left it there. Eleven years up and down the river, and she had never once sat next to a man who knew the price of a thing the way he knew it. He had said the same few sentences to strangers over their own kitchen tables until they signed — that you cannot keep all of it, that the water is always coming, and that a man's only say in the matter is where he lets it in. He had said them to her out here at seven and at twelve and at twenty, with a thermos and an egg, until she could have said them back in her sleep, and had.

He read her the level off his phone without being asked — plus twenty-eight, holding — and put it away and stood a while longer looking at the water he had spent his life deciding the manners of. Then he said it was getting cold and they had best go up. On the steep of the terp he took her arm, on the river side, the way you would steady anyone on a bank in poor light. She let him have it, and let him think it was for him.

···

The eighth she did at the office, by the book, because she had stood out on that dike and let herself be his daughter for an afternoon and she knew it. Before he came she played the seventh back off the recorder and marked one place where she had said *we* and should have said *you*. She held the eighth to the form. Had anything changed. Nothing had changed. Had anyone pressed him toward this, or away from it. The dominee — the village's Reformed minister — had tried the once, months back, and had let it lie when Willem asked him to; and Anneke herself had never in the whole of it said a word to turn him one way or the other, which he wanted set down as the reason he had asked for her and no one else. She wrote it down exactly as he said it. Her hand stayed level the length of the line. She was proud of the hand that day, and of not much else.

···

The ninth nearly did not happen, because Doortje's afternoon slipped and an independent second has to be in the room in person for the closing meetings. It held. He had the tie on and two cups poured and waiting when they came. Eight minutes. White.

···

The tenth was the hardest hour of the twelve, harder than the dike.

He had begun to let the house down. The good aluminium ladder was gone — given to the neighbour, he said, who had been after it for years. The leek bed was cut and turned and left black and flat, not replanted, done for the year and, by the settle of the soil, done for good. On the first of the month the cellar had not been tested; the service tag by the boiler stopped two dates back, the last line in his own hand and then nothing under it. She stood at the top of the cellar stairs out of an old habit and listened for the one long note, and there was none, because he had not gone down to raise the float and make it come.

That was the loudest thing he said to her in four months, and he said it by not doing it, and there was no field on the form it would go in.

She did not ask him about the alarm. Which things he had let go and which he had left, she kept out of the file. On the shed wall the old peilschaal still hung where it always had — the painted level staff, the enamel numbers climbing up from the ground, that he had carried home off a lock they decommissioned the year he retired. That he had left. He had taken down the far pages of the calendar and given away the ladder and let the cellar go quiet, and he had left the level staff on the wall. So was the little radio on the counter, with Mirthe's voice-notes still saved on it; that he had not given away and would not. A man setting his affairs in order is not a flag, it is the most ordinary work in the world, and only a daughter would stand in a cellar doorway and grieve an alarm that no one had run.

She got through the questions. She got the answers. White in the box. In the lane afterward she sat with the motor off and did not read the river level on her phone, the one time in four months she did not look it up, because she did not want to know what the water was doing out there without him to watch it.

···

The eleventh was the one Doortje came out for in person, as the independent second must for the closing meetings. She drove the same dented Corsa she had driven a decade and ate her bread standing up at Willem's counter, because she had never in her life managed to sit through a coffee. After, the two of them took the whole file back through at the office — the twelve fields, the white boxes, the notes worked into the margins — and Doortje read it the way a good second reads, hunting the daylight between what the applicant wants and what the family wants, and she found none, because there was none in it to find. Willem had wanted this in his own order, the works and Rie and the house, since before there was a form to want it on. The file wanted only the last meeting to close it.

Doortje shut the folder and said the finding was clean and she would co-sign it and she was sorry, and Anneke said thank you, and neither of them made it any more than that. Going out to the Corsa, Doortje put a hand on Anneke's shoulder and left it there a second and said nothing, which was the only thing that fit the shape of the space, and drove off toward Gorinchem in the dark with the one taillight out she had never got round to.

···

The twelfth interview was the shortest of them, and the one that closed it on paper. He wore the tie. She read him the finding and asked him, the last time the form would make her ask, whether the wish was still his and still what he wanted. He said it was. She signed where the lead assessor signs, and wrote above her name the sentence she had written for strangers a hundred times, in the same words, because the form did not know he was her father and she would not let her handwriting be the thing that told it: *the wish is voluntary, well-considered, and enduring; the applicant has capacity; no treatable condition is driving it; the assessment is complete.*

Then the day was his to set. He asked for the soonest the Act allowed once the file closed, and she counted it off on the kitchen calendar he had not yet taken down that far ahead, and it fell on a Tuesday. He wrote it in himself, in pencil, the way he would have logged a date to open a gate. He walked her out to the car. He told her the water was expected to keep falling all the week. That was the twelfth interview.

···

The day he chose was a Tuesday in March. The Merwede at Werkendam that morning stood at plus twenty-four and falling, which Anneke looked up in the car before she went in, the way he would have, so that one of them in the house would be holding the number.

The physician was a steady woman named Elly who had done this a long time and wasted none of it. She set a small cool-box on the waxed table and did not hurry. She had come alone, the way the Act's physicians mostly worked now, and she talked him through each step before her hands did it, which was hers to do and not Anneke's. By the book she asked him twice whether this was still what he wanted — once in her own plain words and once in the words the Act put in her mouth — and both times he said yes, the second time with a flick of impatience, because he had answered that question twelve times over for his daughter and considered it thoroughly asked.

Elly laid the line into the back of his hand and taped it down and told him he could take all the time he liked. He did not want much. He looked at Rie on the shelf in her headscarf, and at the window where the light came in off the water, and then he reached across with the hand that had no line in it — the broad flat hand that had banked the clay against the leeks and steadied her on the terp — and took Anneke's hand, and held it. He was holding hers. Not she his. He said her name the once. Then Elly did the thing she had come to do, and it was quiet, and outside the level went on down toward the sea the way it had gone down every hour of his life, and Anneke held on and kept the number for the two of them, plus twenty-four and falling, because someone in the room ought to carry it, and the carrying of the number had always, all her life, been his.

···

The following Monday she had a new file — a man in Woudrichem, eighty, on his own request. She drove out with the grey recorder on the passenger seat and the office coffee going cold in the holder, and she found the house and sat down at a table that was not her mother's and stood the machine upright between them. She filled in the date, the number, the name. The man folded his hands and waited. Somewhere out past the roofs the river was at whatever it was at, and she would learn in time to look it up for him too.

She turned the machine on. The six words had been a procedure for eleven years, worn smooth as a doorsill under a thumb, and she had said them to her father twelve times, and she set them down now the way he had taught her to set a level and walk off the dike and trust it — because it was true, and because there was nothing else to be done with the morning.

"Tell me why you've come."
