## 6. Conjunction

*2038*

Noor takes the handoff at 22:00 with the queue at nineteen boxes — nineteen close passes between objects in orbit, each to be screened for the odds it comes to a collision — and Reyes, the screener she is relieving, already into one sleeve of his coat.

He walks the queue standing, hip against the console, cracking pistachios one-handed and dropping the shells into a paper cup he will leave for her to empty. Two operators pushed fresh ephemerides inside the hour, he says — updated predictions of where their satellites will fly — so the screening re-runs and the numbers move under you; trust nothing older than the last pass. One yellow, on the Halcyon platform — a fragment out of the '33 deorbit clearances, catalogued rough, probability of collision near six times ten to the minus six and dropping as more tracking fills the orbit in. Nothing red. Nobody to wake.

He signs his last determination while he talks, thumbs his operator ID into the field and lets the certification dialog take his PIN, and the record writes the minute, and the floor is hers.

"You're the name till oh-six," he says. He knocks the little brass plate someone bolted to the console years back — SIGNER OF RECORD, worn to a yellow shine where thumbs have hit it — and takes his cup of shells and goes. Since the reforms, one certified human owns every no-maneuver call on anything flying people — every decision to let a crewed spacecraft ride out a close pass instead of burning fuel to dodge — and after 22:00 that human is Noor.

The floor has one window. Past the reflection of her own screens the sky over Colorado's Front Range holds its usual wash, the low constellations strung across it corner to corner, the managed brightness that comes up every clear night now, and not one real star in it she could set a finger on. They run the sky from a room with no sight of it.

She takes the queue by time of closest approach. A weather bird and a dead upper stage: eleven kilometers radial at the miss, both covariances tight — each object's position well pinned down — Pc, the probability the two actually collide, two times ten to the minus six. Green. MONITOR. She keys the number into the determination field and signs — ID, PIN, dialog, timestamp — and the record commits, and the next box loads. The catalog runs to the tens of thousands now, most of it junk, and the screening throws her a few hundred close approaches a shift, and nearly all of them are eleven clean kilometers and a number with a lot of zeroes after the point. The work is the sorting. The work is deciding which of the hundred nothings might be the one thing.

Two other screeners work the far consoles tonight, heads down, the floor keeping the hum it always keeps — fans, disks, somebody's radio tuned low to nothing she can name. The catalog she reads is mostly what the '33 clearances left behind, broken small, and the boxes come faster every year for it. Two hundred and forty close approaches on her sector last shift. More tonight.

She clears four more before the top of the hour. A defunct relay and a spent kick stage, sixteen kilometers, green, signed. Two constellation birds a plane apart that the screening flags and she clears in a glance because the operator flies them to a keep-out tighter than anything she would call. A dead cubesat and a dead cubesat, which is the easiest box there is, two corpses that will drift past each other for a thousand years and never once be told to move. Each one a number, a disposition, a PIN, the record breathing shut behind her.

···

The night it happened she had this chair and a game of chess three days from its end.

Ade played Black and played it patient, the Caro-Kann every time, the pawn wall she would sit behind for thirty moves while you talked yourself into overreaching. She flew flight engineer on Vireo, the older platform, and between her tasks and Noor's boxes they moved a move a day through the crew relay, Ade signing off with the notation and nothing else — *...c6*, *...Bf5* — because words came out of her downlink allowance and a move said hello well enough. Taped by her sleep station she kept a paper photograph of a lake in Minnesota, the one thing of weight she had carried up, and once on a slow pass she turned the crew camera to it so Noor could see the dock and the two red chairs. Three days before, Ade had resigned a rook ending she could not hold, a pawn light off one loose move made twenty moves earlier, the loss already sitting in the position long before it surfaced on the board. She owed Noor White next game.

They had never met on the ground and never would have. Ade was six years older and four hundred kilometers up, a voice that reached Noor only as text and, twice, as a face gone soft and slow on a lagged downlink. The game was how they talked. A move was *I'm still here*. When Noor drew a bad shift, the kind where a box turns red at 04:00 and you spend the dark hours with a stranger's platform in your hands, she would find a move waiting from Ade in the morning, sent at some hour that made no sense until you remembered there is no night up there worth the name, only ninety-minute days and the work between them.

Once, the week she went up, Ade spent real words — four lines of downlink she must have hoarded against her allowance — to tell Noor that from four hundred kilometers the lake was a coin of light you could cover with a thumb, and that she had covered it with her thumb, and that it was still there when she took the thumb away. Noor read it three times and answered with a knight to f3, because she did not know what else to send a person that far up who missed a dock that badly.

The conjunction came up on the 03:00 screening. A fragment from the '33 clearances crossing Vireo's track, closing near thirteen kilometers a second. Noor worked it the way she worked all of them. She tasked more tracking. The covariance tightened over four passes, the state — the fix on where the fragment was and where it was headed — firmed, and the Pc settled at four times ten to the minus five. Below the line. The line is ten to the minus four and has been for years, and below it you do not spend a crewed platform's propellant to put it somewhere new in a crowded shell for a chance under one in twenty thousand — every maneuver is a move you cannot take back, and it opens conjunctions of its own that somebody then has to screen. She entered MONITOR. She keyed the number. She signed it and went to the next box, because fourteen sat behind it.

Closest approach was 04:41. What the floor did at 04:41 she holds in pieces, out of their order, and has stopped trying to set straight.

After is cleaner, because after is paperwork. Her badge pulled for the review inside the hour. The console imaged, the logs impounded, this chair flagged out of service for two days and then not. The shift lead that night set a hand flat on the console beside hers and left it there a while and said nothing, which was the kindest thing on offer and the only true one. Someone left a plate under foil on her porch step and was gone before she opened the door. She ate none of it and could not throw it out, and it sat in the refrigerator through the spring, a kindness she had no procedure for. The printout that went into evidence had her PIN-time along the bottom and the Pc in the body, four times ten to the minus five, the number she had read correctly off a screen she had built correctly and entered under her own name. She went in each day of the review and answered what they asked and drove home under the washed-out sky and did not look up, because there was nothing up there to see and everything up there to account for. Ade's lake stayed taped where it was until they brought the module down eleven weeks later, and then it did not.

For a while the resigned game stayed live in her account, the final position frozen one screen back where Ade's last message lived, White up a clean pawn, a win Noor never played out because there was no one across the board to play it against. She did not close it. She did not open it either. It is in there still, under a tab, the way the finding letter is in the drawer at home — a thing decided, filed, and impossible to throw away.

···

The queue is at eleven when Gene comes by on his round.

He is the lead tonight, thirty years of it, and he carries a challenge coin worn smooth as a creek stone that he turns across his knuckles when he stands and reads your screens over your shoulder. He reads hers. The Halcyon yellow has ticked up — the fragment's latest pass moved the miss in, Pc near nine times ten to the minus six now, still yellow, still hours from closest approach. He turns the coin once. "Keep tasking it," he says, which she is already doing, and he moves down the row, and that is the whole of it, because Gene has never once in fourteen months said the other thing to her, and she has learned to take his quiet as its own kind of respect.

She screens two more, greens both, signs both. The night has the shape the good nights have — box and number and signature, box and number and signature, the queue breathing down one at a time. A cluster of constellation birds threading a decayed rocket body, all of it noise, all of it hers to clear one box at a time. Then the 02:20 pass drops a fresh CDM — a conjunction data message, the updated screening on that pair — into the Halcyon box and the number has stopped being yellow.

Pc four times ten to the minus four. Over the line. Halcyon has nine people aboard.

···

The review board sat eleven weeks.

They met in a windowless room two floors down with read-ahead binders tabbed in colors, and they reconstructed her shift to the second — the screening runs, the tasking requests, the passes, the covariance at each epoch, the moment she keyed MONITOR and the moment she signed. An independent team re-ran her Pc from the raw tracks on a different code base, blind to her result, and got four times ten to the minus five to two figures. The math was hers and the math was right. They asked whether more tracking could have been tasked, and earlier; the record showed she had tasked everything the schedule allowed, when procedure said to task it. They asked her to walk the covariance. She walked the covariance. The board's chair, a woman who refilled the water glasses herself from a steel carafe between questions so no one had to ask, wrote it all down.

Eleven weeks is a long time to be reconstructed. They had her keystroke log and the interval between passes to the second, and they asked the same questions in different clothes across the weeks — what she saw, when she saw it, why she signed when she signed — until her own night came back to her in their language instead of hers, a timeline with her name on the events. She sat through it in the same blazer every session, because choosing a second one felt like a decision she did not have in her. The recompute team never once looked at her, which was procedure too; they had to stay blind to her result to be worth anything. When their number came up matching hers to two figures, someone read it into the room like a weather report, and it was the loneliest thing she heard all spring — her arithmetic confirmed by strangers who had worked for months to see whether it would break, and it had not broken, because it had never been wrong.

The finding letter came in March. She has it in a drawer at home and does not need it in front of her to say the sentences. *The screening was conducted in accordance with established procedure. No deviation was identified. The certification decision was correct on the information available at the time of signature.* Not one line of it about fault, in either direction — the document is colder and more exact than that. It does not tell her she is not to blame. It tells her the determination was correct, which is a different finding, and the harder one to carry, because a correct determination is a thing you would make again.

At the bottom of the last page a field waited for her to acknowledge receipt. She thumbed her ID and entered her PIN and the record wrote the minute. Another signature. She had come to think of her whole life as a column of them.

···

Pc four times ten to the minus four, and the clock to closest approach reads three hours nine minutes.

For a second she does not move at all, and the floor keeps its hum, and the brass plate under her hand says whose call this is. Then she works.

She tasks it wide — every sensor with a horizon on that orbit, optical and radar both, priority override, which she can set because tonight she is the name on the plate. The first thing to understand is why the number jumped, and the number jumped because the fragment's covariance was loose, a long thin ellipsoid smeared down-track from a thin observation history, and a loose covariance is a liar in both directions: it can drown a real threat in uncertainty until the Pc reads safe, and it can throw up a four-times-ten-to-the-minus-four out of a miss that is actually clean, because when you barely know where a thing is, you cannot yet rule out that it is exactly where you are. You do not answer a loose covariance with a burn. You answer it with more looking.

She pulls the operator up on the coordination line. Halcyon's flight director is a tired man named Okwú who keeps a maneuver ready as a matter of standing procedure, a debris avoidance burn already planned and screened, seven-tenths of a meter a second retrograde, propellant costed, the crew briefed to secure for it if the call comes. He wants to know if the call is coming. She tells him she is refining the state and will have the next pass in twenty minutes and the pass after that in fifty, and that the last time she can accept his burn and still screen the orbit it puts him into is 03:52, because a maneuver only trades one position for another, and the new one has to be read for its own threats before you commit a platform to it. He knows this better than she does. He says he will hold. His breathing sits on the open line. She can hear his floor through him, the flat call-and-response of a crew running a contingency they have drilled a hundred times in the sim and never once for real, and under it, if she listens, the particular quiet of people waiting on a number that a stranger two states away is going to hand them.

Gene comes and stands at her shoulder and does not turn the coin.

The 02:41 pass lands. Twelve new tracks on the fragment, the observation arc stretched, the covariance drawing in from a smear to a fat cigar, and the miss distance opens as the ellipsoid tightens off her own object's ephemeris. She reruns the Pc. Two times ten to the minus four. Still over. Better. She does not say better out loud. Over the line is over the line, and Halcyon is nine people, and one of them keeps a photograph taped to a wall she will never see.

Chess is a game about the move you do not make. Ade taught her that behind the Caro-Kann, thirty moves of not-yet, of improving the position by a hair and handing the pressure back, of making the move only when the position forces it — because the player who moves for the sake of moving is the player who loses, and the position was usually decided long before either hand touched a piece. The fragment and Halcyon were on this track weeks ago. The geometry was set the day that thing came off the '33 clearances with the number it had. All Noor is doing tonight is reading a game already played, and deciding, on the strength of the reading, whether to reach in and change it.

02:58. The wide tasking pays. A radar site low on the horizon catches a long clean arc and the state firms hard, the covariance collapsing to something she can nearly trust, and when the fit converges the miss walks out to nine hundred meters cross-track with an ellipsoid that no longer overlaps her hull volume the way the loose one did. She reruns the Pc. One point three times ten to the minus four. A hair over. Okwú's breathing changes on the line.

Behind him his floor is doing the quiet arithmetic every crewed floor does with a live one — securing loose mass, briefing the burn attitude, nine people told to stay near the node and off the cupola for a closest approach they will ride through blind at thirteen kilometers a second whether or not the thing is there. One of them is running the checklist Ade used to run. Noor does not follow that thought to the wall it is taped to. She has a number to firm and forty-nine minutes to firm it in.

She has fifty-one minutes to his deadline and one more scheduled pass at 03:31, and the whole grammar of the night comes down to whether she trusts the trend or trusts the number in front of her. The number in front of her is over the line. The trend is a covariance still tightening and a miss still opening, pass on pass, the way a firming state is supposed to behave when the two things are actually going to miss. If she waits for 03:31 and it drops under the line, she signs a no-maneuver and leaves nine people to ride through a nine-hundred-meter miss at thirteen kilometers a second on the strength of her arithmetic. If she calls the burn now, she spends Okwú's seven-tenths of a meter a second and puts Halcyon somewhere new and never knows what the fragment would have done, and neither does anyone else, and that not-knowing is its own soft place to land — no one writes a board report on the collision that didn't happen because you moved.

That is the whole seduction of the burn, and Ade would have named it: the move that feels like safety because it puts the game somewhere no one can grade. You can always defend a maneuver. The wait is only ever defensible by the number, and the number is a thing she has to trust further than her own hands — hands that have keyed a correct call before, on a screen exactly this one, at an hour not far off this one.

The 03:10 look she counts on comes back thin. The optical site she wanted is socked in under weather on the Pacific, three tracks where she asked for thirty, and for six minutes the covariance stops shrinking and simply sits, and she has to read whether a stalled fit is a fluke of cloud or the state telling her it has found its true shape a hair over the line. She holds. Weather is weather. She has worked enough degraded passes to know the difference between a state that has converged and a fit that has only run out of light to see by.

She waits.

She waits the way Ade would have waited, sitting behind the pawns, and it is the hardest single thing she does all night, harder than the eleven weeks, harder than the drawer at home. Okwú counts her down without meaning to, just his breath and the low hum of his floor behind him. Gene stands and does not turn the coin. The queue behind Halcyon has grown to nine while she has held on this one box, greens stacking up unsigned because she is the only name on the floor and her hands are full of nine people. She lets them stack. A screener who signs a green fast to clear her plate is a screener who signs the wrong green fast some other night. She keeps the box in front of her and her eyes on the covariance and her thumb, without deciding it, on the brass plate. The clock burns down through 03:20. Okwú says nothing and does not hang up. The 03:31 pass comes in four minutes late and she does not breathe through the fit, and the state firms one more notch, and the miss holds at nine hundred and forty meters, and the covariance is a tight bean now, honest, no longer lying in either direction. She reruns the Pc.

Seven times ten to the minus five.

Under the line. One in fourteen thousand. Real, and small, and hers.

"Sixteen minutes to your deadline," she tells Okwú. "I'm reading seven times ten to the minus five on a converged state, nine-forty cross-track and opening. I'm not going to take your burn."

The line is quiet for a second. "Confirmed no maneuver," Okwú says, in the flat voice a man uses to write a thing into a log, and he does write it into a log, and somewhere above the wash of the sky his nine people stand down from a burn they mostly never knew was loaded. He does not thank her. He signs off. That is the courtesy — he leaves her to sign it alone.

The disposition field is open on her screen. She selects NO MANEUVER. She keys the Pc into the determination field, seven times ten to the minus five, and the TCA — the time of closest approach — 05:29, and the converged epoch, and the miss components, radial and in-track and cross-track, the numbers she read correctly off a screen she built correctly, entered under her own name. She thumbs her operator ID. The certification dialog rises and asks whether she, the signer of record, certifies this determination on a crewed asset, and a second field asks her to confirm the maneuver disposition, NONE, and she confirms it, and she puts her PIN into the dialog a digit at a time the way she has put in ten thousand PINs, and her thumb finds the brass plate on the top of the console the way Reyes's did on his way out, worn yellow, cool under the pad of her thumb. She presses enter.

Under her name the log writes 03:47:52. The Halcyon box drops to MONITOR and slides down the queue. The count reads seven. The next box loads.
