## 10. Tonewood

I had the top pinched between my thumb and one finger at the corner, the way you hold a plate so it can still ring, and I tapped it once with my knuckle and it came up around an F, a shade sharp of it, and that was when you asked me whether it was true the salt had made the wood worth more.

Fair question. Wrong one, but fair, and since you flew in on it and you've set your case open on my bench like the guitar is already yours, you may as well have the whole answer. It runs about as long as the glue takes to reach. Sit where you won't put your elbow in the shellac.

This is red spruce. Picea rubens, the Acadian spruce, the same wood the old Boston houses put in their good guitars before the war and then couldn't get, because the stands that would give you a soundboard were logged off by 1930 and what grew back grew fast and loose and useless for anything but a stud. My grandfather's father left forty acres of it standing on the back of the marsh when everyone around him was cutting, and nobody in the family ever knew whether that was foresight or laziness, and it doesn't matter, because the trees can't tell you which and they came out the same either way. Tight. Slow. Rings you could barely fit a fingernail between. My father used to hold a fresh round up to me and make me count to twenty before my thumb had crossed an inch of it.

You want to see what makes a top. Come to the window. Tilt this so the light runs down it — there. Watch how the one half goes bright and the other half goes dark, and the line where they trade sits dead down the center joint. That's the two halves reading their grain against the light the same and opposite, because they were split off one billet and opened like a book, and where that bright-and-dark flips even, both sides matched, that's low runout. Runout is the grain diving under the surface instead of running flat along it. You can't see it looking straight on. You see it in the flip. And every degree the grain dives is stiffness leaking out the side of the board where you can't get it back, so my father wouldn't saw a top, ever. He split them. Rived them off the billet with a froe and a maul, let the wood choose its own plane, because a saw cuts where you point it and a split follows the grain wherever the grain already goes. He said sawing a soundboard is telling the wood where it lives. Splitting is asking it.

He taught me the tap on this same stool, when I was small enough he had to hold the plate for me and I only got to knock it. You pinch the plate at the corner where a mode line runs cold, the node, the place the wood isn't moving, and you tap out over the belly where it is, and you don't listen for loud. You listen for long. A live red spruce plate rings and then keeps ringing, a bell held under water, and a dead one gives you a knock and quits. He'd tap and name the note off it. G. A-flat. He named them out of a lifetime of it the way Marj up the marsh names the tide without looking, and half the time I couldn't hear the note and I could always hear the difference, the ring against the knock, which is the only ear the work actually needs. He'd thin a top with a scraper and tap it, thin it and tap it, chasing the note down and the ring up — voicing, he called it — until a plate you could read newsprint through rang like something twice its weight. Then he'd put it in my hands and make me knock it and say nothing, only watch my face, because he said your face hears it a second before your head will own up to it.

The marsh is why any of it was there. You drove in over it and you didn't know what you were looking at, so I'll tell you, because the marsh is half the answer and you only asked for the wood half.

Everything flat you crossed on the dyke road was salt marsh once, Spartina and mud, flooded twice a day by the Fundy tide, and the Fundy tide is not a tide you have where you're from. Fifty feet of it, top to bottom, at the head of the bay. It comes up the Cobequid a wall of brown and it goes out and leaves the bottom of the world showing for a mile. Two hundred years ago the Acadians walled it off — built the dykes by hand out of the marsh sod itself, cut in blocks, and set an aboiteau at every low place where a creek wanted through. An aboiteau's just a sluice through the dyke with a wooden gate hung in it, a clapper, hinged at the top. Fresh water and rain push it open from behind and drain out at low tide. The salt tide pushes it shut. It swings closed against the sea and it opens for the land, and it does that twice a day on its own with nobody watching it, and behind it the salt leaches down out of the ground over years until the marsh turns sweet and you can hay it, pasture it, and up on the high back edge where it's been sweet longest, grow forty acres of the tightest spruce on the eastern seaboard.

That's the whole trick. The dyke keeps the sea off and the aboiteau does the keeping. My father walked ours every morning of his life. He had a tide clock on the shop wall, brass, older than me, and he set it to the marsh and not to the town, and he wound it before he wound anything else, because a man who works below a fifty-foot tide keeps the tide's time and lets the town keep its own.

You learn a tide like that in your feet before your head gets it — that the ground you're standing on is bottom, that twice a day the bay comes up over where you're standing to the height of a house and goes out again, and that the only things between the two facts are a wall of dug sod a dead man cut by hand and a wooden gate that shuts itself. You don't take it for granted, raised on it. You watch the low spot in the creek. Every spring the marsh body walks the whole run of the dyke looking for a soft place, a muskrat run, a rot coming up through the sod, because a dyke doesn't fail all at once. It fails at one bad foot of itself, and then the tide finds the hole and does the rest of it in a night.

Two years ago the province put our marsh on the realignment schedule. I'll say it the once and then leave it, because it's not the part that matters and you'll want to make it the part that matters. Realignment means they stop keeping the dyke. Sea-level's up, the dykes all want raising, and there's more marsh in this province than money to wall it, so they ran the numbers and drew a line, and marshes below the line get given back to the tide on a date. They stop the crews. They quit the aboiteau. They let the gate rot open and the sea comes home on its own schedule after that. Ours was down for the spring after next. We had two harvests coming before the water did.

We got none of them the way we planned.

Marj found it. Marj farms the next parcel down and she walks the dyke every morning the way my father did, boots rolled to her knee, and she keeps the marsh-body's tide log in a duck-cloth book she's had since before the book meant anything, and she came up the shop lane one morning in April with the book still in her hand and told me the aboiteau at the low creek was standing open on a flood tide.

Not rotted open. This was a year and a half before the province was going to let it rot. Standing open on the flood, the clapper hung back on its hinge, and something under it holding it back — a jam of driftwood, she said, could've been driftwood, a cut length of two-by wedged up under the gate where the current wouldn't have set it square, could've been the tide put it there. Could've been. We stood on the dyke and looked at it and neither of us finished the sentence, because you don't, and the salt was already three floods into the marsh by the time she saw it and there's no getting salt back out of the ground once it's in. You can shut the gate. You can't unsalt the mud. The tide had come home a year and a half early and let itself in the low creek and stood in the ground.

I want you to hear how slow it is, because you'll be imagining a flood and it wasn't a flood. It was three weeks. The salt goes down into the ground water and the ground water is what the spruce roots drink, and spruce has no salt in it and never learned to, and it browns from the inside on its own time. The needles went first, up on the back forty, a rust coming into the green that you'd take for a dry spell if you didn't know. Then the small stuff on the low edge died outright and stood there dead. By June I had forty acres turning the color of a burnt-out stove, standing dead on their feet, a whole woodlot of snags, and you can drive people out from the city to see a thing like that now and they have a word for it, they call it a ghost forest, they take a picture of it and they feel the way they came to feel.

My father was eighty-one that spring and he'd stopped coming to the shop by then, mostly, but he made me drive him out to the back forty when the brown came up, and he got out at the field edge and stood on his cane and looked at it a long time, and he didn't say the province and he didn't say the gate and he didn't say who. He said, *well, they're cut now,* the way you'd say it of a thing already down and bucked and stacked, though every one of them was standing. And he got back in the truck.

I'll tell you what I know and no more, because the rest is other people's mouths. My father knew that aboiteau better than the man who hung the last gate in it. He'd have sooner drowned that marsh with his own hands than watched a provincial crew walk off and let the sea take it on a Tuesday to a schedule. He said as much, more than once, to Marj, to me, into his tea. And my brother Curtis, who'd been at Dad for three years to take the buyout and be done, who'd already had the standing timber cruised and carried the number around on his phone like a man carrying a winning ticket he can't cash — Curtis knew what the wood would do the day it went scarce. Curtis knew that better than anyone. The both of them had a reason and neither of them has a alibi that means anything on a marsh where the nearest witness is a heron, and the RCMP came out the once, wrote *driftwood, undetermined,* and closed it, and I have never asked either one of them the question straight because there is no version of the answer I could carry back across the yard.

I'll give you one more thing off that morning and then I'll leave it. When I went down to shut the gate myself, ahead of the crew, my father's froe was leaning in the grass at the head of the aboiteau. His froe. The one he'd split every top of my life with, that lived on a nail on that wall behind you. I carried it home and hung it back on its nail and I have never once asked him how it came to be a mile down at the low creek on legs that could barely make it across the dooryard. The froe could have gone down there a hundred honest ways. I can't name you one of them. Both of those are true at the same time, and I've decided that's the ground I live on now.

So I don't know. That's the true thing. I don't know, and I built nine guitars out of not knowing, and I'll build no more.

Here's where you come in, you and yours, and I'll try to keep the meanness out of it because you didn't do the individual harm, you just made the weather that the harm pays off in.

The wood went scarce in a morning. Forty acres of red spruce, the last of the last, standing dead — and dead standing is not ruined, that's the thing people get wrong. A spruce that dies on its feet in April and comes down before the next winter's checks and blue-stain get into it is sound wood, better than sound, because it seasoned two seasons standing before I ever touched a saw to it. The tree died upright. It didn't drown lying in the mud. I got a crew in with a skidder before the ground froze and we took the best of them down that fall — felled, bucked, hauled up to the yard — and I quartered the good rounds into cants on the mill and cut the sap side off every one where the salt had gone into it grey. Out of the heart of them I split billets with the same froe he used, followed the grain the way he'd shown me, and I stickered them up in the loft over the shop to come down slow through the winter, a sticker every foot, weights on top, the woodstove going below them. And all that winter I did the one thing that tells you anything. I tapped them. Held each plate at the node and knocked it and listened for long and not for loud. Most of them rang. Some came up dead — a knock and done, the salt gone too deep to hear past — and those I split for kindling and fed to the stove, and I was warm off my own inheritance for a winter.

And the phone started. From your side of the country and then from farther. Because the story had got out ahead of the wood the way a story does, the last spruce off the drowned marsh, the ghost-forest wood, and men who had never in their lives held a guitar wanted a guitar made of it. A luthier three provinces over called to buy my billets green off the mill and I said no. A dealer offered me for one top what my father cleared off the whole woodlot in a good year of Christmas trees. There is a grey haze come into some of the wood now, up off the medullary rays, where the salt sat in it, and the market has decided to call that salt figure and to pay a third again for a top that shows it. It's a stain. It doesn't move the tap tone the width of a hair. They pay for it.

You tapped this top with two knuckles when you came in, hard, the way a man knocks on a door, and you laid your ear down against it and nodded like it had told you something. It hadn't. Two knuckles damp it dead; you hang it off one point and hit it once, and I've been telling you that all afternoon. But you nodded, and I let it go, because a man who has already settled what a thing means doesn't tap it to hear it. He taps it to be seen with his ear against the wood.

You asked me at the door for a certificate of provenance. You want the paper that says these acres, this marsh, the drowning, the date. You're four years down the list for the dark preserve out in Nevada, you told me on the drive, you can't see a star off your own deck any more for the strings of satellites you helped put up there, and now you'd like the paper that proves the wood you're buying came off the last of a thing before the last of it went. You have got a real taste for the end of things. It's an expensive taste and you can afford it and I don't hold it against you personally.

But I want you to understand what the paper would be certifying. It would certify that this wood came out of a family fight I can't see the bottom of and a gate that hung open on a flood tide and my father standing on a cane calling live trees cut. It would certify the worst spring of my life. And you'd pay more for the guitar with that paper stapled to the case than without it, and that — that right there, the paper being worth money — that's the whole thing I can't get square, and I'm not going to get it square for you tonight because I haven't got it square for myself in two years of standing at this bench.

Because here is what I actually know, the only thing I'd sign my name under. I know the tap tone. I hold a plate at the node and I tap it and it rings or it doesn't, and that ring is stiffness and mass and the twenty rings to the inch my grandfather's father left standing, and it does not know one thing about the marsh or the gate or my brother's phone. The wood is exactly as good as it was the day before the salt, to the hair. Everything that's changed is the story we've hung on it. And I have watched grown men lay their hand on a dead-common Sitka top and go quiet, and I've watched them tap this and hear nothing and buy it anyway for the paper, and I cannot for the life of me tell you which of us is the fool — the man paying for the story, or me, standing here telling you the story doesn't matter while I build guitars I won't sell.

Nine of them. I built nine out of the good billets and every one of them went, and I priced them fair by the tap and not by the tide and I still cleared more than I have in any three years, which I have not spent, because I don't yet know what that money is.

This one is the tenth. It's the last. There's wood behind it in the rack but not top wood, not heart, and I'm not going to cut the story any thinner than one more guitar. I built it out of the best billet on the property, the one my father set aside years back and never told me why, quartered so clean the flip runs dead even the whole length, twenty-two rings to the inch and not a spot of runout you could measure. I resawed it on the bandsaw into plates a hair over a fifth of an inch, took the two off the center split for the top, and shot the joint by hand on the board until the edges met with no light showing between them and held themselves shut against the window with no glue in them yet — that's the test, if the two halves stay closed against the light on their own they're ready. Jointed them, then thicknessed the top down to two point nine millimeters over the lower bout where the sound lives and thinner out to the rim. Bent the sides over the hot pipe, mahogany off a board I'd had twenty years, and clamped them to the mould to cool overnight. Bracing off the same spruce tree, scalloped and voiced by hand and by tap till the top came alive under my knuckle. Hot hide glue, 192 gram, the pot at a hundred forty-five, and I'll tell you why hide glue when the modern stuff is stronger — because hide glue lets go. Heat it, wet it, and a joint I closed today comes back apart in fifty years for the man who has to fix it. It's the one thing I build that's made to be undone. Everything else on this instrument I mean to hold.

I strung it up three weeks ago. It's done. And when your money came in over the wire this afternoon, the full number, more than the nine before it put together, I sent it back, and you drove out here to stand in my shop and ask me why to my face, and the answer is I don't know that either, and I'm done pretending to you that I do. It's not for sale. Take your case. It's a long drive to the airport and the tide's making on the dyke road, so watch the low spot by the creek or you'll be swimming for your plane.

That night I turned the sign, and I killed the shop lights but the one over the bench, and I wound the tide clock the way he did, first, before anything, and it read the flood coming full on the marsh in the dark outside.

I took the guitar down off the wall hook. I sat on the stool in the sawdust with the one light on it and I tuned it by ear the way he tuned, low string first, and my thumb found the twenty rings under the finish without looking for them.

The top let the note go whole and then held the tail of it long past where the room should have taken it, longer, the way the marsh holds the top of a spring tide a full minute past the flood before it turns for home, and I sat in the dark below the tide and did not stop it.
