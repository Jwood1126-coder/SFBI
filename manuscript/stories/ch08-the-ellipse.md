## 8. The Ellipse

**1,900 km × 210 km**

Dell leveled the tripod on the pad Clay had poured her east of the propane tank, ran the wedge down to the latitude scratched in the aluminum, and had the camera recording before the star cleared the cedars. Eleventh magnitude, a nothing in Capricornus. The rock crossing it that night owned a nine-day arc and no name a person would keep — a string off the prediction list that afternoon, path center two counties north, the error so fat the whole state lay inside the maybe. She timed it standing, red lamp on her chest, the welder's stool too cold in August to sit for long. You time the wide ones. Your chord is the thing that makes them narrow.

The star stepped out at 03:14 and change and came back a tenth of a second later, one clean drop and one clean climb on the light curve, and she read the times off the inserter before she trusted her own eyes to them.

By the second winter the Center had a name for it and a corridor to go with the name, and the corridor had walked off the map's edges and onto a strip you could drive. Off Denver. Off Wichita. Off the turnpike, down the section lines into the short-grass, the three-sigma footprint lying across a run of counties like a swath a combine leaves. Comanche sat under the fat part of it. Dell watched the plot redraw itself on the Center's page across a fall and did not tell her third-hour classes that the county line their fathers farmed ran through the wide end of an error bar.

The Program held its meeting in the school gym under the district banners, folding chairs set out on the free-throw lines, and Alcorn ran the slides while the county sat in its coats and did the thing a county does, which is wait to be told the part it came for. He told it plainly. The corridor was what it was and would only tighten; the money was real and appropriated; the choice stayed theirs. A man from the north townships stood and asked why they didn't just push the rock, and Alcorn read from a folder the sentence Dell already had by heart off the Center's page — that a push does not erase a corridor, it walks the point back up the line through everyone the corridor has already let go — and the man sat down, because there is no arguing with a thing that owns one bad end and a worse one. Dell stood at the back by the trophy case, the softball hardware going green behind the glass, and watched the room do the arithmetic she had done alone on the pad two years before, and did not stand, and did not say that three of the numbers drawing the line across their gym floor were hers.

**340 km × 90 km**

The strangers came up US-160 in ones and twos and then by the busload — buyout men in white sedans with the rental barcodes still gummed in the windshields, a couple with a foundation who wanted to film the last of something, and the ones who set up on the ballfield with a bullhorn and a folding sign about the day and the hour. The co-op sold out of bottled water twice in June. Dell counted eleven tents from the pad one night and went on with her run, because the star did not care how many people had driven a long way to be under it.

*From the sermon of the Rev. Ansel Doerr, Protection Community Church, 3 August 2035 —*

> Jeremiah is shut up in the guardhouse and the Babylonians are already in the field, and the Lord tells him to go buy an acre off his cousin Hanamel. So he buys it. Seventeen shekels of silver, weighed out on the scale, the deed signed and witnessed and the money counted in front of men who can all of them see the smoke rising. And then the part I keep on. Doerr kept the church's baptism book in a fireproof box he carried home to his kitchen every night and back every Sunday, because a building in the corridor was a building and a name in the book was a name. He had baptized Dell's own mother in that church, and when the district let the school go he moved the Wednesday science club down into the church basement so the last two seniors would have a bench to work on, and Dell taught the shadow geometry under the fireproof box on its shelf, the deed of the whole town sitting in its jar three feet over the whiteboard.

Twenty-two minutes was the new distance to a heart attack. Dell wrote that number in no ledger and could not lose it. She started leaving the porch light off so the pad would hold its dark, and Clay started leaving it on at the north gate so the seedling rows would not get run over by a stranger's truck, and the two of them arrived every evening at a house lit exactly where each needed it lit and dark exactly where each needed it dark, and called that agreement, and it was.

The ones on the ballfield stayed through two winters in a cluster of ice-fishing tents and a school bus lettered with the day and the hour, and most of them were gentle — a woman who left jars of okra on the porches, a man who fixed the Wares' abandoned glider where it hung and oiled the chain so it would not squeal for nobody. She did not tell him she had the thing that mattered timed to a tenth of a second, that it threw no sign at all, only a rock keeping its schedule.

**60 km × 18 km**

Households sold. You could read the town by which windows went dark and stayed that way — the Wares first, their porch glider left chained to the rail because a glider will not fit a moving truck and will not fit a new life either; then the two farms east; then the Kliewer place, though Kliewer stayed on the commission and drove in from Coldwater to sit it, cap on indoors, and vote the town's small business through with his hat throwing a shadow on the minutes; and the parsonage, though Doerr held Sunday for the fourteen who still came, driving up from a rented room in Coldwater with the fireproof box belted into the seat beside him.

Nobody asked the Hartsooks straight out why they stayed, because in a town that size you do not ask what you can watch — the porch light, the seedling rows going in another spring, the red lamp moving in Dell's yard past midnight — and reading your neighbors by what they keep up was the whole of the local news. Clay ordered three hundred more redcedar off the form the year the Wares left and heeled them in by the tank, and that was his answer and needed no saying. Dell's answer sat in the ledger, forty-odd nights of a rock she could have looked away from and hadn't.

The offer did not expire. That was the cruelty in it and the mercy both — that the money would wait as long as they would, patient as the rock, so that every morning was a fresh refusal and no morning was ever the last one made for them.

Alcorn drove out himself the once, in a white sedan with the rental barcode still gummed in the glass, and sat at the table where the first offer had lived under the sugar bowl six years. He kept the lanyard on indoors and set a tablet flat on the oilcloth and declined the coffee Clay poured him, which Clay left steaming at his elbow anyway, because you do not un-pour coffee in that kitchen. He had done four hundred of these households up and down the corridor, he said, and had learned to lead with the true thing: the money was theirs, it would keep, nobody would make them go, and the county coordinated departures now for anyone wanting company in it. He had a girl starting kindergarten in Hutchinson. He squared the loose papers on the oilcloth as he talked, tapping their edges even against the table the way a man does whose hands have been told to stay busy and kind, and he did not once say the word for what the corridor was. Clay asked him what he made of cedars — whether a windbreak carried any value on the form, thirty-year stock eight years in. Alcorn looked through the window at the rain notebook on the truck dash and said improvements were valued as found and shelterbelt had no line. Clay nodded as though that settled something and topped off the coffee the man hadn't touched. When Alcorn left he set his card under the sugar bowl on the old letter, and the sedan went back down the section line trailing a quarter mile of dust the wind carried east over the young cedars, and Dell watched it go from the pad while she uncapped the finder for the night, and then quit watching it and got on with the run.

**9 km × 2.6 km**

The fairgrounds Community Building held four card tables end to end and a coffee urn that had done every funeral in the county for thirty years, and by seven the folding chairs were full and the overflow stood along the block wall in their coats because the furnace only ran off one thermostat and it was set for a smaller crowd. Verla set the gray steel box on the middle table, worked the shoelace over her head, and opened the padlock in front of everyone, and the room watched a woman open a box the way a room watches at a graveside, which is to say without a word and without looking away.

The urn came to its boil and somebody filled the church-basement carafes off it and set them down the tables, and nobody drank. There were faces in the folding chairs that had not shared a room since the school closed — the two seniors' mothers, the Betzold uncles down off their quarter, old man Doss who had quit the commission and come to watch the county he'd blade-graded forty years decide whether to keep its roads. Doerr sat in the second row with the fireproof box flat on his knees, the baptism register shut inside it, because he had said he would not leave it in a car on a night like this. Clay held Dell's coat. Verla put her reading glasses down her nose and said, We read every one out loud and we count it twice, and if the power quits we count by the trucks pulled up to the doors with their high beams on, so somebody go tell the trucks. Somebody went and told the trucks.

They ran it clean. Verla read each ballot aloud off the fold and passed it left; Dell called the mark; a Betzold girl home from Hays kept the tally in fives, four down and one across; and a man Dell did not know from the foundation stood as a witness with his hands folded and filmed nothing, because Verla had told him at the door that he could observe or he could leave and he had chosen to observe. Clay sat along the wall with his cap in both hands, the dibble callus on his palm gone shiny, and did not once look at the tally sheet, only at Dell's mouth as it called the marks, the way he read the rain gauge from the truck without getting out.

It went slow because a hand count is slow and because Verla read every one entire, the whole language, so that no vote passed through the room as a checkmark only. Remain open, depart together in spring. Close now. Remain open. Remain. Close. Somewhere in the second hundred the furnace kicked off on its cycle and the room heard the wind come up under the eaves off the Gyp Hills, and nobody moved to check the power, and the count went on by the overhead lights that stayed on, and Dell called the marks in a voice she kept level the way she kept the inserter level, because a called number that shakes is a called number somebody has to call again.

They kept a spoiled pile and a challenged pile and needed neither much; the town marked its ballots the way it filed its taxes, once and hard in pencil, folded once as told. One came through with both boxes filled and a firm line struck back through CLOSE NOW and a thumb of tractor dirt beside the other, and Verla held it up to the overheads and the room allowed the voter's mind was plain, and Dell called it remain and the Betzold girl fived it. One came through blank, folded careful around nothing, and Verla read the nothing aloud the same as the rest — blank ballot, no mark — and passed it left, and nobody said whose it might be, a person who had driven to the fairgrounds in the cold to fold a blank and feed it to the box, which is its own kind of vote and gets counted as none. The stacks grew. Dell's hands squared and banded on their own, fifty and a band, fifty and a band, while her eyes stayed down on the folds, the way her hands could cap a finder in the dark with her eye still at the tube on a fading star.

Four hundred fifty ballots. The town that was left, and the farms in the wide part of it, and the ones who had driven in from Coldwater to vote a place they had already left because a name in a book is a name. The Betzold girl ran her fives twice and got the same total twice. Verla put her reading glasses up on her forehead and read the tally sheet at arm's length and then read it again close, and stood.

"Two hundred thirty-one to remain," she said. "Two hundred nineteen to close."

Nobody cheered, which Dell had known nobody would. A margin of twelve is not a thing you cheer. It is a thing you carry out to the trucks. Over by the doors the two tent people who had stood the whole count in their coats looked at each other and kept their hands still, having driven a long way to be present at a thing and finding it was only arithmetic in the end, four hundred fifty folds of paper and a margin of twelve. A woman near the urn put her hand flat on the table as though the table might go somewhere. Clay turned his cap once around by the brim and put it on.

Then the work, because there is always the work. Dell squared the four hundred fifty ballots into stacks of fifty and banded each stack, and the Betzold girl slid them into the box in order, and Verla laid the poll book on top and threaded a numbered seal through the hasp and pressed it till it clicked and wrote the seal number on the tally sheet in her own hand and slid the sheet across for the counters to sign. Dell signed under her name the way she had signed four hundred nights up the line to the Center — her name at the bottom of a number she had read correctly off a thing she had read correctly, and would read again the same. Verla worked the shoelace back over her head, tucked the sealed box under her arm, and carried it to her car to lock in the trunk for the drive to Coldwater, and the room began the slow business of finding coats.

Dell went out ahead of the coats, through the double doors, into the lot. The wind had laid down. The furnace exhaust ticked behind her as it cooled. Out past the fairgrounds fence the section-line road ran off north pale under no moon, and along it, black against the pale, stood the four rows of cedar Clay had put in on his knees eight springs back, chest-high now, a windbreak a third of the way to the thirty years it was cut for. And over the cedars and the road and the boxed count going down the blacktop to Coldwater, the sky she had spent eleven years measuring stood open to the county, unwashed out here, the true dark, more of it than a town person would credit was left anywhere — Capricornus low in the south where the whole of it had started, the eleventh-magnitude nothing up there somewhere in the field she could no longer pick out by eye, and the thing that had crossed it that first August still up there too, faint, on time, a hundred and forty meters wide, exactly where the arithmetic put it and exactly where she had put the arithmetic. Behind her the doors opened and let the town out into the cold. Clay came and stood at her shoulder and looked where she looked, which was up, and neither of them said the name of the star.
