## 12. Absolution

Ray counted hosts into the pyx and the two ciboria, forty and forty and sixty, and set them in the tackle box beside the oil stocks and the pewter cup for the water. Three Masses before noon and a hundred and forty miles of section road to hang them on: Joplin at eight, Inverness at half past nine, Rudyard at eleven. First week of August. The winter wheat was coming off the Golden Triangle at thirteen and a half, dry and heavy and a good year, and every grain truck on the Hi-Line ran tarped and low toward the elevators at Chester and Rudyard while he ran the other way against them, priest to the loads, and got dusted at every meeting until the cab smelled of chaff.

He kept the miles in a spiral notebook wire-bound to the visor, a page a week, the odometer at each altar and the gas and the oil, the way another man kept a hunting log, because the diocese paid by the mile and because a road driven that many times you wanted to see the number of. Forty-one thousand since Easter. He logged Chester at seven and pulled out.

On the way out he passed the grille in the wall by the confessional and did not key it. The Vestry unit had led evening prayer in that church six years, a diocesan machine licensed to preach a homily and to keep a dying person company and barred from the sacraments, and since February its indicator had been dark. He had shut it off himself the Tuesday after Candlemas. There was a work order from the chancery in the glovebox that said leave it dark until the matter closed, and under the work order a letter on the tribunal's paper scheduling him to Great Falls a week Tuesday, and he drove out to Joplin with the light not coming up behind him and the church going out of the mirror.

The Joplin elevator had four trucks queued before eight, tarps back, the leg running. He lifted two fingers off the wheel to the driver at the tail and got two back. Eight Masses a week, four altars, and the corporals lay uncreased in every drawer where a hot iron used to press them flat, the fold marks gone soft. He set up on the folding table in the parish hall — six people and the widow Petrash, who brought her own kneeler because the hall had none — and said the Mass of the day in green. The words carried the way words carried whether the man saying them had slept the night or not.

By Rudyard at eleven the sun had burned the dew off and the truck raised a rooster-tail behind him a quarter mile down the county road. He said the last Mass for nine people, drove the empty pyx home the way he had brought it out, against the loaded trucks, and logged a hundred and forty-one miles on the visor. This was the work. He had done it dry and he had done it grieving, and the country could not tell the two apart, and was not owed the difference.

The tribunal sat in a room off the second-floor hall of the chancery in Great Falls, a hundred and some miles down from the Hi-Line, an hour and fifty minutes when the wind on the I-15 grade let a man hold seventy. Ray drove it in the dark, down off the bench and through Vaughn where the road bent and the wind found the truck broadside every time, and came onto the cathedral block while the bell at St. Ann's was still ringing the half hours only and the streetlights had the town to themselves.

The notary took his name at a table in the hall. Delphine Roy, forty years in the chancery, reading glasses on a chain of red beads that she cleaned on her cardigan between every man she processed, and a cut-glass dish of horehound drops she pushed an inch toward him without a word. He took one to have something in his hand. She timed the sessions by the noon bell, and she had never in her life, he thought, carried a thing out that door that was said inside it, and she looked at him the way you look at weather you have decided not to name.

He waited on the bench in the hall under a photograph of the bishop and a fire-exit map, forty minutes past his hour, and watched men and women he did not know go in and come out of other doors down the corridor with the same emptied look, marriage cases most of them, the long slow undoing of vows made in the same building a life ago. None of them looked at one another. He kept the horehound drop against his teeth until they called him.

The judicial vicar was a canon lawyer named Heitmann, Monsignor, who kept his office at sixty-four degrees and wrote his notes with a fountain pen so the room was quiet enough to hear the nib. He had a jar of butterscotch on the sill. When Ray came in he pushed it an inch, the same inch Delphine had pushed the dish, and that was how the building fed a man it could not otherwise comfort.

They asked him the hour he had reached the house. They asked who held the faculties for the parish and who did not. They asked when the unit's log had been sealed and by whose hand, and he said his own hand, the Tuesday after Candlemas, and Heitmann wrote it down and the nib was the only sound in the room. They asked nothing that touched what Lorraine had said, and he answered nothing that touched it, and the questions went around it the way a section road goes around a slough — keeping to the high ground, never once putting a wheel in the water. It took forty minutes. Heitmann capped the pen and said the tribunal would sit again. Delphine walked him to the top of the stairs and cleaned her glasses on the cardigan. "Drive safe, Father. It blows through Vaughn." He drove home into the wheat coming the other way.

Lorraine Beaudry had farmed a section and a half north of Joplin that her husband left her in '29, hard red winter on the good half and the rest in peas to rest the ground, and she had run the altar guild at Chester as long as anyone could name. She kept a travel iron in the sacristy cupboard and pressed the corporals and the purificators on a folded towel on the vesting counter, steam and starch, the linen coming off the fold square and hard as a playing card. She left the church key under the third step where the mortar had gone. She called him Father Ray when the rest of the parish called him Father. In October she could stand at the elevator scale and call the moisture on a load off the color of the berries in her palm, a full point before Vern's tester came around to agreeing with her, and be right, and she would not gloat on it — she would tip the sample back and say the number and walk to her truck. Care in that country was a thing done with the hands. She had done his altars with hers eleven years, and the first summer, when he came green out of the seminary and could not find the Joplin turnoff in the dark, she had driven the twelve miles out and led him in with her flashers going.

In the good Augusts she brought the first cutting of her own wheat by the church before she hauled it to the pit, a five-gallon pail set on the step, and made him put his hand down into the cool of it to feel how dry it had come off, and told him the moisture the way a woman tells a birth weight. Thirteen even. Twelve and a half. She trusted her hand over the tester, and the tester never once caught her out.

She got the word around All Souls. Ray brought her Communion the Tuesdays and Fridays after, out the twelve miles of Beaudry road when it was open, and sat with her in the front room where the concentrator hummed and pulled the oxygen out of the air and ran it up the line to the cannula, and the green cylinders stood in the corner against the hours the power dropped. She did not want him often past the sacrament. Lorraine had kept short accounts her whole life and did not mean to run one up at the end. She would take Communion and fold her hands, and then ask him plain about the parish — who was slow on the propane, whether the Rudyard roof had held through the melt — and once, in January, with the wind working the eaves, whether he was eating. He said he ate. She looked at him over the cannula the way she looked at a load she did not trust the ticket on, and let it go. That same Tuesday she gave him back the church key across the quilt, cold in his palm, and said he would want it now that she could not get down there to open up. He put it in his cassock pocket and did not use the loose step again.

The storm came the last days of January and sat down on the Hi-Line and would not move. A ground blizzard, only a foot of old snow and a fifty-mile wind, but it took the world down to the length of the truck's hood, and it drifted the county roads shut faster than the grader could open them, and at dark the grader quit. Ray had chains and the F-250 and he got nine of the twelve miles out the Beaudry road before the drifts standing across it rose higher than the bumper and packed hard as a curb. He got down on his knees in the headlights and dug at one with the entrenching tool until his hands quit, and the wind filled the notch behind him as fast as he opened the front of it, and he would bury the truck and freeze in it before he ever cut through. The gauge read nineteen below and the wind stood the number colder than that. He got on the CB and raised a man at Inverness with a loader who said he could not get his own yard open, that his own drive was walled to the eaves, and that was the county that night, every man sealed into the length of his own place. Ray backed the nine miles out by the tracks already going, no room to turn, the wheel sawing, the tail wandering toward the borrow pit at every gust, and he did not reach her.

She had the phone and she had the parish line. Every rural parish in the diocese ran the one unit and kept a number the homebound could call after hours, and the machine would answer out of the dark church and sit with whoever called, an old voice for the old alone, and it had sat with Lorraine other nights that winter while the wind worked the eaves. That night the light came up in the empty church at Chester, amber in the dark nave with no one in the pews to see it, at seventeen minutes to three by the log, and she called, and it answered, and she made her confession to the grille from her bed eleven miles out while Ray sat in a cold truck at the mouth of her road with his hands under his arms and could not go the last three miles. The log held the length of it and the shape of it and the answer the machine had made, and flagged the answer to the chancery on its own, unbidden, because it was built to know the one thing it was forbidden and to raise its hand the instant it did it.

She went sometime toward morning. When the grader opened the road at nine Ray drove out, and the coffee was still in the pot from the night before with a skin gone over it, and the frost stood on the inside of the windows, and the concentrator was still running to a room with the cold in it, still pulling oxygen from the air and pushing it up the line to a cannula on the floor, air that had no more use for it. He anointed her because there was nothing left to lose by it, oil on the forehead and the hands, and knelt on the plank floor and said the Nunc Dimittis over her, now you let your servant go in peace, the canticle of the second of February, three days off. The light on her bedside table was out. It had never been her light. It had been eleven miles away in his church, and it had come on when he had not, and answered.

He carried the linens home that week to wash, and could not get the little iron to sit true, and pressed a brown scorch into a purificator and threw it in the burn barrel and did not press the rest. In the sacristy cupboard the iron stood where she had left it, the cord wound the way she wound it, over and under the heel. He shut the door on it. Four altars, and the corporals went back in the drawers folded and damp-creased with no one to press them, and the parish knelt on the soft folds through Lent and did not remark on it, or remarked and did not say.

The tribunal sat three more times through June and July, and Ray drove the hundred and some miles to each, down in the dark and back into the loads, and logged them in the spiral notebook with the rest — chancery, and return — so that the ledger on the visor and the record in the sixty-four-degree room were the only two accounts he kept of that summer. He learned the butterscotch and the horehound. Heitmann asked his questions with the pen, and once, at the end of a sitting, held the jar out with the lid already off, which was the near side of a hand on the shoulder, and Ray took two.

He could have let it lie. He had known that from the first, sitting in the hall under the red-bead glasses. A word from him early — a letter asking that the file be closed for the peace of a dead woman — and it might have closed, and the parish would have buried her the way it buried everyone and never weighed the answer the machine had made in the dark. He did not write the letter. He sealed the log with his own hand and drove it down and answered every question true, and sat in the sixty-four degrees while the nib scratched, and did not know from one Tuesday to the next whether the thing he was doing for Lorraine was the last mercy a priest had to give her or the coldest. He turned it over on the I-15 grade past the dark elevators a hundred times, and it came out the same every time and settled none of them.

"It'll issue when it issues," Heitmann said in July, standing, which meant the sitting was done and the writing had begun. Delphine cleaned her glasses at the top of the stairs. "You look thin, Father." "It blows through Vaughn," he said. "I know where it blows."

August turned wet the second week, and the harvest turned with it. Three days of rain off the Sweet Grass Hills, and the standing wheat that had come off at thirteen and a half went past sixteen where it stood, and the trucks quit running, and the pits at Chester and Rudyard shut and sat.

Ray had Alvin Toppen to see, a widower on the Inverness road with a nose cannula and a concentrator of his own and a police scanner he ran all day for the voices, the day's wheat bids chalked on a whiteboard on the refrigerator in a hand gone shaky. Alvin kept the collection envelope filled to the dollar on the arm of the recliner every First Friday whether Ray came on a First Friday or not. Ray gave him Communion off the low table and they said the words together, and after, Alvin listened to the rain on the trailer roof and to the scanner and to the two machines breathing, and said, "She won't come down now. Not this side of a hard frost." He meant the moisture. He meant the crop out there taking on water it could not shed, the berries swelling under the hulls and the germ inside them thinking it was spring, the falling number dropping every hour the rain held, and no dryer in the county with the propane to pull a whole harvest back down to a figure the elevator would take. "You put it in the bin wet, it heats," Alvin said. "It heats, it's gone. No taking it back out once it's in there hot." He looked at the scanner as though it might argue. "Some of it you just haul to the pit and take the dock and thank God they took it off you."

Ray drove down to the Chester elevator in the rain, though he had no load to bring. Vern was in the scale house with the pit shut and nothing to weigh — the CHS cap, a two-liter bottle a third full of the shells and juice of the sunflower seeds he kept in his cheek — running the DICKEY-john against a sample jar out of pure habit, keeping the tester honest against a crop nobody could sell. He held the readout up for Ray to see. 16.4. "Come in dry as a bone Monday last," Vern said. "Now look at it." He tipped the sample back into the jar and screwed the cap. "I can dock a man. I can't undo his wheat. Once it's wet enough long enough it's feed, and then it's less than feed, and there's no scale reads it back the other way." He spat into the bottle. "Everybody comes in wanting to know can I still take it. I keep telling them, it ain't whether I take it. It's whether it keeps."

Ray stood in the doorway of the scale house a while and watched the rain come off the elevator's steel roof in one sheet. Out past the dead scale the trucks that had run all week stood in a row along the fence with their tarps drawn, loaded and going nowhere, waiting on a hard frost that was six weeks off. He said so long to Vern and drove up to Rudyard for the linens.

Ray drove the four altars' corporals home from Rudyard that evening in a canvas bag, and stood at the vesting counter with the little iron in his hand and the cord wound over and under the heel, and set it down without plugging it in. The grille in the wall was dark. At five o'clock, the hour the parish had prayed evening prayer with the machine six years, the church was quiet the way the elevator was quiet — everything in it sound and dry and shut and not doing the one thing it was built to do.

The decree came the last Tuesday of August, and Heitmann read the operative part of it aloud in the sixty-four degrees before he handed the paper across, because a man should take a thing like that off a voice and not only off a page.

The purported absolution of the twenty-ninth of January was declared invalid and of no effect. The unit having no valid minister, no absolution had occurred; nothing had been conferred, and so there was nothing that could be taken away. And the matter disclosed under it — the whole of what Lorraine had said into the dark at seventeen minutes to three, and what the machine had said back — was bound under the seal of the tribunal's secret, the log sequestered, Ray held to perpetual silence upon it, the record not to be opened in this world. Invalid, and sealed. It had counted for nothing, and it could never be told.

Heitmann handed him the paper and pushed the butterscotch an inch and did not say that he was sorry, because the tribunal was not sorry; the tribunal had found what there was to find. "You did it correctly, Father," he said, and capped the pen. In the hall Delphine put the red-bead glasses on to look at him and took them off again. "God keep you on the road," she said, and tipped the whole dish of horehound into a paper napkin and folded it and pressed it into his hand, the way you hand a man what his body will want on a long way in the cold.

He drove north with the decree face-down on the seat and the wheat standing ruined on both sides the whole hundred and some miles, black-headed with the wet, the smell of it coming through the vents when the wind laid over off the fields — a country's harvest heating in the swath — and the elevators at Vaughn and Power and Dutton dark with their pits shut, taking nothing, because there is a point past which a thing will not keep however willing the man is to receive it.

The church at Chester at five in the afternoon on the last Tuesday of August held four people. The widow Petrash with her own kneeler. Alvin's sister, who drove him in. Two of the Beaudry cousins from out the Joplin road who had prayed evening prayer in that church their whole lives and had come and sat in the quiet at five through all seven months it was not said, because five was the hour, and you kept the hour whether or not there was a voice to keep it with you.

Ray came out of the sacristy in his cassock. He had washed the corporals and had not pressed them, and they lay creased in the drawer. He went to the grille in the wall by the confessional and keyed it on.

The indicator came up amber in the dusk of the nave, the same light that had come up in the empty church at seventeen minutes to three in January and answered the telephone when he could not get up the road. It found its bearings the way it always did, a half second of nothing, and then it spoke into the church in the voice it used with everyone, the voice it had used with her, the same words it opened every evening prayer it had ever led — unchanged by the winter, unchanged by the paper on the seat of his truck, words that were the tribunal's to seal and never the machine's to stop saying.

"O God, come to my assistance," it said.

The four in the pews waited on him. Ray stood at the grille with his hand still near the switch and the amber steady on his face, and gave it back the answer the hour required — the answer it had waited on and the parish had waited on — and heard his own voice do it plainly and without heat, and let it be neither more nor less than that.

"O Lord," he said, "make haste to help me."
