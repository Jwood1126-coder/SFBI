## 3. Fluent

*2031*

Aoi fired the boiler at two so the water would stand at forty-three by four, and she banked it the way her father had shown her the winter she turned nine, which was her daughter Mio's age now — the flue open a hand's width, the oil valve cracked a quarter, the pilot held to the burner until the low roar caught and came up through the concrete into the soles of her feet. The kama, the oil-fired boiler, had heated this water since 1968. It fed the deep bath and the jet bath and the electric bath on the men's side, and the family's own small tub off the same line, and the back room where her mother-in-law lived, and it drank a barrel of heavy oil a week that Aoi could no longer quite meet, and it was the loudest thing she loved.

She scrubbed the men's side first, because the men came straight off shift and would not wait, and then the women's. Forty years of bodies had worn the blue tiles pale in a track from the door to the deep bath. The mosaic on the far wall was a mountain in cut tile, snow at the peak in white squares and pines below it in three greens, and it lost tiles off the left slope faster than she could match them, so the mountain came apart a little every year, regrouted, the new white a shade too blue against the old. She rinsed the yellow basins under the tap and stacked them dripping, the light Kansai ones, thirty-eight of them now, two cracked in November. She set the stools in their rows. She knelt at the lip of the deep bath and put her wrist in past the bone, because the float thermometer lied by half a degree in the cold and her wrist never had.

By three the room had begun to breathe. The tile took the heat and gave it back, the high windows went to milk with steam, and the empty hall carried the smallest sound the length of itself — the drip off a tap, the tick of the pipes finding their temperature, her own knees on the wet stone. A bathhouse before it opens is the quietest loud room there is. She had grown up in that one held breath, between the water gone hot and the door not yet open, and she stood in it a moment, alone, before the ward came in and filled it with themselves.

Through the wall the radio came on. English, a man and a woman talking easy, and then a swell of strings — the station her mother-in-law had found that played the songs of the country she'd left, that she kept a hair too loud since her hearing went. Under it rode the old woman's voice, saying something to the room or to the radio, the shapes of English that Aoi had lived inside for eleven years and had never once been able to open, the way you can live your whole life beside water and never think in it.

Mr. Ueno came at four, first through the door as he had been first through the door since before Aoi's father died. He would not touch the yellow basins. He brought his own, hinoki — cypress — gone thin and pale as a fingernail, and he made straight for the electric bath and sat in the low buzz of it until his shoulders went the color of the inside of a shell, though his heart was bad and his daughter had told him and told him. He paid with the coins he kept in a flat tobacco tin. Aoi took the coins warm from his hand and gave him back the little bow that was the whole of their talk, and turned the wooden sign, and the day was open.

David, her husband, came home from the brewery at seven with the cold still on his coat and the smell of it in his hair, steamed rice and something roasted underneath, the roasted-chestnut smell of the koji room that never quite washed out of him from November to March. His hands were split at the knuckles from washing rice in water just off ice, the way the toji, the master brewer, made them wash it, by hand, by count, and he had stopped, years ago, minding that it hurt. He set a small unlabeled bottle on the shelf by the door, the toji's cloudy first-press that he was let carry home a cup of, and he said, in his Japanese, that the moromi was singing.

He meant that it had begun to work, the tanks gone loud with their own small weather. He had built the sentence wrong the first winter and never fixed it, so that what he said, each time, was closer to *the mash is a song,* and Aoi had stopped correcting it around the same winter he stopped minding his hands, because a man who has learned an entire craft with his shoulders and his nose and no words to speak of does not need his wife to sand his grammar. He had walked into that brewery eleven years ago knowing how to make beer in Oregon and not one useful thing else, and the toji had taught him the koji the way you teach a dog to the water, by putting his hands in it, and now he could tell by the smell coming off a tank at four in the morning whether the temperature had run, and he could not order lunch without Mio.

They had met at this counter. He had come in his second Osaka winter red to the ears and not knowing the men's door from the women's, a foreigner off the brewery line who'd been told there was a bath in the ward that still burned oil, and he had stood dripping at the shoe cupboard holding a coin out on his flat palm like a child. She took the wrong coin and gave him the right basin and pointed, and that was the courtship, more or less — a year of the wrong coin and the right basin, a man who could name the temperature of steamed rice to the degree and not one thing he felt, learning her as he'd learned the koji, by standing near it until his hands knew. Her father had died that spring and left her the boiler and the barrel of oil and the mountain coming apart on the wall, and David had married into the whole of it without, she sometimes thought, any clear idea of what he'd said yes to, because no one had ever been able to tell him, in words, exactly what it was.

Aoi ran the counter through the supper hour. She sold Mrs. Doi a bottle of coffee-milk from the cold case and watched her drink it standing with one hand on her hip and her towel turbaned on her head, the way everyone drank it, the way it was meant to be drunk.

The women's side filled by seven. In a room of wet tile with no soft thing in it, every sound went up and came back doubled — a basin knocked down, a palmful of water thrown, a grandmother telling a granddaughter to count to a hundred before she climbed out of the hot bath, the child going up the numbers loud and flat and true. Mrs. Kado kept her soap in a tin box with a mermaid on the lid worn to a ghost, and she washed the same shoulder forty strokes and the other twenty, and had for as long as Aoi could remember. Voices came over the partition from the men's side, where the wall stopped short of the ceiling, so that the whole place was one room with a wall in it that pretended otherwise, and you learned, growing up inside it, to know a person entire by the sounds they made on the far side of a thing you could not see them through.

She skimmed the deep bath and adjusted the valve a quarter when the four-o'clock crowd thinned. And she carried the tray back to the sliding door of her mother-in-law's room, the small pot and the one cup, and set it on the mat at the threshold, and did not slide the door, because eleven years had taught her the exact distance at which the old woman would accept a thing.

The voice came through. Aoi caught *bath* in it, and *him,* and the flat cold that had been in it since the woman arrived four winters ago, off a plane she swore she'd never take, after David's father died and there was no one left to keep her and no chance on earth she would learn a syllable of the place she'd been dragged to. Aoi caught the tone and set down the pot and heard, before she'd straightened, the ring rap twice against the lacquer, the woman's wedding band, which meant the tea had come too cool again, and the tin of horehound drops shifted and rattled on the sill.

Mio was on the third stair with her notebook, the marbled one, two columns down every page, Japanese in the left and the round English in the right, and she came when the ring rapped because she had come to that sound since the winter the woman arrived. She stood at the door a moment with her head tipped, and Aoi watched her daughter's throat move once, the small swallow she did before she carried anything across, and then Mio turned and said, in Japanese, that Grandma asked would it snow, and that she'd like the tea hotter next time, please.

"Tell her by the weekend," Aoi said, though the woman had not asked about snow, Aoi knew now and had half-known for years, the way you know the deep bath is a degree low before the float admits it. She reheated the pot. Mio carried it back and stood and did her small swallow and rendered whatever came through the door into three kind Japanese words, and went back to her stair, and this was the machinery of the house, oiled so long that no one in it could any longer hear it run.

There were nights the rapping came in the middle of Mio's homework and she went with the pencil still in her hand, and nights it came at the good part of a show and she went, and she had never once been asked whether she wanted the carrying or only been born into the middle of it, the one door in the house that opened both ways. She was nine and had three grammars in her mouth — her mother's, her father's, and the third one she'd built herself, the soft one, the one where the old woman asked about snow. Aoi had watched that third grammar go up since the girl was four, and had been glad of it, and had helped to lay the block, some nights at the sink, setting the kind version of the day over the true one for the child's own good, so that Mio grew up ferrying a house whose grown people were quietly ferrying it too, everyone downstream of everyone, no one at the spring.

David tried a joke at the table. He had a joke most nights, built the way he built everything, out of parts that didn't quite fit, and it died the way most of them died, and Aoi laughed anyway at the shape of him trying, at his split hands going up to help the words along, and Mio laughed the real laugh, and David looked from one of them to the other pleased and uncertain, a man who has heard applause through a wall and can't tell which side of the joke it's for. Later, at the sink, Aoi said to Mio, low, in the voice she kept for the two of them, that Grandma was only old and a long way from home and they would be patient with her, and Mio nodded over the plates the way she nodded at everything Aoi asked of her, and dried, and stacked, and the radio played its old strings through the wall.

Their anniversary fell in the coldest week, the way it always had, eleven years of Januarys. Aoi closed early. She let the last of the water down the channels and swabbed the drains and killed the boiler, and the great room ticked and settled as the tiles gave their heat back to the dark. She ran the family's small tub instead, and while it filled she took David's hands across the low table and worked the cracked knuckles with the yellow salve from the pharmacy, thumb by thumb, the way she did every winter, because he would not do it himself and would let them split to the bone.

He watched her do it. He always watched her do it, and he never had the words for it and had stopped reaching for them, and the not-reaching was its own kind of speech she had learned to read as surely as the wrist reads the water. When she finished he turned his cup in his salved hands and lifted it, and said the thing he said, the six wrong syllables he had said every anniversary since the first, that he had built badly in a language he did not have and set like a joint that dries crooked and holds anyway.

Mio, at the table in her pajamas, allowed to stay up, said it for him the way she had said it for years, the family's version, worn smooth: that Papa says you are the warm water of this house. And they laughed, all three, and Aoi squeezed his ruined hands, and the tub ran full behind the wall with a sound like rain coming closer, and the old woman's radio went on playing to the back room, and it was, Aoi thought, holding her husband's hands on the coldest night of the year in the smell of steam and menthol salve, enough. It was a good marriage. It had never once in eleven years been able to say why.

Mio brought the buds home on a Thursday, in a flat white box with the ward office's mark on it, and a folded sheet Aoi could not read but understood from the picture on it — a family at a table, lines of little arrows going between their heads. The buds were translators. You wore one, and any voice that reached you came back a half-beat later in your own language. There had been such things for years — in phones, in the kiosks at the station, in the ears of the tourists who came through the baths on festival days — and a pair had never crossed this threshold. Nobody had decided that. It was only that the house had its interpreter, and the family had stopped being able to imagine the table without her in the middle of it. The school had given a pair to every child, and to the households where the paper said the home language and the school language did not match, they had given the family set. Mio set them out on the table at supper like a girl laying places. She was pleased. She showed them how it went, the small bead of it warmed in the ear until it stopped feeling like anything, no more than a grain of rice, she said, and she looked at her mother and her father and she said the thing that broke it open, that now they could just talk, the two of them, straight across, and she wouldn't have to.

David put his in because Mio wanted him to. Aoi put hers in because David had. The old woman's radio was going in the back room, and the strings came through the wall the way they came through every night, and then a voice laid itself over the strings inside Aoi's ear, level and clear and idiomatic, in Aoi's own language, close as a thought — *he could have had anyone he wanted and he brought home the girl who runs the baths. Listen to her out there. Sloshing.*

The pot was in Aoi's hand. She had risen for the tea without deciding to, the ring not yet rapped, and she stood with the pot and the clear kind terrible voice in her ear finishing the sentence it had been saying for four winters through a wall, and she did not move.

Across the table David had gone the color of the inside of a shell. He was looking at Aoi and his face was doing something she had watched him try to make it do for eleven years and never manage, and she understood that the bead in his ear had just handed him something too, out of the air of the house, some small Japanese thing she had let fall a thousand times toward Mio and never toward him — that they would be patient with the old woman, that she was only far from home; the mercy Aoi had been spending in the dark for years on the woman who despised her, laid now in David's own ear in words he could finally hold.

And Mio, who had forgotten the beads were in, who had been rendering the house for so long that her body did it before her mind could stop it, heard her grandmother's voice come through the wall and did her small swallow, and turned, and said to her mother, in Japanese, gently, that Grandma said the water sounded nice tonight.

The bead had already said the true thing. Both of them had already heard it, whole. And Mio saw it in their faces, the way a child sees the thing an instant before she can name it — that the wall had come down, that the soft Japanese she'd just handed her mother was lying naked beside the truth it had been built to cover — the marriage's eleven years of it, and every kind lie she herself had made since she was four to hold the table together — all of it lit at once. Her hand went up to her own throat. She took the bead out of her ear and set it on the table very carefully, as if it might still be listening, and she did not cry, and she did not explain, and she went up the stairs, the third one, the landing, and her door.

David was still holding his cup. In the wreck of the table he did the only thing the table had ever taught him, which was to lift it, and say his six wrong syllables, his anniversary sentence, the warm water of this house — and then, for the first time in eleven years, he kept going, in English, low and fast, his own whole tongue at last, and the bead in Aoi's ear, still in, still faithful, laid it down clean and level and eleven years too late: *I'm sorry. I brought you into a cold house. She will never be kind to you and I can't make her and I'm sorry.*

Aoi took the bead out of her ear. She set it beside her daughter's on the table, the two of them small and yellow-white under the light, grains of rice, and she went and turned off the tub before it ran over the lip, and she stood in the steam of it with her back to the room until she could breathe in the wet air the way you breathe in the wet air, and behind her the radio kept playing its old songs to the back room, in a language she was grateful, standing there, that she still could not understand.

Mio did not translate again. Not the next morning, not that week, not the sharp thing the old woman sent through the wall on Sunday nor the blunt one David tried to send back. She went to school and came home and sat on the third stair with the marbled notebook, and when the ring rapped against the lacquer she looked up and looked at it and looked back down, and let it rap. Aoi carried the tray herself and set it at the threshold and stood in the cold English coming through the door and understood, now, every fourth or fifth word of it the way you understand a thing you've been given the key to and can never give back, and she reheated the tea when the ring rapped, and she did not ask her daughter to carry anything across for anyone ever again.

The buds went into their white box and the box went on the shelf over the shoe cupboard, where the winter gloves went, and Aoi did not put hers in again and did not see David put his in, and the household ran, after a fashion, the way the boiler ran when a jet clogged — hot enough, wrong somewhere, carrying its load with a knock in it you learned to stop hearing.

She kept the woman's bath at forty-two, a degree cool of the men's, the way the old woman liked it and had never once said thank you for, and she filled the pill organizer David marked in English on the lid, the seven shapes of the days that Aoi read as shapes, and she laundered the things that came out of the back room and folded them and set them at the threshold and did not slide the door. She did the maintenance. She had always done the maintenance. The difference was only that she now knew what she was maintaining, and knowing did not stop her hands, which had never needed to know a thing to do it right.

David stopped trying the joke at supper. For a week, two weeks, the table ran silent but for Mio's fork, and Aoi let it, because there was nothing the beads had said that a word from her could unsay, and because she had run enough water through enough pipe to know you do not fix a knock by wanting it gone.

Then one night she took the white box down off the shelf, over the gloves, and she carried it out to the alley where the empty oil barrels stood, and she did not throw it away, because it was the ward's and Mio would have to answer for it, but she set it down out there in the cold by the barrels where no hand would reach for it by habit, and she came back in and washed the smell of oil off her hands at the deep sink, and that was the whole of what she decided, and she said none of it to anyone.

The moromi came in near the end of February, and David came home smelling of banana and melon under the rice, the sweet high smell the good yeast throws when it's happy, and Aoi could tell from the door that it had gone well, that whatever the tanks had done in the dark they had done right.

She was in the deep bath when he came, after close, the sign turned and the last basin stacked, her sleeves pushed up and her arms in past the elbow, running her hands along the tile at the waterline for the grout that had gone soft, the slow yearly work of keeping the mountain from coming apart faster than she could hold it. The water stood at forty-three. It moved when she moved and lay still when she was still and found its own flat level against her arms without being told, the way it had for her father and would for whoever came after her. The boiler was off and ticking as the iron gave its heat back to the dark, and steam came up off the surface and hung in the high cold air of the emptied room and beaded on the tile mountain and ran back down it, slow, the mountain's own weather.

David stood at the lip in his coat with the cold still on it. He did not have the beads. She knew it from how he stood, from the long breath he took before he tried. He could have put the bead back in his ear. He could have stood in that doorway and said the whole of it in his own tongue, where he had every word he needed, the esters and the temperatures and the sorry, and let the thing in her ear hand it across to her clean and level and right. The white box was out by the barrels in the cold where she had set it down. He had left it there. And he reached instead for the six syllables he had never once gotten right, and got them down the same wrong way he'd gotten them down for eleven years, laboring, his split hands coming up to help — and this time neither of them laughed, and neither of them said the family's easy version of what they meant, and Aoi kept her arms in the water and her eyes on the tile and let him get to the end of it, all of it, in his own bad mouth, no voice laid over his but his.

Upstairs a door was open, and down through the boards came Mio's voice, careless and loud, singing along to something on the little speaker, dropping a Japanese word into the middle of an English line and an English word into the middle of a Japanese one and never once stopping to trade one for the other, singing across the seam in a language that was only hers, for no one, carrying nothing across for anybody. Aoi held her hands in the warm water and did not turn around.
