## 7. Deorbit

*2039*

The morning's job was the potable line in the node, and Yuki bled it down into a contingency bag at forty milliliters a pull, reading the pressure off the gauge between pulls and calling the numbers to the floor in Houston. When the line ran dry she capped the quick disconnect, safed the pump, and marked the step complete on the laptop. That was the fourteenth line she had drained since the crew went home and left her alone aboard the station. Six more, then the racks, then the lights.

The tug — the deorbit vehicle sent up to bring the station down — had mated at the aft port the week before she started the closeout. It had come up empty and drunk propellant from the ground ever since, its tanks filling for the burns that would come after she was gone — apogee first, the orbit walked down over three weeks, then the last shove over the South Pacific to put what was left of Meridian in the water at Point Nemo, where they put these things. She had watched it dock through the cupola. She had not gone back to the cupola.

```
GMT 128 / 12:41
HOUSTON   Meridian, Houston, on Space-to-Ground two.
YUKI      Go ahead, Houston.
HOUSTON   Morning, Commander. Dale Marsh has you till eighteen hundred. We copied all fourteen loop drains overnight — that's ahead of the line. Good work up there.
YUKI      I'll give it back on the racks. I always do.
HOUSTON   Understood. Seas are two feet off Tampa, wind northeast at six. The Megan's on station and it's flat as a parking lot down here. In case you were keeping track.
YUKI      You're the one who keeps track, Dale.
HOUSTON   Somebody's got to. We've got you loud and clear, by the way. You've got a lot of ears on the loop this week — whole world can hear you say good morning.
YUKI      Then good morning, world.
HOUSTON   Copy that. When you're ready we'd like to start the PPK reconciliation, block four in the timeline. No rush on it.
YUKI      Give me twenty. I've got a bag to close.
```

Meridian smelled the way it always had, of hot electronics and the inside of a canteen and the staleness of air that had been through people and scrubbers for eleven years. She had stopped smelling it around day forty of her first flight and would smell it again the second she was down, when the hatch cracked and Florida came in — cut grass and salt and diesel off the Megan — and she would carry the station out in her suit and her hair for a week and then lose it. Nobody told you that part. The first thing you lose when you come home is how home stopped smelling like anything.

She did the second potable line before lunch and the third after, and between them she safed the coolant loop in the lab, closing the manual valves in the order the card gave them, capping the quick disconnects, calling each cap down so the ground could watch a number settle on their screen and agree with her that it had settled. The work was not hard. The work had never been hard. The work was a list, and you did the list, and the list did not care how you felt about the items on it, and that was the mercy of it and always had been. On a bad flight, the summer the fires took the whole coast into a haze you could see the shape of from up here, she had done the list. The list was how you got from one end of a day to the other with your hands busy and your name on every step of it.

```
GMT 128 / 14:02
HOUSTON   Meridian, Houston. We watched the lab loop go safe, that's a clean config. Onyx says you're go to pull the two EXPRESS racks whenever you get to them.
YUKI      Copy, go for the racks. I'll get the barcodes off them first — some of that's going down.
HOUSTON   Understood. And Yuki — no rush on any of it. You've got nine days and a list built for twelve. Somebody padded it on purpose.
YUKI      Somebody always does. It's the one kind thing a timeline knows how to do.
HOUSTON   Copy that.
```

She emptied her personal kit against the manifest, item by item, holding each thing to the barcode reader and letting the inventory system say its weight and its name. The reader knew the razor and the second razor. It knew the elastics, the good pen, the three foil packets of miso she had hoarded and would now carry down uneaten. Voss, from an earlier crew, had left his multitool velcroed inside the aft panel — the one with the bottle opener worn bright — because he said it belonged to the station now and the station could keep it. She left it where it was. It was not on any manifest and the tug would have it in three weeks and there was a rightness to that she did not examine.

The last thing in the kit had no barcode. A gray anodized cylinder the size of a film can, thirty-one grams, no part number and no lot. She entered it by hand under her own kit, the way you entered anything the machine could not read, and the system asked her for a description, and she left the field blank and moved it to the return column and closed the record. Then she opened the record again and moved it back to disposal and closed it again, and floated to the galley to start the coffee, because block four could wait twenty minutes and so could she.

···

By day three the station had gone quieter in a way you heard with your skin. She had powered down the second lab rack that morning, thrown the breaker at the RPCM — the power unit that fed the rack — and watched the panel go dark, and a fan somewhere upstream had wound down with it, and now the air in the lab did not move on its own. It hung. She pushed through it like water. The scrubber in the core still ran and would run until the last day, and near it the air was still Meridian, but the lab had started to smell of nothing, which is what a room smells like when it has decided you are already gone.

```
GMT 130 / 02:14
HOUSTON   Meridian, Houston, Priya Menon with you for the night. Loud and clear?
YUKI      Five by five, Priya. You drew the graveyard again.
HOUSTON   I like the graveyard. Nobody's on the loop, it's just you and me and the recorders. You're coming up on the Tasman right now, by the way. Clear all the way to the coast — you could count the lights on the North Island if you had the window.
YUKI      I've got the window closed on this side. I'm in the lab.
HOUSTON   That's a first. You usually make me tell you what's underneath you.
YUKI      Tell me anyway. I like hearing you find it.
HOUSTON   Okay. You've got the whole bottom of the world coming. New Zealand, then nothing, then ice. Your ground track tonight doesn't cross a single city till you're most of the way to Chile.
YUKI      Good. Chile can wait.
HOUSTON   You want me to keep going? I've got the whole Pacific after that.
YUKI      Keep going. I'll steep the night out of you.
HOUSTON   There's a squid fleet works off Chile some weeks, couple hundred boats pulling them up under lamps. From up there it reads like a city that got up and walked out to sea. You're too far south to catch it tonight. But it's down there.
YUKI      It's down there. That's usually enough. That a thing's down there whether or not I can see it.
HOUSTON   Copy that, Commander.
```

The core scrubber threw a caution at 03:40, a nuisance flag it had thrown for a year, and she cleared it and logged it and did not fix it, because you do not fix a thing you are about to drop in the ocean. That was the strange grammar of the last weeks. Half of maintenance is the belief that the machine has a tomorrow. She had kept Meridian eleven years on that belief, torqued its bolts and swapped its filters and talked to it the way you talk to anything you mean to keep, and now she was withdrawing the belief a module at a time, on a schedule, with her own hands.

She had built the return manifest twice and both times it came out the same weight and the same wrongness. Everything that flew home flew home for a reason the ground could defend — the drives, the samples in the cold bag, the two failed pumps engineering wanted to cut open on a clean table. Everything else stayed and burned. That was the whole physics of it. What you carried down had to earn its mass against a splashdown, and what it could not earn, the tug took to Point Nemo, and the ocean there was four kilometers deep over a plain nobody would ever stand on.

In the galley she found the tea. A tin of hojicha, roasted so dark it smelled of woodsmoke, packed into her kit by a hand that was not hers and stowed behind the drink bags eleven months ago and never opened, because up here you drank what came in the pouches and there was no still water to steep anything in and she had known that and packed it anyway. She turned it once. She velcroed it to the galley rail with the label out and marked it disposal in the system and did not steep it, because there was still no still water, and because the tin weighed a hundred and ten grams, and because if she was honest the tea was not hers to drink. She had a private conference with Denver, her daughter, at 04:00, and she wanted to be doing something with her hands when it came.

```
GMT 130 / 04:52
YUKI      Houston, Meridian. One for the log, while it's just us.
HOUSTON   Go ahead, Commander.
YUKI      I haven't decided I'm coming down.
HOUSTON   ...Copy that, Meridian.
YUKI      I'm not asking for anything. Keep the timeline where it is. I just won't have it said after that I didn't say it.
HOUSTON   Understood. It's on the loop and it's on the log. You've got me another forty minutes, and then it's Marsh, and nothing's different till you say different.
YUKI      Nothing's different. Thank you, Priya.
HOUSTON   We're here, Yuki.
```

···

Day five ran long because she let it. The plan called for the core avionics and half the stowage in the same shift, and she took the stowage slow, one cargo bag at a time, floating each item to the reader and letting the system name it and deciding, and the deciding was the part that took the hours. Return or disposal. Down or gone. Two hundred and forty items in the aft node alone, and every one of them a small verdict she signed with a keystroke, the record breathing shut behind her the way records do.

The lab drives went in the cold bag. The urine processor spares went to disposal — nobody wanted them down, and the tug was welcome to them. A crew of six had lived here two years and left the ordinary silt of people behind: a sleep mask, a deck of cards short the three of clubs, a paperback swollen fat with humidity and read to the spine. Down or gone. She sent the cards down because a deck short a card was somebody's, and the mask to disposal, and she held the paperback a while and then let it burn, and could not have said why one and not the other except that the hand has reasons the manifest does not.

The tools were the easy part. Tools have owners and homes and a rule someone wrote before you were born. The soft goods were the hard part — the fabric six people leave behind after two years in a can, the odd socks, the birthday bunting a crew had taped up and never taken down, a child's drawing laminated and velcroed inside a locker by a father three rotations gone. She sent the drawing down. She had a rule for the drawing that she did not have for the tea.

```
YUKI      Tell Surgeon I ate standing up with my hands full. It counted.
HOUSTON   She says it counts if you log it.
YUKI      It counts if I say it counts. Up here I'm the last word on lunch.
HOUSTON   Can't argue that. Logging it for you — chicken and rice, we'll call it.
YUKI      Call it shrimp. If you're going to invent my lunch, Dale, invent me something good.
HOUSTON   Shrimp it is. Onyx says stop feeding me straight lines.
```

```
GMT 132 / 15:52

YUKI      Copy the gap. I'll keep working.
```

The loop went to the soft hiss it went to when there was no one on the other end of it, and she worked in it. She had spent four hundred and some days of her life inside that hiss, more of her life up here than in any single house she had ever paid rent on, and she knew its weight the way you know the weather of a room you have slept in for years. It was not silence. Nothing up here was silent — the fans, the pumps, the ticking of metal finding the shade and the sun sixteen times a day. It was only that no one was talking, and she had learned a long time ago that those were different things, and that a person could live a long time in the second one mistaking it for the first.

When the relay picked her up again she was in the aft node with the gray cylinder in one hand and the manifest open on the laptop with the other.

```

YUKI      Good. A room like that should have a dog in it.
HOUSTON   That's what I said. Everybody else said allergies.
YUKI      Everybody else is wrong. What've you got for me?
HOUSTON   Nothing you don't. You're go to keep pulling stowage, go to safe the core avionics when you're ready. And Onyx wanted me to pass something up. He asked me to say it plain, so I'm going to read it and then leave it alone.
YUKI      Read it.
HOUSTON   There's no clock on you from the ground, Yuki. The window on the ninth is the window we've got, but nobody down here is standing over it. It's yours. Whatever you need to do up there before you're in that seat, you do it, and we'll be here on the loop.
YUKI      That's a lot of words for Onyx.
HOUSTON   He wrote them down. I could hear him reading.

HOUSTON   I'll pass it. Two feet and falling, last I saw.

First deployment I ever pulled, we sat three days in it with nothing to do but chip paint. I've hated painting and loved that water ever since.
YUKI      You never told me that.
HOUSTON   You never asked. Eleven years, and you never once asked me what I did before I sat in this chair.
```

And he did. He talked about the water off Tampa and the water off Norfolk and a bay in the Sulu Sea that went the color of a struck match at dawn, and she pulled cargo bags and named their contents to the reader and sent them down or gone, a man reciting every ocean he had ever crossed to a woman four hundred kilometers up who was giving away everything she owned a gram at a time, and neither of them said so.

```
HOUSTON   Stand by. ...Ground says down. Somebody wants it back.
```

She entered the cylinder in the return column. She looked at it in the return column for the length of a full orbit, ninety-one minutes of the terminator crossing the aft window and the light going from white to gold to gone and back, and then she moved it to disposal, and then she stopped touching the manifest because her hands had begun to make the decision without her and she did not trust them to make it in a hurry.

She went to the cupola.

She had told herself she would not and she had a reason she had believed on day one, and the reason did not survive contact with the fact of the windows. Six of them, and the seventh overhead, and the Earth filling all of it — the Pacific just then, that particular blue that has no name on the ground because on the ground you never see enough of it at once to need one. She hooked a foot under the rail and hung there. A typhoon was spinning itself up east of the Philippines, a slow white pinwheel with a black eye, and it would be somebody's terrible week and from here it was only weather, only the machine of the planet doing what the planet does, indifferent and gorgeous and running whether or not anyone was up here to log it.

The atmosphere sat on the limb in a band she could have hidden behind a thumb, blue thinning to nothing, the whole breathable world of it no thicker than the film of anti-fog wiped on a visor. Every ninety-one minutes the terminator came at her across the water and the sun went out like a thrown breaker, and the band along the dark edge lit green with airglow, oxygen giving back the day it had swallowed. She had seen it four thousand times. It had never once looked like a thing that needed her to see it, and she had stopped, years ago, holding that against it.

She had a gray cardigan in her sleep station, and she had brought it up eleven months ago, and it still smelled — or she told herself it still smelled — of the cedar drawer in the rented house on South Tropical Trail, the one on the Banana River with the dock that flooded at the spring tides. Salt and cedar and the particular green of that yard. She had not worn it once. You did not wear your own clothes up here; you wore what the program flew you, in the colors the program chose. But she had brought it, at a hundred and forty grams of an allowance measured to the gram, and it had ridden four hundred kilometers up to hang in the dark of a sleep station and smell of a drawer in a house neither of them lived in anymore.

```
GMT 132 / 17:40
HOUSTON   Meridian, Houston, one minute to your PMC. We'll clear the loop and pick you up on the private channel. Denver's already on.
YUKI      Copy. Going private.
HOUSTON   We'll be here when you're back.
```

The loop went to hiss. She stayed in the cupola with the Pacific under her and took the private conference where nobody could hear it, and what was said there was not said on the loop the whole world had its ears against, and stayed hers, and stayed Denver's, and that was the point of a private channel and the point of a daughter and the whole small architecture of the things a person is allowed to keep. When it was over she floated back to the aft node and opened the manifest and did not change anything on it, and powered down the core avionics on schedule, forty minutes late, exactly as she had told Marsh she would be.

···

By day eight Meridian was mostly dark. She lived in the core now, in the one lit volume around the scrubber and the galley and the capsule's forward hatch, and the rest of the station ran off in tunnels of powered-down grey where the fans had stopped and the air did not move and her flashlight found frost blooming on a cold-soaked panel. The smell had gone with the power. The core still held it, thin now, the last of the hot-electronics-and-canteen that had been the smell of her whole adult life at the top of its arc, and past the core hatch the station smelled of nothing at all, of clean cold metal, of a thing that had finished being lived in.

She slept strapped to the galley wall now, because the sleep stations had gone dark and cold-soaking and there was no reason to power a whole module to lie down in it. She woke to the scrubber's note and the tick of the hull cooling on its way into the shadow, and to the small green constellation of the one panel still alive, and for a breath each time she did not know the year. Then she knew it.

She had read the timeline back to Marsh top to bottom, the way you read back anything you mean to do exactly once, and now she carried it the way she carried her own name — the hooks at 09:58, the sep, the burn she would ride flat on her back watching a screen while the ground flew her home to a lain-down sea off Tampa, and the Megan coming alongside, and men she had never met cracking the hatch and letting Florida in.

She stowed the cardigan in the return bag. It went in folded, a hundred and forty grams the manifest could not defend and no one asked her to, and she zipped the bag and marked it return and clipped it to the seat frame where it would ride down in the capsule with her, and she did not open the manifest to look at where the gray cylinder was filed, because she knew where it was filed, and she had known for two days, and the knowing had stopped needing her hands.
