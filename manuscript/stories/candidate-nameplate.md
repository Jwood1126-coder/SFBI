## Nameplate

*2043*

**SEP 14 — one house, $9,400. Main breaker OFF. Zero watts on the wire.**

I put my hand up at nine thousand four hundred and the woman from the county says *sold* before Ruben can get my wrist down, which he is trying to do, grinning, because we agreed in the truck on nine even and here my arm is straight up in the air four hundred over, like the kid who knew the answer.

Eleven people in the room and eight of them are here for a parcel out by Crane with water rights that go back to statehood. Nobody else wants the house. It sits in Krider, thirty-eight miles southeast of Burns off the 78, on the flat under the rimrock where forty families used to get their mail, and the county has been trying to give it away since the power company came and pulled the line off the pole.

That's the whole reason it's cheap. The Grid Rationalization Act let the utilities cut the ends of the wire that cost more to serve than they'd ever sell across it, and Harney County went dark on the eighteen-month schedule the year I turned twenty-two. A house with no juice on the pole, in a town with no juice on any pole, is worth about what the copper in its walls will bring at scrap.

So: nine thousand four hundred dollars, the price of a running truck with a soft clutch, for a two-bedroom on a quarter acre with a drilled well, a woodstove, and a roof that keeps most of the sky out.

The clerk slides the paper over. *Whose name goes on it,* she says.

"Both," Ruben says, before I get there. "Ochoa and Ames. However they'll fit."

Fourteen months we've been together and nothing that any paper anywhere would call a thing, and now there's a paper, and it has both our names typed on it under the legal description of a house neither of us has slept in. I fold it into the glovebox for the drive out to Krider, and it rides there the rest of the fall like a ticket we haven't used.

What the deed doesn't tell you is how dark dark is. Not dark like the power's out. Dark like there was never any power — no meter, no wire, the service drop cut and coiled at the top of the pole where a lineman left it, the pole itself leaning off toward Steens like it's tired of holding nothing. Whatever this house does this winter, it does because the two of us make it do it out of sun and wood and propane and arithmetic. That's the romance of the thing, and I won't pretend the romance wasn't half of why my arm went up.

**SEP 30 — 12 × 460 W = 5,520 W nameplate. Delivered: TBD.**

The panels come up from Bend on a Tuesday, twelve of them strapped to a pallet, four hundred sixty watts each, and I want to tell you the number stamped on the back of them, because this whole winter turns on it.

Nameplate. That's the word for it, printed on a foil sticker on the back of every panel: 460 W. It is a promise a factory makes under a lamp — noon-bright light square on the glass, the cell held at seventy-seven degrees, nothing in the way. Nobody's yard is a lamp. What a panel actually gives you is the nameplate minus the day: minus the angle of the sun, minus the dust, minus the loss in the wire, minus December. Twelve panels say five thousand five hundred twenty watts on their stickers. On the shortest day of the year, on the flat at Krider, with the sun crawling low and quick across the southern rim, I'll be glad to pull a third of that at noon and nothing at all by five. Nameplate is what a thing *is.* Delivered is what it *does for you.* The gap between the two is the whole science of not freezing to death in your own house.

Ruben welds the rack out of two-inch square tube, in the dirt behind the house, and stands the glass due south at fifty-eight degrees off level. Steeper than looks right. In winter the sun never gets high, so you tilt the panels up to stand almost facing it, and you tilt them steep on purpose so the snow slides off instead of sitting there throwing shade on your whole day. He builds like a man who has spent his life pulling apart things that were put together by somebody who never expected to have to. He is a diesel mechanic. Was, at the Freightliner shop in Burns, still is on Tuesdays and Thursdays, which is what we're living on until the house lives on its own.

We raise the last four panels in the afternoon with the sun full on us, and it is the last hot day of the year and we both know it, and he's got his shirt off and there's dust stuck to the sweat down his spine, and I hand him lag bolts one at a time so I have a reason to keep my hand near his back. He catches me at it. He always catches me at it. *Tess,* he says, and sets the impact driver down in the dirt, and kisses me against the cold glass of the array, the whole panel at my back cold through my shirt and full of the afternoon, wired to nothing yet, all that caught sun and nowhere to send it. We do not get much done in the next hour. The panels wait. They're rated to wait a lot longer than that.

**OCT 16 — 51.2 V · 300 Ah · 15.36 kWh. Usable at 80% DoD: 12.3.**

The batteries are four gray boxes in a steel rack in the utility room, lithium iron phosphate, and together they cost more than Ruben's truck and my car put in a pile and lit. Fifteen point three six kilowatt-hours, which is a number I can now say in my sleep. But you never get to spend all of it. You run a lithium bank down to about a fifth left and then you stop, because the last of it is what keeps the whole pack healthy for the ten thousand mornings after this one. Take the top eighty percent. Leave the floor. Twelve and change is what we actually have to live on between one sundown and the next sunup, and between one clear day and the next one, however many gray days God stacks in the middle.

I keep the count on the wall. We haven't painted the utility room, so the drywall is bare and I write on it in fat black marker next to the inverter — what came in off the array that day, what the house drew, where the bank sat at dark. The column climbs the wall. Most notebooks I've ever kept were things you subtract from. This one only goes up, day over day, a ladder of numbers running up toward the ceiling, and some nights I stand there with the marker and just look at how far up the wall we've gotten.

Ramona Ybarra shows up on the day we commission the bank, in a truck older than me, with a blue heeler riding the toolbox and a flat of eggs on the seat. She ranches the section and a half east of us and has run her place off the sun for twenty-four years, which in this county makes her the university. The eggs are blue-green, from Ameraucana hens, and she sets them on the tailgate like a fee.

"Your inverter's a hybrid," she says, not a question, reading the box on the wall through the open door from forty feet off. "Nice. Mine's a Trace older than my marriage was. Fan's going in it. Screams like a hurt cat when the sun's good."

Ruben has it apart on her tailgate inside the hour, the eggs moved to the shade, and finds the bearing dry and the cap on the driver board bulged up like a biscuit. He has a fan close enough in a parts bin and a cap he pulls off a dead board and reflows by headlamp while she watches his hands and misses nothing. She watches mine too, on the marker, on the wall of numbers.

"Lead-acid, mine," she says. "I never take it below half. You take a flooded bank past half enough times and it won't come back up to full, and then one January you find that out." She looks at our gray boxes, at the 80% I've written on the wall. "You kids can go deeper than me. That's the new chemistry. You can run yours way down and it forgives you." She says it flat, about the batteries, and lets it lie there in the room being about the batteries.

**OCT 22 — Breaker ON, 4:11 p.m. First loads: fridge + 2 lamps = 240 W. Bank 96%.**

We throw the main breaker at four in the afternoon on a Wednesday and the house comes on all at once — the two lamps we've hung, the fridge kicking over with that first hard shudder a compressor makes, the well controller in the corner waking up with a little green eye. Ruben whoops. I laugh so hard I have to sit down on the plywood subfloor. It is the least dramatic miracle you ever saw, a fridge starting, and it undoes me completely, because I made that. We made that. The sun came down out of the sky and we caught it in glass and stored it in a box and now it is keeping our milk cold in a house the whole state gave up on.

We don't have a bed frame yet, just the mattress on the floor in the back room and the woodstove ticking as it warms, and the one lamp on the crate throwing everything gold. He comes to me still smelling of solder and cold air off the array, and I get his shirt over his head, and his hands are rougher than they were in June from a season of steel, and I am not going to give you the rest of it except to say that a house you built the power for feels different under you than any house you ever rented. There is no meter running. Nothing we do in here tonight is being counted by anyone but me, on a wall, in the next room, in the morning. He falls asleep with his hand flat on my sternum like he's reading the charge on me, and I lie awake doing the arithmetic of it, the good arithmetic, the kind that adds.

**OCT 29 — Pump start: 240 V, LRA 34 A. Inverter surge rating 16 kW / 5 s.**

The well is the monster. I keep saying that to Ruben until he makes me stop. Everything else in the house sips — the lamps, my laptop, the fridge that clicks on and off all day pulling less than a hair dryer. The well pump drinks, and worse than drinks, it *lunges.* It sits two hundred fifty feet down a hole in the ground, a one-horse submersible on the end of a wire, and every time it starts it grabs a fistful of power to break the water loose and get the motor spinning — three, four, five times what it pulls once it's running, for about a second and a half. Locked-rotor, it's called. Thirty-four amps at two hundred forty volts, gone before you can say it.

That lunge is why an inverter has two numbers. Continuous is what it'll make all day without complaining. Surge is what it can throw for a few seconds before it faults and drops the whole house. Ours will do eight thousand watts steady and sixteen thousand for five seconds, and the pump wants nearly all of that headroom for its second and a half of lunging, and the first time we start it cold off the battery the inverter's fan roars up and the lights all over the house dip amber and come back, and the water bangs into the pressure tank, and Ruben and I stand at the kitchen tap watching it spit air and rust and then run clear and cold and *ours,* off the sun, up out of the dark, and I put my whole face in it.

We can't do that fifteen times a day. Every lunge is a bite out of the bank. So Ruben plumbs it the way you're supposed to out here — the pump fills a fifteen-hundred-gallon poly cistern once, in the middle of a sunny day when the panels are making more than the house can spend, and a little on-demand pump the size of a lunchbox pushes water to the taps from the cistern the rest of the time for almost nothing. One big greedy lunge while the sun's paying for it. Then the house drinks quiet out of the tank for two days. He wires a soft-start onto the big pump besides, a gray box that feeds the motor up gentle instead of all at once, and the lunge drops from thirty-four amps to twenty, and the lights stop dipping, and I add a line to the wall.

That night I stand in the first hot shower this house has ever run, well water the sun pumped and a propane coil heated, steam fogging the little window, and Ruben leans in the door with a beer and says we are the only people in a town of empty houses taking a hot shower right now, and it's the filthiest, luckiest feeling, being warm and clean in the middle of all that dark.

**NOV 8 — 26°F at 6 a.m. Battery accepting 0 A. Will not charge below 32°F.**

Here's the one that nearly got us, and it isn't in any of the ratings on any of the stickers.

Lithium won't take a charge when it's freezing. You can pull power *out* of a cold battery all night long. But when the sun comes up and the panels start pushing, a pack below thirty-two degrees will not accept it — shove current into frozen lithium and you plate the inside of the cells and ruin them for good — so the battery's own brain slams a door and refuses the charge, and you sit there on the shortest, coldest, most precious mornings of the year with a yard full of sunlight and a bank that won't drink it.

November eighth I come out at six and the utility room is twenty-six degrees and the bank is sitting at forty-one percent from a long cold night of running the propane furnace's blower and the fridge, and the sun clears the rim at seven-forty and the array wakes up making power, and the charge controller reads zero amps into the battery. Zero. The panels are making it. The battery won't take it. It's like watching money blow across a parking lot you can't run out into.

Ramona has known this since before I was born. She comes over with a roll of foil-faced insulation and a little thermostatic heat mat, the kind for starting seedlings, and we build the bank a coat — a stud wall around the rack, insulation, the mat under the batteries set to click on at thirty-eight and off at forty-five, run off the bank itself, a few pennies of its own power a night to keep it warm enough to take tomorrow's sun. You spend a little charge to keep the door open for a lot of charge. By afternoon the room holds at forty and the controller is pushing thirty amps into a grateful bank and I could cry, and I do a little, over a heat mat meant for tomato seedlings.

Ramona stands in the door of the little warm room we built inside the cold house and looks at the whole rig, the wall of numbers, the array out the window standing steep at the low sun.

"Forty families had their mail come to Krider," she says. "County quit the line, the bank quit the county, and everybody with a paycheck to drive to drove to it and never came back. You two bought a whole house for what a man out here couldn't get for his whole place, the year they all left. Somebody paid for your good deal." She picks up her empty egg flat off the workbench. "Well. You're paying for it now, keeping it warm. That's more than the bank ever did." And she goes out to her truck and the heeler and the section and a half, and that's the last she says about it, ever.

**NOV 24 — Clear. In 13.8 kWh, out 5.6. Bank 100% by 2 p.m. Surplus dumped to cistern & hot water.**

Cold and clear is the whole game out here, and November hands us a run of it, and this is where I fall the rest of the way in love with the arithmetic.

A clear day at Krider in late November, sun on cold glass, and the steep array throws twelve, thirteen kilowatt-hours at the house. We spend five, maybe six — the fridge, the lights, my laptop, the furnace blower, the little water pump, the internet dish on the roof. That leaves seven or eight fat kilowatt-hours over, and by two in the afternoon the bank is topped clean off and has nowhere to put the extra, so we dump it: heat the whole cistern's worth of water, run the big pump and fill the tank to the brim, charge every tool battery in the house, bake something in the little electric oven we swore we'd never run. A house making more than it can hold is a kind of wealth I never had in any apartment on any grid. On the good days we are rich. I write the surplus on the wall in a color I bought just for surplus.

The catch, and there is always a catch out here, is that the good days are short and the sun sets at half past four and the clear cold nights are long and hungry, and every kilowatt-hour the bank banks at two in the afternoon it starts spending back at half past four. Enough is not a clear day. Enough is a clear day *carrying the night behind it,* and the next day, and the gray day after that. Ramona says it takes people a full year to stop cheering on the sunny days and start counting the cloudy ones. We are not there yet. We cheer. We eat a whole pie because the oven was free.

**DEC 9 — Three days overcast, snow on the glass. SoC 38%. Generator: 1.4 gal/hr.**

Then December sends the first gray stack — three days of high overcast and a skiff of snow that sits on the panels because it isn't cold enough to be dry and slide, and the array that made thirteen kilowatt-hours on the twenty-fourth makes two, then one and a half, then not quite two, and the bank walks down the wall — sixty-one percent, forty-nine, thirty-eight — and on the evening of the third day, at thirty-eight, Ruben goes out and pull-starts the generator.

It's a little dual-fuel thing, five thousand watts, out in a shed we lined so the neighbors can't hear it, except the neighbors are all gone, so really so *we* can't hear it. We run it off propane an hour and a half after supper and it puts four kilowatt-hours back into the bank at a buck-something a gallon, and that is the honest bottom of the whole system, the part that isn't free, the part that isn't the sun. I hate the sound of it. Ruben doesn't. Ruben says a backup you're too proud to run is just a heavy box.

We have the fight that night, the one we've been not having since September. Not a real fight. The good kind, the kind you can only have once you're sure enough of a person to say the scared thing out loud. I want to sell the generator in the spring and add a second string of panels and another battery and never burn a gallon of anything to stay warm. I want the house to stand completely on its own. He wants to keep the generator forever, wants it there for the third gray day and the fourth, wants the fallback under us no matter how good the array gets, because you can love the sun all you want and there will still be a February that doesn't shine for a week.

"You want no backup," he says. "You want it to be all or nothing."

"I want it to be *enough,*" I say. "On its own. I want us to be able to say we did the whole thing ourselves."

"We did do it ourselves." He's got the propane bill in his hand, forty dollars, and he's not mad, he's laughing, which is worse and better. "Tess. You want to run this house at a hundred percent depth of discharge. Down to zero and trust it comes back. Nobody sane runs a bank to zero. You keep a floor. You keep a generator. That's not not-trusting the sun. That's just how you stay married to it for thirty years instead of one great summer."

He didn't mean married. He hears himself say it and I hear him hear it, and the generator hums out in the shed putting our night back together a gallon at a time, and neither of us says anything for a second, and then I say, "So we keep the generator," and he says, "We keep the generator," and it is the most romantic sentence a man has said to me in my life.

**DEC 21 — Solstice. Sunrise 7:41, sunset 4:20. Clear, 9°F. In 9.2 kWh, out 5.0. Bank 100% by 2 p.m. Generator cold.**

Shortest day of the year, and I've been dreading this square on the calendar since September, the bottom of the whole bathtub, the least sun this flat will ever get, and it dawns clear.

Clear and hard, nine degrees at sunrise, the kind of cold that sticks the truck doors shut and makes the panels strong, because glass likes the cold and a cold cell pushes a little harder than a warm one. The sun clears the rim at twenty to eight and drops behind it again at twenty past four, eight hours and change of it, the least this flat will ever get, and the steep array drinks all eight. Nine kilowatt-hours on the shortest day of the year — not the twenty it'll throw in June, and a long way under the five thousand five hundred twenty watts stamped on the stickers at any second you care to check, because at winter noon the array never once shows its nameplate, never comes close. But nine, against a house that spends five. By two in the afternoon the bank is topped clean off and the surplus is going into hot water, and the generator out in the shed is cold and hasn't turned over in a week.

Because here is the number I have been walking toward since I put my arm up in that courthouse: on the worst day the calendar owns, the shortest, coldest, least-sun day of the whole year, we make near twice what we spend, and the batteries end it full, and the house is warm. Woodstove roaring on juniper Ruben and I bucked and split ourselves, propane holding the back rooms off freezing, the sun even in its thinnest slice more than paying the bill. We did the December arithmetic all the way down to the bottom of the year and it came out the right side of *enough,* and from tomorrow on the days get longer.

Ramona comes by at dusk with a dozen eggs and a jar of something and looks at the wall, at the column of numbers climbed clean up to the ceiling now, four feet of days.

"You made it to the bottom," she says. "It only gets longer from here. The days, I mean. Everything else gets harder." Which is Ramona for *congratulations.*

After she goes we stand out in the yard in the cold, Ruben and me, in front of the black array under a sky so full of stars it looks like static — no town-glow anywhere on this whole flat to wash it out, our own dark bought at a courthouse for the price of a truck — and I'm already doing next year on my fingers. Second string of panels along the south fence. Another battery. The greenhouse off the cistern overflow. Chickens of our own so we stop owing Ramona eggs. A bigger everything.

"You're counting," Ruben says. He can always tell. "I can feel you counting."

"I'm not counting," I say, counting. "I want to run a line out to the shop next summer. And I want more panels. And I want—" and I don't finish it, the last thing I want, the biggest load I want to put on this little bus we built, because it is nine degrees out and there is a right way to say a thing that size and it is not standing in the frozen dirt with cold feet.

"Say it," he says.

So I turn around and I say it.
