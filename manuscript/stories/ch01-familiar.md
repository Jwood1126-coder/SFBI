## 1. Familiar

*Floor beam 14, fascia side: fatigue crack at the weld toe, 40 mm. Arrest hole drilled last cycle. Monitor.*

Marcus wrote the length in the field book before he wrote anything else — forty millimeters — and inked a small circle at the tip of the crack the way you cap a sentence. Eighty feet down the Monongahela went by the color of a nickel left on a sill. Lou ran the snooper — the bucket truck that walks an inspector out under a bridge's deck — from topside, easing the arm in under the fascia girder to where the road salt hadn't reached, and Marcus laid the flat of the hammer against the web and listened.

Sound steel rings. Bad steel takes the blow and keeps it. Floor beam 14 rang the length of its web, bright, all but the one joint, where for thirty winters a hairline had been opening about a thousandth of an inch a year, patient as a mortgage.

"Talking to you?" Lou said in the headset.

"Arrest hole's holding. Same forty."

"Then write your forty so I can bring you in. I lost my thumbs an hour ago."

He wrote the forty. He set the scale card against the drilled hole and shot the photo, the number in his own block print beside the steel. A crack you have found and measured and entered in the book stops being the thing that will drop you in the river and becomes a thing you visit. That was the whole trade in one sentence. You did not fix every flaw; there was not money or lane closure on earth for that. You learned which ones were talking and which were only old, and twice a year for thirty years you put your hammer on them, and the deck above carried its eleven thousand cars a day and never knew a man had been worried on its account.

Lou brought him up out of the cold and they sat in the truck with the heater roaring while the windshield cleared. Lou kept a Sanka can wedged in the console for the shells; he ran through a palmful of sunflower seeds the way other men smoked. On the mirror hung his father's plumb bob, brass, plumb since Eisenhower, useless now that a laser did it truer, and Lou would no more take it down than saw off his own arm. He called every bridge by its state structure number. He was calling this one 0231 and reading Marcus the next three on the ticket while Marcus watched the river take a barge downstream toward Braddock, nine hoppers of coal riding low, the pilot holding the tow off the pier like a man threading a needle in a moving car.

"You good?" Lou said, not looking over, because it was the fourth month and he had learned to ask it the way you'd ask a bridge. Not *are you well.* Are you carrying.

"I'm good," Marcus said, and he was. That was the strange part he told no one. He was better than he had been in forty years.

He came home at dusk to the house in Hazelwood his father had bought off a millwright in 1971. The porch light was on. Renata always left it on, had left it on through every shift he'd ever worked, and the yellow of it caught the wet slate and the loose iron rail by the back stoop that he had told her twice he would get to. She opened the door before he reached it. She had the dish towel over her shoulder and her reading glasses pushed up into her hair and the small chip in her front tooth caught the hall light when she said his name, the chip she'd taken off the neck of an Iron City at a Pirates game in 1994 and never fixed because the dentist wanted four hundred dollars and she wanted a washer-dryer.

It was Renata. Every gauge said Renata. Her voice, the chip, the ring worn thin as wire that had been her mother's, the way she'd salted her coffee since before he knew her because her grandmother swore it cut the bitter. All of it read true, and none of it arrived. A stranger with his wife's whole history stood in his doorway wearing his wife's dish towel, and the warmth that for thirty-one years had come into the room a half-second ahead of her did not come.

The dog came instead. Duke, the beagle, gray at the muzzle now, dragging the same bald tennis ball he'd carried since he could carry, and he leaned his whole weight on Marcus's shins the way he did, and something in Marcus's chest still had nothing at all to say about it.

"You're froze through," the stranger said in Renata's voice. "Sit. I salted the door but the bottom step's still ice."

He sat. She fed him. He was polite to her the way you are polite in another man's house.

*Fracture-critical member. Single load path. Failure of this member would drop the span.*

The trial was eleven days, and it sat four months behind him now. He wanted that written down somewhere true, because everyone kept saying it like it was nothing, eleven days, as though a thing that fast could not have cut deep.

He had been sad his whole life. Not the kind you tell a doctor. The kind that is just the weather where you live, that you were born into and assumed was the air. Every bridge carries two loads. Dead load is the steel itself, the deck, the rail, the weight the thing was built out of and will hold up before a single car ever crosses. Live load is the day's traffic, the trucks and the wind and the ice, all of it changing minute to minute. His whole life the dead load had been the sadness. It was there in the morning before anything happened. It was the weight he was made of. He carried his live load over top of it the way every span does, and he did his work, and he raised a daughter, and he loved his wife, and no one on the deck ever knew the girder underneath was already loaded to nine-tenths before the first car came.

They put him in the machine for a lifelong depression that had stopped answering to anything with a name on a bottle. Focused ultrasound. A helmet of a thing, a thousand beams of sound crossing in the dark of his skull to meet at one point the size of a pea and quiet it. He lay still and heard the water circulating and thought about caissons, the great sunk boxes they pumped dry to build a pier, men going down into pressed air to dig under a river. On day eleven the caisson came up dry. He woke in the gray of the ward and the dead load was gone. The span rose. He lay there weightless and forty years lighter and he wept, the good way, for the first time since he was a boy, and Renata drove in from Hazelwood to sit with him, and the woman who came through the door in her good coat was not his wife.

The consent form ran nine pages. He had read all nine, because he read everything, because a man who signs off on what eleven thousand cars drive over reads the fine print. It listed headache, it listed the pin-sized chance of a bleed, it listed a numbness that might not come back. It said nothing about her face. It could not have. No one had built the gauge that measured what came off with the sadness. The warmth had been welded to the dead load somewhere down in the dark he could not inspect, and when they cut the one away clean they took the other with it, and the cut was so neat there was no burr left to catch a fingernail on.

He knew her. That was the whole cruelty of it, that nothing was lost that a stranger could see. He could have told you her mother's maiden name and the year of the washer-dryer and which side of the bed she took and why. The file was complete. The recognition was gone. She was his wife the way a photograph of a bridge is a bridge — every rivet in its place, and no steel in it, and nothing you could stand on.

They had only ever been the two of them, really. Their daughter Ana out in Chicago since her divorce, and before Ana just the two of them, and after Ana gone the two again. No third member to carry the load if one let go. He understood the drawing of his own marriage now the way he understood a fracture-critical span. Non-redundant. Built for one path down through the two of them and no other, and beautiful for it, and one weld from the river.

*Repaint recommended, cycle before last. Deferred. Recommended again. Deferred.*

The follow-up clinic was in Oakland, up the hill from where the mills used to run, and the waiting room had the radio going low behind the desk the way those rooms do, a man and a woman talking easy about the Adeyemi anniversary, industrial gases in the air of a planet no one would ever stand on, proof of somebody, they said, a decade of it now and still no way on God's earth to answer back. Marcus filled in the form on the clipboard. Renata sat one chair over with her coat still buttoned and did the Post-Gazette crossword in ballpoint, in pen, always in pen, and did not once look up to see if he was watching her do it.

The neurologist kept a glass dish of butterscotch on the desk that she never offered and never ate, gone tacky in their wrappers with the years. She had his scans up on the wall, the bright small quiet where the pea of sound had been. She was pleased with the depression. She named the other thing once, acquired Capgras, and set it beside the sadness like two entries in the same book, and then she said the things they say. Transient, probably. The brain is not a switchboard. Pathways reorganize. She had seen the affect come back in weeks, in others; she had seen it come back in pieces, out of order, the way feeling in a frozen hand comes back to the palm before the fingers. He should give it time. He should not test himself against it, she said, because a watched pot, and here she trailed off, because even she could hear that it was the wrong thing to say to a man whose living was watching for the failure before it came.

He drove them home the long way, over his own bridges, the ones with his initials in thirty field books downtown. Renata rode with her hands folded on the crossword in her lap. Somewhere over the water she said, not to him exactly, that she'd started a chicken before they left, and he said good, and that was the whole of it, and the tow lights moved on the black river below them, and neither of them said the name of the thing, then or ever. There was nothing to explain. You do not explain the weather to the man standing in it.

*Pack rust in the gusset, forcing the plates apart from the inside. Nothing shows from the deck.*

Saturday he began to build his tests, because he did not know another way to be a man.

He clipped the pulse oximeter he'd bought for his mother onto his own left thumb and sat at the kitchen table reading the paper while Renata moved through the morning. Sixty-eight, the pulse line read. Sixty-seven. He turned the pages. She came in from the yard with cold in her sweater and stood at the counter and salted her coffee, the small ritual, the shake and the stir, and he kept his eyes on the box scores and his thumb on the table, and the number sat in the sixties and did not so much as think about climbing. He had watched that number jump to a hundred and twenty in the machine at the mere idea of the sound starting. It knew fear fine. It knew nothing of the woman four feet away pouring cream.

He kept a box of her letters in the cellar, from the two years he'd worked the turnpike bridges downstate and come home only Sundays, 1989 and 1990, her hand young and slanting hard to the right. He read them at the workbench under the one bulb while the furnace ticked. She had loved him in ink better than most men get loved in the flesh. He read the one where she told him she was late and scared and glad, the one that made Ana, and he waited at the bottom of the page for the thing to come up in him the way the river comes up in spring, and it did not come up. The words were true and he was the man they were written to and the man they were written to felt the way you feel reading someone else's mail. He put them back in the box in order. He was careful with the order. He had always been careful with what other people would not think to check.

He slept on his own side. He had started, some nights, lying very still on his back on his own half and setting his hand flat on the cool of her empty side while she was still up locking the house, the way you'd set a hand on a girder to feel for the traffic through it, for the live load coming, and he would feel the sheet warm slowly under his palm and nothing else move through, and then she would come to bed and he would take his hand back and say goodnight in a voice he had gotten very good at.

She was courting him. He worked it out slow, the way you work out that a bridge is being loaded past its posting — not one big thing, a hundred small readings that only mean something stacked. She wore the green she'd worn the year they met. She put a record on after supper, Sam Cooke, and did not say why, and sat closer on the davenport than the room required, close enough that he could smell the rye of the bakery still in her hair, and she talked to him the way you talk to a man you are trying to get, telling him things about her days he already knew, laughing first at her own stories to show him where the laugh went. She had figured, without a word to him about it, that if the man could not remember he loved her she would make him fall for her again from the beginning, standing start, cold engine, and she had set herself to it the way she set herself to a hem, pins in the tomato cushion on her wrist, mouth full of pins, no complaint.

He let her. He answered her right on every turn. He laughed where the laugh went. He was, he thought, a well-maintained bridge — posted, monitored, every joint sounded on schedule — carrying her live load across a span that had no dead load left under it to hold the two of them up, and neither of them able to see from the road that the plates were being forced apart from the inside, slow, by a rust with no color, in a place the deck would never show.

Dominic came at four for his lesson — she taught the piano to the neighborhood, three dollars less than anyone in Squirrel Hill — and dropped his Penguins backpack by the bench and murdered the same Clementi he'd been murdering since October and kicked the piano leg on the hard part, and left a juice box sweating a ring into the wood. Renata sat beside him and put her hand over his hand to slow it and said *listen, listen,* and Marcus stood in the kitchen doorway with a dish towel he'd picked up for no reason and watched her teach a stranger's boy to hear a thing, and he thought that he would carry her across on his back the rest of his life before he would ever let her know how far the reading had fallen.

*Expansion bearing seized. The span can no longer move the way it was built to move.*

The dog came back first.

It was a Tuesday, nothing to it. Duke put his gray muzzle on Marcus's knee in the blue light of the ballgame and Marcus went to rub the long ears the way he had ten thousand times, and the warmth came up out of nowhere and filled his hand and his throat, whole and stupid and enormous, love for a beagle who would be dead inside two years, and Marcus sat in the dark with his hand full of the dog's ear and his eyes running and he did not dare turn his head toward the kitchen where Renata was doing the dishes, because he did not want her to see it land on the dog. He heard the water shut off. He heard her stop. She had seen the back of his neck do something. She stood in the door drying a plate that was already dry, drying it and drying it, and then she went back to the sink and ran the water again so he would not have to say anything, and that was a kindness he had no name in him to answer.

His mother came back next. He drove out to Munhall on the Sunday, to the home where she kept the television on the weather channel with the sound off and folded the paper towels into careful quarters and stacked them on the sill though the aides brought fresh, and she took his face in both her ruined hands the way she had since he was a boy, and it came, it all came, forty years of her at once, and he held on to her too long and she patted his back and said *there, there, what's this,* and did not know she had given him something the doctors couldn't. He drove home over the river in the last light with it still warm in his chest and he thought, coming down off the bridge into Hazelwood, that it was going to come back for Renata now, any day, it was warming up through him room by room, palm before fingers, and he let himself believe it the whole way home.

Ana called that night, Sunday being when she called, and read him her grocery list before she got to anything, oat milk, the good coffee, dog food for the dog she'd finally got, the way she'd narrated her whole life to him down a phone line since she was six. She asked how her mother was. She asked, careful, how he was, and he said better, and she said she could hear it, and she could, the depression was gone and would stay gone, that part had held. She did not ask the other thing because Renata had not told her the other thing, because Renata protected them both from it the way she protected everyone, quietly, at cost, without a receipt.

But it stalled. It came up through him for the dog and for his mother and for the smell of the rye and for a Clementi played right at last, and it stopped at the one door, the way an expansion bearing seizes with rust and the whole span goes rigid and cannot answer the heat and the cold the way it was drawn to, and the loads it was built to shed with a quarter-inch of give have nowhere now to go. He felt it warming through every room in the house but hers, and he began, God help him, to be afraid that the machine had not broken something at all. That it had measured. That it had found the one weld in him that had gone hollow years before any sound ever touched it, and had only, at last, told him the reading.

*Load posting reduced. The structure carries less than the plans allow. Adequate.*

They posted the Glenwood on a Thursday. Corrosion in the lower chord past what the rating could carry safe, and the money three years out, and so they hung the black-and-white signs at both ends dropping it to fifteen tons, no combination loads, and the trucks that used to cross it would go around by the Hot Metal from now on, six miles the long way, for years, maybe for good.

"Feels like a funeral," the young one on the crew said, torquing the last sign bracket down, and Lou spat a shell into the Sanka can and said no. A funeral was when you closed it. This was the other thing. This was you looked at what the span could still truly carry, and you carried exactly that and not one pound more, and you told the truth about it in black and white at both ends so nobody drove onto it expecting what it used to give, and it stood. It carried its fifteen tons a day for another forty years and held up every car that trusted the number. There was no shame in a posted bridge. The shame was in the deck that let on it was sound when it wasn't, and dropped a school bus in the river to keep its dignity.

Marcus signed the rating. His initials, the date, the reduced number in the box. He drove home the long way over his own bridges in the dark and did not test himself once the whole way, and it was the first quiet he'd had.

*Recommendation: full repair, not monitoring. Restore the load path. Close the ticket.*

The cold snapped down hard that night, single digits, the kind that finds every flaw. Renata went up at ten. He heard the boards of the old house take her weight, the third stair, the landing, and then the pipes and then quiet. He sat with the dog until the game ended and the dog was asleep on his boots.

Then he got the drop light and the box of anchors and went out the back in his coat.

The stoop rail had been loose since October. He'd told her twice he'd get to it. He'd deferred it the way you defer a repaint, telling himself it was talking but not yet, and every one of those cold mornings she had come down these steps in the dark to open the bakery and put her hand on it, and it had given a half inch under her hand and held, and she had never said. He hung the light off the gutter and he ran his bare hand down the rail in the cold the way he'd run it down ten thousand feet of steel, feeling for the story, and the rail told it to him plain. The bottom post was the fault. Whoever set it had lagged it into the mortar joint instead of the block, and thirty winters of water had got in and frozen and thawed and packed the joint with rust and blown the mortar to sugar, and the whole rail stood now on two lags turning in a hole full of orange dust. Section loss at the bracket, eating up from the base. A stranger leaning on it would have called it solid. It would have let go the first bad morning, with her whole weight coming down on the ice.

He did it right. He did it the way he would have done it eighty feet over the water with three men watching. He backed the ruined lags out and raked the dead mortar from the joint and vacuumed the dust and wire-brushed the bracket down to bright metal, the faying surface bare and clean the way the spec wants it, no rust between the steel and what holds the steel. He drilled the block itself, not the joint, three holes true, and blew them clear, and set the wedge anchors and drew them up. He torqued each nut to the number, turn of the wrench, turn of the wrench, past the give and into the solid, and felt through the wrench the moment each one seated home and stopped being a bolt going in and became a bridge. He wiped the rail down and put his cold hand on it and leaned, and then he put his boot up on the bottom step and stood his whole two hundred pounds on it out over the ice, both hands, and rocked, and it did not move. It took the load and gave nothing back and it would take hers every dark morning for the next forty years and never once tell her a man had been worried on her account.

Nothing came up in him while he did it. He had stopped waiting for it. He gathered the light and the brushes and the box of good anchors and stood a moment in the yard in the killing cold, and the kitchen window was yellow above him where she'd left the light on, the way she left it on through every shift he had ever worked, and he went in out of the dark and up the stairs he'd sounded a thousand times and got into his own side of the bed, and set his hand flat on the warm of her back, and left it there, and did not take it away.
