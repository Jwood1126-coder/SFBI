## 5. Sibship

Greta could seat a hundred and forty in the Bendel barn if she ran twelve sixty-inch rounds down the long axis and killed the sweetheart table, which the Kaczmarek bride would not hear, having seen a photograph of a sweetheart table on a bluff at golden hour and aimed the rest of her engagement straight at it. A sixty-inch round sits eight in comfort and ten if the ten of them like each other. Greta blocked the client's file — head table for two, the sesame aunt beached at Table 11 where the passed apps go to die, the divorced parents on opposite sides of the dance floor with the DJ booth as a wall between them — and she saved it, and she opened the other file. The one with no client, because the client was her.

She had named it SIBSHIP, in the word the letter used, the tidy one, the one that means a set of brothers and sisters taken together, full or half, as though they were a clutch of anything, eggs or bolts or a stack of folding chairs off the same truck. Forty-one of them the Donor Transparency Act had shaken loose the winter the records opened, gamete files unsealed back to 1980 and mailed out certified, signature required, the way the state serves a summons or a jury notice or a thing you owe. Cohort 148. Nine states. Nineteen had answered yes. Four had answered a clean no, and one of the four was suing the state over the very letter that had introduced them all. Eight had opened her message — she could see that they had opened it, the little read receipts sitting there like place cards at a table nobody claimed — and left it standing in the doorway with no chair pulled.

Nineteen strangers with her chin. She had arranged four hundred families in her working life and survived every one of them, and she had never once had to build the head table out of people who did not yet know what they were to each other.

The registry gave Greta names and states and a percentage of shared DNA, and nothing about a person that would help her seat them. She built the guest list the way she built every guest list, which was to say she built it out of what people could not eat.

Three anaphylactic, one of them to sesame, which knocked the tahini off the passed-apps tray before she had a caterer. Four gluten. Two who wrote *no pork, thank you* in a way that meant do not make me explain it. A vegan, a keto, a woman in Oregon who typed a paragraph about nightshades. Greta read the paragraph twice, entered *no tomato / no pepper* into Priya's color-coded cell, and understood the woman better than she understood some of the sisters she had grown up with, because a dietary restriction is a confession people make without noticing, a small true thing offered up to a stranger who is only trying to feed them.

The venue took the least deciding, which was a mercy. She booked the Bendel barn outside Granville, forty minutes northeast of Columbus off 16, a restored dairy barn with a hayloft the Bendels had turned into a getting-ready suite that this cohort would not need, and gravel parking for sixty cars, and a caterer on the preferred list who would do chicken, walleye, or a mushroom thing, and who did not blink when Greta said she needed a hard count by the Wednesday prior and would be phoning in a guarantee. Every barn in central Ohio was a dairy barn once. You could still read it in the concrete, the gutters cut into the floor where the milk parlor drained, filled and troweled over now and holding the polish a little differently, so that at four in the afternoon the light came across the floor and showed you exactly where the cows had stood.

RSVPs came the way RSVPs come, which is not the way people promise they will. She sent the form the first of March for a supper the second Saturday in June, and by the deadline she had six. She chased the rest the way she chased every wedding, by text and then by phone, and the notes came back in with them, because she had left a field at the bottom of the form the way she always did, *Anything we should know?*, and people cannot leave a blank field alone.

The letter was one page, typed on a home printer, the toner going pale in the last third the way toner does when a man will not buy a new cartridge for the sake of one page. He had been Donor 148 at a clinic in Columbus for six years in the eighties, he wrote, when he was a young faculty member and the stipend was fifty dollars and paid the electric. He had taught human anatomy for thirty-one years, most of them at a state university outside Phoenix, and was retired now to Tempe, and his wife had passed, and his knees were shot, and he understood entirely if the answer was no and would think no less of anyone for it. He signed it *Sincerely, Lawrence "Larry" Vance,* and under the name he had written a phone number and *any time before nine, my time.*

Greta read it three times looking for the thing she was supposed to feel and did not find it, because it read like every father-of-the-bride note she had ever fielded, the man circling the edge of an event that was not his, asking permission to stand in a room where the important people were other people. He had taught anatomy. He had spent thirty-one years with the body opened on a steel table, naming every part of it, the origin and insertion of each muscle, and he had scattered forty-one bodies across nine states from a vial and a fifty-dollar stipend and could not have named a single one of them on the street. She sat with that and it would not resolve into anything but a man with bad knees who wanted to come to a supper.

She did what she did with any late, awkward, load-bearing guest. She started moving him around the chart.

Head table was wrong. There was no groom to seat him beside, no marriage he had made, no toast that ran through him. Put him at the head and you crowned him, you made forty-one lives into a story with him at the source of it, and Greta had watched enough fathers try to stand at the front of a day that was not about them to know how that reads from the back of a room. Put him in the far corner by the exit and you had done the opposite thing just as loudly, you had built him a penalty box and sat him in it in front of his own children, and there would be a photograph, there is always a photograph.

She penciled him at Table 1, between Kayla and the high chair, so he could meet the baby, the one grandchild who would be in the room, and she looked at that for a while and did not like what it did to Kayla, who had a father going quiet at home and did not need a second one handed to her over the walleye.

She left him unseated and worked the rest of the chart, the way you leave the hardest guest for last and hope the table fills in a shape that answers the question for you.

The barn smelled of cut grass and the orange oil the Bendels ran over the tables, and Greta was there at nine in the morning for a five o'clock supper because load-in was the only stretch of any event she could hold in her two hands. She set the four rounds herself rather than trust the rental crew, sixty-inch tops on the cross-base stands, and she leveled each one with the folded coasters she kept in the kit for exactly the barn floors of the world, because a round table on an old dairy floor will rock toward the gutter line every time, and a rocking table is the first thing a nervous guest fixes on and the last thing they forget. She laid the linens, ivory, and steamed the fold-creases out kneeling on the concrete over the drained-and-troweled place where the cows had stood. She set out the escort cards she had hand-lettered at her own kitchen table, one per guest, name and table, the only handwriting most of these people would ever see of hers.

They came in off the gravel in ones and twos from noon on, early the way people come early to a thing they are afraid of, and Greta stood back by the drinks and watched forty years of a stranger's face walk through a barn door nineteen times.

The chin, first, every time — she caught herself checking for the chin and made herself stop. But it was there, hers, and then past the chin the rest of them scattered wide, because they shared one man and forty-one mothers, and half-siblings are a lesson in how little half is. Priya came in brown-skinned and small with a label maker actually in her handbag, and set a printed name tag on the gift table that read GIFTS, and one on the trash bin. Colleen came in a nurse's clogs and stood inside the door until she had found both exits with her eyes, and only then let her shoulders down. Dale came in a green seed-corn cap he did not take off, and went straight to the coffee, and Greta had kept the pot going, and watched him find it and pour it black and something in her closed like a good count.

Two of them, Doug and Ana, had the same widow's peak off the same high forehead, and they clocked it on each other across the room and could not stop looking, Doug rolling a half-dollar back and forth over his knuckles the whole time he talked, a close-up habit from some other life, Ana with a fanny pack cinched over a church dress, and the two of them ended up standing skull to skull comparing hairlines and laughing in a register that carried. Russ handed out the shirts from a rolling cooler that also held, Greta would learn, sixteen bottles of a brown ale he had made in his garage and labeled COHORT with a rubber stamp. Kayla came last of the early ones with June on her hip and a diaper bag printed all over with little foxes, and did not, in the end, put the baby in the onesie, and nobody asked why, and that was its own kind of kindness going around a room.

Theo made it. The window had held, he said, still in the launch badge and the lanyard he apparently did not take off on the ground either, three laminated passes on a strap around his neck, and he found the aisle seat Greta had promised him and set his phone face up beside his plate where he could see it, and did not once look at it the whole supper.

Marta came in past four, later than the shy ones usually manage, and pressed a jar of chokecherry into Greta's hands still warm from her own kitchen, wrapped in a square of cloth, and said *you have to fight it to a boil,* and Greta said *you told me,* and Marta said *I did, didn't I,* and put herself at the end of a table near the door where she could leave.

They did not cry. Greta had braced the whole barn for it, had a case of the good tissues in the kit and had told the caterer to hold the toasts loose in case the room could not take them, and instead the room did what a room full of strangers with one thing in common does, which is talk about the one thing until it wears smooth and then, blessedly, talk about anything else. They compared the letters, where they had been standing when the certified mail came, who had signed for it and who had let the second notice slide. They compared the states. Somebody had a map. It went smooth, and then it went to weather and jobs and the drive up and whether the walleye was Lake Erie, and it was, and the barn filled with the ordinary noise of people finding out they liked some of each other, which is all a reunion has ever been able to promise.

Larry Vance came at five on the dot, because a man who has waited to be told he might come does not come early.

He parked a silver Camry at the end of the gravel and walked in slow on the shot knees, a tall old man gone stooped, in pressed khakis and a golf polo the pale green of a hospital corridor, and orthopedic sandals over white socks, and he carried, in both hands, a sheet cake from a grocery bakery in a clamshell lid, half a sheet, white with a blue gel border, because you do not come to a person's house empty-handed and he had not known what else the occasion asked of him. He stood inside the door holding the cake and the room caught the fact of him and went quieter in a ring that spread out from the door, the way a round table breaks its own conversation when one voice at it stops.

Greta went to him because it was her floor. She took the cake so he would have his hands. Up close his eyes were pale and watering a little in the way of the old, and he had her chin, or she had his, and it landed on her as nothing, a chin on an old man, mass-produced.

"You'll be Greta," he said. "You're the one that put it all together. That's a talent. My wife couldn't seat a card table for four." He looked out over the barn, the rounds, the ivory, the lights she had strung in the loft rail, the nineteen faces turned half toward him. "It's a real nice turnout," he said. "I didn't think it'd be so many."

He was ushered around, and he shook hands, and he was, at close range and at length, exactly as ordinary as his letter. He told Dale about the drive, the two flights and the layover in Denver and how they don't feed you anymore. He told Colleen about his knees, the left one bone on bone, the surgeon in Scottsdale who wanted to do both at once and how he'd said no thank you, one at a time, a man's got to walk out of there on something. He told Theo he'd read about the launches, the trains of them going over, and wasn't it something, and Theo said it was something, and left it there. He asked Priya what she did and did not hold the answer past the next question. He held June for a photograph, stiff, the way a man holds a baby he does not expect to hold again, and gave her back the instant it was decent, and June was fine, June had been held by strangers all day.

Someone finally asked him, gently, the thing, why he'd done it, back then, and he said, "Fifty dollars," and there was a laugh, and he did not seem to know he had said a thing that would ring in some of them for a year, and then he said, "and they said it helped folks that couldn't otherwise. That was true, they told me. I taught anatomy thirty-one years. I always figured the body's a good thing to pass along, more of it in the world." He said it the way a man says a thing he settled a long time ago and has not turned over since, and reached for the sheet cake, and asked Greta where he might set it out, in case anybody wanted cake.

He did not remember the clinic street. He thought it had been near a Perkins. He did not remember any of the nurses. He had donated for six years and could not, when Doug asked, say what year he'd stopped, only that it was around when he made associate and the stipend stopped mattering. He was warm and he was courteous and he was completely without the thing they had each, in the privacy of their own certified letter, half-hoped and half-dreaded to find in him, which was a father, or an answer, or even a good enemy. He was a retired professor with a grocery cake and a bad knee who had passed along more of the body into the world and could not name a molecule of what he'd made, and he sat where Greta put him, and ate the chicken, and at seven-thirty, before the toasts, before the cake he'd brought was cut, he found her by the drinks and said the drive back to the airport hotel was a long one in the dark and he didn't see so well at night anymore, and thanked her for the seat, and went.

Marta had gone the same way an hour before, quietly, out the near door, and it was the same leaving, the shy sister and the ordinary father slipping out before the toasts to spare a room their goodbyes, and only Greta stood in a spot to see that the two exits carried the two of them out into the same gravel dusk within an hour of each other, the guest who could not bear the crowd and the guest the crowd could not place, and that the supper closed over both their empty chairs and went on being loud.

The rental crew came for the tables at nine. Greta sent them off with the last of Russ's brown ale and swept the barn floor herself, over the milk gutters, under the loft, and turned off the lights she had strung, and sat down at last at one bare sixty-inch round with her kit and the master chart, the paper one, the one she worked in pencil because a chart is never finished until the last guest is in a chair, and by then it does not matter.

She had the whole cohort laid out on it in her own hand, four rounds of them, green cells made into penciled names, and every crossing-out from the day, the two amber sibs who'd firmed to green, the guest Colleen had brought and then hadn't, the high chair added and moved. She found Larry Vance where she had finally penciled him that afternoon when the floor made her choose, at Table 1, at the head, between Kayla and the baby, in the seat she had known even as she wrote it was the wrong seat, the crowning seat, the seat that made forty-one lives into his.

She took the eraser to it. She rubbed the letters of his name out of Table 1 until the pencil was gone and only the little burnish of the graphite stayed in the paper, a ghost you could read at an angle in the right light, the way you could still read the milk parlor in the floor.

Then she found him a table.

Not the head. The head belonged to the nineteen who'd driven in from nine states because a letter had made them into a word, and to the baby, and to Dale who drank it black, and to Theo who'd held a launch window loose for it. She wrote *Larry V.* small, in the round by the near door, the one that had emptied first — Marta's chair, gone; the two husbands who'd married in and stood all night at the edges; Russ's cooler; Colleen who kept a line to the exit; the guests and the plus-ones and the grandchild's fox-printed bag, the table of the almost, the married-in and the slipped-out and the not-quite-kin.

She penciled his name where the arithmetic finally let a stranger sit among his own, and set the number beside it in her own hand, and capped the pencil, and there he was, seated at last, at the far table by the door, the one you leave by:

Table four.
