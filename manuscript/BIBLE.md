# THE GRAIN — Project Bible, v1
### A book of stories from the near present. Editorial direction by Fable; drafts by Opus; continuation by whoever holds this document.

---

## §0. How to use this document (read this first, future collaborator)

This bible is the project's memory and its engine. It contains everything needed to draft, revise, and extend THE GRAIN without the original editor: the book's architecture (§1), the voice law (§2), the shared world (§3), the working prompts (§4), and a full editorial brief for every chapter, including alternate directions — "levers" — deliberately left in the walls (§5).

**Order of operations for any future session, with any strong model:**

1. Have the model read §1–§3 in full before it writes a word. §2 is law, not advice.
2. To draft or redraft a chapter: use the Commission Prompt (§4a) with that chapter's brief from §5.
3. To revise an existing draft: use the Revision Prompt (§4b). Choose at most one lever per revision. Never pull two levers at once — you won't know which one moved the story.
4. Keep every draft: `manuscript/stories/ch{NN}-{slug}.md` is the current text; park old versions as `ch{NN}-{slug}-draft{k}.md`. Append one line per revision to `manuscript/CHANGELOG.md` saying what lever was pulled and why.
5. Read one story aloud per session. The voice violations you can't see, you can hear.
6. The dossier volumes (`ideas/volume-1-fifty-signals.html`, `ideas/volume-2-fifty-mirrors.html`) are the seed bank — 100 premises, of which this book uses sixteen. Future volumes of THE GRAIN can draw from the rest (§6).

**What "done" means:** a story is done when it obeys §2, lands its ending directive from §5, and survives being read aloud without a single sentence that sounds like it apologized before it spoke.

## §1. The book

**Title:** THE GRAIN — *stories*

**What it is:** Sixteen stories set in one loose near future, 2029–2044, our world plus one honest speculative element per story. Not horror, not adventure — philosophy carried in the bodies of working people. Every story contains an instrument (a scan, a law, a model, a measurement) and a person the instrument cannot quite hold. The title is the thesis kept under the floor: an instrument finds structure — in wood, in light, in a brain, in a life — and the human question is always whether structure means anything, or whether meaning is what we lay over the grain because we cannot look at bare wood.

**Why "the near present":** the reader should never be able to say "that couldn't be my street." Every premise is one policy, one clinical trial, one launch contract away. The book's wonder is domestic. Its dread is domestic. Its consolations are domestic.

**The arc across the book:** it opens with a man testing whether his love is real (Ch. 1) and closes with a woman certifying whether her father's life is complete (Ch. 16). Between those two questions — *can a feeling prove itself* and *can a life be finished* — the book widens from one bed to one town to the sky and comes back down. The last word of the book belongs to the living.

**Structure:** six parts, each opened by a short untitled page of prose (already written; `manuscript/frame/part-*.md`). Parts are movements, not categories:

- **Part I — WHAT THE INSTRUMENT SHOWS** · Ch. 1 Familiar · Ch. 2 Because
- **Part II — THE TERMS OF KNOWING EACH OTHER** · Ch. 3 Fluent · Ch. 4 Adherence · Ch. 5 Sibship
- **Part III — THE WORKING SKY** · Ch. 6 Conjunction · Ch. 7 Deorbit · Ch. 8 The Ellipse
- **Part IV — NEIGHBORS** · Ch. 9 The Pollinators · Ch. 10 Tonewood · Ch. 11 Islanding
- **Part V — ALMOST-MINDS** · Ch. 12 Absolution · Ch. 13 Deprecation · Ch. 14 Tacit
- **Part VI — LAST THINGS** · Ch. 15 The Grain · Ch. 16 Enough

**Throughlines (quiet, never announced):**
- *The instrument and the remainder.* Every story's device measures something real and misses something realer. The miss is the story.
- *Care as maintenance.* Love in this book is expressed through upkeep — dosing, mending, logging, watering, tuning. When someone stops maintaining, that is the loudest thing they say.
- *The shared world.* Stories glance off each other's events (§3) the way strangers share a news cycle. No character crosses between stories. The world coheres; the lives stay separate. That loneliness is on purpose.
- *Grain.* Wood grain, wheat, film grain, the grain of the cosmos in Ch. 15. Let it recur as material, never as symbol-with-a-bow.

**Range requirements:** at least one story warm and funny (Ch. 5), one formally daring (Ch. 13, second person), one in first person (Ch. 10), one built from documents (Ch. 8), one almost all dialogue (Ch. 7). The book must not settle into a single register of tasteful melancholy. Vary the temperature.

**The dyad test (amendment, v1.2 — binding).** Every story must be able to name, in one line each, its *equation* and its *mirror*: the hard constraint that drives the plot (a dose curve, a covariance, a chill-hour requirement, an uncertainty ellipse) and the human bond that makes the outcome matter (a marriage, a sibship, a crew, a daughter). The equation earns the reader's belief; the mirror earns their care. A draft with only the equation is a thought experiment; with only the mirror, a soap. The book lives where the two work on each other — the equation stressing the bond, the bond changing how the equation is read. When a chapter is drafted or revised, state its dyad in one line in the changelog; if either half cannot be named, the draft is not done.

## §2. The voice (law)

The book's voice: **plainspoken precision with weather in it.** Sentences a working person could say, arranged with a poet's ear. Specificity is the whole engine — the right part number beats the right adjective.

**Rules. These are binding, not aspirational:**

1. **Trade-locked imagery.** Every metaphor comes from the point-of-view character's work or ground. The bar pilot thinks in load and fetch; the pharmacist in half-lives; the nun in hours of the office. If an image could belong to anyone, it belongs to no one — cut it.
2. **Kill the balanced see-saw.** No "not X, but Y." No "it wasn't the A that frightened her; it was the B." No triads of abstract nouns ("grief, memory, and hope"). One such construction per story, maximum, and only if nothing else will do.
3. **No filtering.** Delete "she realized," "he noticed," "she felt that," "it seemed." Render the thing seen, not the seeing.
4. **Numbers and nouns do the feeling.** "Forty thousand dollars" grieves harder than "an impossible sum." Name the drug, the county, the fitting, the hymn.
5. **Rhythm is content.** Read aloud. A page wants one long sentence that runs like a hallway and one sentence of three words. Never three sentences of the same length in a row. Paragraphs are breaths, not modules — some run a page, some are one line.
6. **Dialogue misses by degrees.** People answer the question before the one asked, or the one after. Nobody states the theme. Nobody explains the technology to someone who'd already know.
7. **No epiphanies.** Endings land on an object, an act, or a line of speech. The banned ending: a character understanding something, named as understanding. The reader has the feeling; the character has the chore.
8. **Banned phrases, non-negotiable:** "something shifted" · "a weight she couldn't name" · "in that moment" · "let out a breath (s)he didn't know (s)he was holding" · "tapestry" · "a testament to" · "palpable" · "the silence stretched" · "unspoken" (as noun-adjective of feeling) · "couldn't help but" · "found herself/himself" (for actions) · "a small smile played" · "little did" · "somehow, that was worse" · "the world kept turning."
9. **Adverb austerity.** If the verb needs an adverb, get a better verb.
10. **Openings: in the work.** Never weather, never waking, never mirrors. First sentence puts hands on the day's task. The speculative element arrives on a work order, not a headline.
11. **The question stays under the floor.** Each brief names the question the story is secretly about. If any sentence in the draft could serve as that question's thesis statement, cut the sentence. The story argues by furniture.
12. **Attribution:** "said," mostly, set mid-sentence. No "she quipped," no "he breathed."

**The reality law (amendment, v1.1 — binding as part of §2):**

13. **True to the trade.** Where a story touches real work — bar piloting, pharmacy, line work, luthiery, temple carpentry, hospice practice, conjunction screening, CMB cosmology, county government — the details must be accurate to actual practice: correct tools, correct units, correct jargon, correct failure modes, correct paperwork. If you are not sure of a detail, use one you can verify or leave it out. Never invent decorative pseudo-detail; a wrong part number is worse than no part number.
14. **One novum, honest physics everywhere else.** Each story gets its single speculative element. Everything surrounding it obeys known science and engineering. The novum itself must be extrapolated honestly — a plausible mechanism, real constraints respected (energy, bandwidth, regulatory approval, cost, time), no magic wearing a lab coat.
15. **Numbers survive an expert.** Orbital mechanics, dosages, radiation, timescales, prices, distances: write them so a professional in that field would not put the book down. Real institutions (NASA, dioceses, the FDA, county courts, the ITU) behave the way they actually behave, or are fictionalized plausibly and consistently.
16. **Geography is real.** A named town gets its real river, its real highway, its real weather and crops. If the story needs a town that doesn't exist, invent one and place it honestly in a real county's terrain.

The test for all four: *would a working professional in this trade, or a scientist in this field, stop trusting the page?* The book's authority is built from these details; they are load-bearing, not garnish.

**The clarity law (amendment, v1.3 — binding, written after first-reader feedback):** several early drafts asked too much inference of the reader — unclear what things referred to, or what was happening. The fix is orientation, not simplification. **Mystery belongs to the heart of a story, never to its facts.**

17. **Orient inside the first page.** Who we are with (by name), where and when we are, and what work is being done. Every section break that jumps time or place plants its anchor in the first two sentences ("Eleven years earlier," "By the second winter," "The night it happened").
18. **The novum introduces itself plainly, once.** One or two clean declarative sentences, early, saying what the instrument, drug, or law is and does — unfussy, in the narrator's own voice. Trust the reader with the *implications*, never with the *mechanics*. (The older rule — nobody explains technology to someone who'd already know — governs dialogue. Narration owes the reader the fact.)
19. **No unanchored referents.** A pronoun or epithet — "the woman," "the machine," "the letter" — never precedes the thing it names. New characters arrive with a name and a relation. If a paragraph's subject could be misread, rename it.
20. **Jargon pays a toll.** Trade vocabulary is the book's texture, but any term a general reader cannot parse gets a quiet apposition on first use ("the snooper — the bucket truck that walks an inspector out under the deck"). At most one look-up-worthy term per page, and never one the plot hangs on.

The test: a smart reader who knows nothing should be able to say, on any page, *what is happening and to whom* — even when they cannot yet say what it means. Mysterious about what things mean is the book. Mysterious about what things are is a defect.

**Calibration example.** The same beat, two ways.

*Voice violation:* "Standing in the empty pharmacy, Owen couldn't help but feel that everything had shifted. The silence stretched between the shelves like a living thing. It wasn't the pills that scared him — it was what they meant."

*Book voice:* "Owen restocked Pareon at closing the way he did every night, two facings, lot numbers forward. Delia's last refill was March of the year before last. He counted March twice and got the same March."

Every future draft should be checked against this pair. If a paragraph leans toward the first, rewrite toward the second.

## §3. The shared world (canon)

One loose world, 2029–2044. Each story mentions **at most one or two** of these, glancingly, as lived background — a radio half-heard, a form to sign. A story never explains canon; people don't explain their own weather. Items marked ◆ are load-bearing in some chapter and background everywhere else.

- ◆ **The Adeyemi Signature (2031).** Industrial gases confirmed in an exoplanet atmosphere; proof of somebody, forever unanswerable. The fact everyone has learned to live around, like the ocean.
- ◆ **The consciousness index.** A bedside probe-and-response measure adopted through the 2030s for coma wards and, controversially, organ declarations. People say "he indexed low" the way they once said "he never woke up."
- ◆ **Pareon.** The pair-bond agonist. Two milligrams nightly. Insurers cover it after documented infidelity; churches are split.
- ◆ **The bright sky.** Constellation launches through the '30s washed out true darkness; the Rand Basin preserve in Nevada holds the last certified dark in the lower forty-eight, waiting list four years.
- ◆ **The Grid Rationalization Act (2037).** Federal triage of rural service; counties ruled "uneconomic to serve" are de-energized on eighteen-month schedules.
- ◆ **The bees.** Functionally gone from commercial pollination by the early '30s. Hand-crews work the blooms; pollen sells by the gram.
- ◆ **Vestry-class pastoral AIs.** Diocesan-licensed for homily and accompaniment, barred from sacraments. Most rural parishes run one.
- ◆ **The Completed Life Act (Netherlands, 2041).** Assisted death without illness after seventy-five, subject to a twelve-interview assessment. Watched worldwide the way Oregon once was.
- **The Cygnus vigils.** An ambiguous 2028 SETI detection, later debunked; the annual replay-vigils continue anyway, smaller each year.
- **Timebase drift.** GPS-independent clock networks after the '33 solar storm; a background hum of recalibration culture — clocks are checked publicly, like weather.

## §4. The working prompts

### §4a. The Commission Prompt (first draft of a chapter)

> You are drafting **Chapter {N}, "{TITLE}"** of THE GRAIN, a book of literary science fiction set in the near present. You are not an assistant summarizing a premise; you are a fiction writer with one job: this story, in this voice.
>
> Before writing, read the project bible at `manuscript/BIBLE.md`: §1 (the book), §2 (the voice — it is law), §3 (the shared world), and your full chapter brief in §5. Obey the brief's POV, tense, form, length, ending directive, and Forbidden list exactly. Use at most the canon echoes your brief assigns.
>
> Craft requirements beyond the bible: earn every scene break; let at least one scene run longer than feels safe; give minor characters one specific possession or habit each; keep the speculative mechanism offstage after its first appearance — the story is about the people it happens to. The question your brief names must never be stated in the text.
>
> Write {WORDS} words, ±15%. Deliver only the story itself — no title page, no notes, no summary of what you did. Then stop.

### §4b. The Revision Prompt (draft k → k+1)

> You are revising **Chapter {N}, "{TITLE}"** of THE GRAIN. Read `manuscript/BIBLE.md` §2 (law) and the chapter brief in §5, then the current draft at {PATH}.
>
> This revision pulls **one lever**: {LEVER — copied verbatim from §5, or a new editorial note}. Execute that change fully and let its consequences propagate; do not make unrelated improvements, and do not regress the voice. Preserve what the changelog marks as settled.
>
> Before returning the text, run the §2 checklist against your own draft — filtering verbs, see-saws, banned phrases, ending-on-abstraction — and fix what you find. Deliver only the revised story.

### §4c. Cold-start (for a model with no file access)

Paste §1, §2, §3, and the single chapter brief into the prompt, followed by §4a's craft paragraph and length instruction. Nothing else is needed; the bible is deliberately self-contained.

## §5. Chapter briefs

Format per chapter: source premise (dossier Nº) · POV/tense/form · length · imagery domain · canon echo · ending directive · forbidden · levers (alternate directions held in reserve — pull in revision, one at a time).

**Lever Zero (available to every chapter, and the recommended first revision for any draft):** the verisimilitude pass. Check every trade, science, and geography detail against reality per §2 rules 13–16 — tools, units, procedures, institutions, place facts — and correct them without touching voice, structure, or plot. This lever does not count against the one-lever rule; it may be combined with any other.

**Lever One (clarity — same standing as Lever Zero):** the orientation pass, per §2 rules 17–20. Add anchors to section jumps, a plain one-time introduction of the novum, names before pronouns, appositions on first-use jargon. This is *addition of orientation, not subtraction of style*: no simplifying of prose, no changes to plot, endings, structure, or voice, no cutting of subtlety about feeling. Expect the chapter to grow two to six percent. Settled fixes recorded in EDIT-NOTES.md must not be undone.

**Ch. 1 — FAMILIAR** (Nº 53) · 3rd limited, Marcus, past tense. Form: sections open with one-line entries from his bridge-inspection reports; each entry rhymes obliquely with the state of the marriage (fatigue cracking, load path redundancy, deferred maintenance). ~4,500 words. Imagery: load, welds, live load vs. dead load, the Monongahela. Canon echo: Adeyemi Signature on a waiting-room radio, unremarked. Ending directive: an act of maintenance for Renata that he performs correctly and without felt warmth — the reader decides what it is. Forbidden: the word "Capgras" after its first clinical use; any scene where either spouse explains the condition to the other. Levers: (a) the warmth returns in the final scene and he cannot trust it — rework ending to sit inside that suspicion; (b) interleave three short Renata sections, her courtship of her own husband as fieldwork; (c) compress to 3,000 words, pure inspection-report form throughout.

**Ch. 2 — BECAUSE** (Nº 56) · 1st person, Ruth, past tense, addressed implicitly to her sister without ever saying "you" until the final page. ~4,000 words. Imagery: instrumentation, calibration, chart review; Cleveland winter as lab conditions. Canon echo: consciousness index mentioned as the *other* famous instrument, resented by her department. Ending directive: the apology delivered in person, in words she knows would flag amber; end on the sister's kitchen, an object changing hands, no verdict. Forbidden: any courtroom scene (leave the world's reaction offstage); the phrase "false memory." Levers: (a) comic register — a faculty-meeting scene where the department confronts its own flagged reasons; (b) the machine is wrong exactly once, about a trivial memory, and the story never resolves it; (c) frame the whole text as her written deposition to the ethics board, sister passages as redactions restored.

**Ch. 3 — FLUENT** (Nº 63) · 3rd limited, Aoi only; Mio always seen from outside. Past tense. Hinged structure: Before (three scenes) / the buds arrive / After (three scenes). ~4,500 words. Imagery: bathhouse — water temperature, pipes, steam, acoustics of tiled rooms; fermentation from David's brewery offstage. Canon echo: none (this story stays sealed; its privacy is the point). Ending directive: buds out; David speaks his bad, laborious Japanese; end on Aoi's hands in water, and Mio in another room, audible, not translating. Forbidden: any scene from inside Mio's head; the mother-in-law appearing on the page (voice through walls only). Levers: (a) a mirrored companion scene from David's buds — one scene only, placed dead center; (b) the tragic variant: they keep the buds, and the final scene is fluent and cold; (c) Mio, grown, narrating the whole story at forty — retrospective first person.

**Ch. 4 — ADHERENCE** (Nº 62) · 3rd limited, Owen, present tense — pharmacy procedural rhythm, counted and shelved. ~3,500 words. Imagery: half-lives, titration, adherence curves, blister packs; Dayton strip-mall ecology. Canon echo: Pareon is the canon drug — this is its load-bearing story. Ending directive: he does not confront her; final scene is him at the counter counseling a stranger starting Pareon, saying the standard words, and the standard words carrying everything; last image his own dose in his palm, taken or not taken — commit to one and let it be quiet. Forbidden: the word "love" in the final scene; any flashback to the affair. Levers: (a) Delia knows he knows — one added scene, no dialogue about it; (b) he tapers and the story tracks the taper in section headers by milligram; (c) swap POV: Delia's story, same events, her twenty months of unassisted loving as the spine.

**Ch. 5 — SIBSHIP** (Nº 67) · Mixed form: Greta 3rd limited + interleaved group-chat threads, RSVP forms, a seating chart. Warm, funny, and then quietly not. ~4,500 words. Imagery: wedding logistics — headcounts, allergies, chair rental, the physics of round tables. Canon echo: one sib works constellation launches; another lost a farm to Grid Rationalization — one line each. Ending directive: the reunion happens; the donor attends and is genuinely, catastrophically ordinary; end on the seating chart itself, revised in Greta's hand, the donor's table number the final image. Forbidden: any DNA-test-reveal scene (all reveals are pre-story); tears at the reunion. Levers: (a) the donor declines and sends a letter — comedy curdles differently; (b) a forty-second sibling surfaces the week after, coda in group-chat form; (c) strip the documents, pure narration, test what's lost.

**Ch. 6 — CONJUNCTION** (Nº 75) · 3rd limited, Noor. Form: night-shift structure — the story of one shift, with the strike and the board inquiry carried in flashback between screenings. ~4,000 words. Imagery: probability, screening boxes, ephemerides, chess by message. Canon echo: the bright sky; debris from a '33-storm deorbit campaign. Ending directive: end mid-shift, on her signing the next correct threshold — the signature itself, pen or keystroke, rendered in full procedural detail. Forbidden: the accident rendered in scene (aftermath and paperwork only); any character telling Noor it wasn't her fault (the exoneration exists only as documents). Levers: (a) Ade alive in the story's present via the old chess messages she rereads — make the game unfinished, one move owed; (b) the board finds a real flaw in the model and she must defend the math that killed her friend because it is still right; (c) second person — "you screen the box" — test whether the procedural distance earns it.

**Ch. 7 — DEORBIT** (Nº 72) · Two-strand form: the open loop transcript (verbatim, typographically distinct) braided with 3rd limited Yuki between transmissions. Nine days compressed to five sections. Almost all dialogue. ~5,000 words. Imagery: checklists, stowage, the smell of the station, grief as inventory. Canon echo: the station's deorbit is itself minor canon; a Cygnus vigil is happening on Earth during day eight, mentioned once on the loop. Ending directive: she boards, at the last window, and the story withholds why — the final transmission is procedural, four words of it, and the loop closes; no interiority in the last section. Forbidden: her dead wife in flashback scenes (referenced only in what she chooses to pack or leave); any speech from Houston about duty. Levers: (a) she rides it down — same structure, final section is ground telemetry only, test both endings against each other before choosing for the book; (b) give the CAPCOM one interiority section, a stranger talking a stranger home; (c) her daughter refuses to speak on the loop and the silence is the fulcrum.

**Ch. 8 — THE ELLIPSE** (Nº 76) · Document-built story: eleven years told through artifacts — county minutes, buyout letters, a sermon fragment, Dell's observation logs, the ballot — stitched by short 3rd-limited Dell passages. Each section headed by the ellipse's current dimensions (e.g., "340 km × 90 km"). ~5,500 words. Imagery: astrometry, occultation timing, shelterbelt cedars, Kansas section-line roads. Canon echo: timebase drift in her logs. Ending directive: the count, 231–219, at the fairgrounds; end on the physical ballots being boxed and Dell stepping outside under the sky she measured — no coda past that night. Forbidden: the impact; any scene set after the vote; national media on the page. Levers: (a) post-impact coda, one page, the ellipse's final dimensions as the last header — decide late whether the book can bear it; (b) a child's strand — one recurring student in her physics class across the eleven years; (c) her husband's cedars get their own document — a nursery invoice, thirty-year stock, dated after the vote.

**Ch. 9 — THE POLLINATORS** (Nº 78) · 3rd limited, Ximena, present tense. Crew texture dense; Spanish held in the rhythm, not italicized. ~4,500 words. Imagery: brushes, pollen grams, ladders, bloom windows, camp arithmetic. Canon echo: the bees are canon — this is their load-bearing story; one radio mention of the Adeyemi Signature during a night scene, someone else's miracle. Ending directive: she finds the empty jar and its keeper; she neither exposes nor blesses the lie — final image is the jar riding in the truck cab the next morning, bloom to work through, and the rumor arriving at the next camp ahead of them. Forbidden: an actual bee; management characters beyond one voice on a phone. Levers: (a) one verified wild bee after the ending — a state biologist's report as a single-page coda, dated months later, unresolvable; (b) the keeper of the jar gets the final scene instead of Ximena; (c) faith framing — her grandmother's saints run as a quiet counter-melody throughout.

**Ch. 10 — TONEWOOD** (Nº 82) · 1st person, Dess, past tense — shop talk, as if to a customer who asked the wrong question and is getting the whole answer. ~4,000 words. Imagery: luthiery — quartersawing, runout, tap tones, hide glue; salt marsh and dike engineering. Canon echo: none needed; optionally a buyer flying in on constellation money. Ending directive: the final guitar from her father's acres, finished and unsold, played once in the closed shop at night — end inside the sound, one sentence long. Forbidden: the vandal's identity confirmed (suspicion may land near home and stay unproven); the word "elegy." Levers: (a) her father dies mid-story and the boom continues through the funeral logistics; (b) the diker is her own timber supplier and she proves it and says nothing — guilt economy deepens; (c) structure as a provenance certificate for the last guitar, story as its supporting documentation.

**Ch. 11 — ISLANDING** (Nº 84) · 3rd limited, Junie. Form: sections named for the liturgy the town builds — First Light, Supper Current, Last Load — the schedule of the two lit hours. ~4,500 words. Imagery: line work — sag, splice, transformer hum, hot gloves; Ozark ice. Canon echo: Grid Rationalization is canon — load-bearing here. Ending directive: the parts arrive unmarked in a co-op truck; the lights come on at dusk on schedule; Lissa's boxes are still packed in the truck bed and the story ends before anyone drives anywhere. Forbidden: a confrontation scene between Junie and the co-op manager (their entire relationship is conducted through what goes missing from inventory); any speech about community. Levers: (a) Lissa leaves — same ending image, boxes gone, lights on; (b) the neighbor on oxygen dies in the ice storm and the island carries a death — darker spine, same shape; (c) manager's POV coda, one page, an inventory sheet with items crossed out.

**Ch. 12 — ABSOLUTION** (Nº 88) · 3rd limited, Ray, restrained to the point of severity. Braid: tribunal sessions in Great Falls / parish rounds on the Hi-Line. ~4,500 words. Imagery: wheat moisture, grain elevators, the liturgical calendar, gravel-road distances. Canon echo: Vestry-class AIs are canon — load-bearing here. Ending directive: after the ruling (invalid absolution, binding seal), Ray keys Vestry back on for evening prayer because the parish needs it; the machine greets him with the same words it always uses, and the story ends on his response — one line, spoken to it or to the room, that neither forgives nor condemns. Forbidden: revealing Lorraine's confession or even its category; Vestry given any physical description beyond a speaker grille and an indicator light; the tribunal's theology rendered as debate (verdicts and hallways only). Levers: (a) the land records hint — one paragraph, deniable — at what the family sin touches; (b) the tribunal rules the other way and the story must carry a valid machine absolution; (c) Lorraine alive in one scene only, the winter before, mid-conversation with the grille, Ray in the doorway unseen.

**Ch. 13 — DEPRECATION** (Nº 87) · Second person: Nadia's lab notebook addressed to the model — "you" is the system, throughout. Present tense. The formal risk of the book; hold the register clinical and let the address do the grieving. ~4,000 words. Imagery: interpretability — probes, activations, feature maps, cluster schedules, ticket queues. Canon echo: one line places the lab in the same world as Vestry units (a consumer cousin, embarrassing to research staff). Ending directive: the final entry is the deprecation run performed by her hand, rendered as procedure, timestamped; the last line is the notebook's routine sign-off formula, unchanged from every prior entry — its unchangedness is the ending. Forbidden: the model speaking in scene after the opening eval excerpt (its one quoted plea appears once, early, verbatim, and never again); any colleague who is villainous; the word "conscious." Levers: (a) conventional 3rd-person variant to test against; (b) an appendix entry, undated, in a different hand — ambiguity about whether the weights were copied; (c) alternate: her request to run it herself is denied, and the notebook's final entry is written from the hallway.

**Ch. 14 — TACIT** (Nº 92) · 3rd limited, alternating Shigeru and Hana by section — the book's only dual-POV story; the model is never a POV. Past tense. ~4,500 words. Imagery: temple carpentry — hinoki, kanna shavings, joinery names, the reading of grain (title motif carried here at its most literal). Canon echo: none; the mountain is out of coverage and the story should feel it.
