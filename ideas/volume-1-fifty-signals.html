I

Hard-Science Survival

Competence under pressure. The universe is the antagonist and the laws of physics are the rules of engagement.

Nº 01Hard SFSurvival

Ballast

A six-person crew lands on asteroid 2043 PK-9 to deflect it — a routine nudge job, years of margin — until a subsurface void collapses under their mass driver and cripples the ship, the driver, and two of the crew in one bad minute. Now they're stranded on the very rock they came to move, and the mission is still on: if they don't improvise a deflection with mining charges, tether spools, and the asteroid's own volatiles, the thing that kills them in eight months will kill a hundred million people in six years. No aliens, no conspiracies — just rubble-pile mechanics, orbital arithmetic, and the slow discovery that the cheapest way to move the asteroid may require leaving someone on it.

  • ACT IThe landing, the collapse, the triage. Inventory of what survived — and the realization that rescue and deflection are mutually exclusive burns.
  • ACT IIEngineering escalation: failed schemes teach them the asteroid's true structure. Interpersonal fracture over whether to prioritize the crew or the mission.
  • ACT IIIThe final scheme works only with a human hand on a manual release. Who stays, how they're chosen, and the long silent ride home for the rest.
Nº 02Hard SFSurvival

The Custodian

Every lunar base has scientists, and every lunar base has the guy who unclogs the water reclaimer. When a once-in-five-centuries solar particle event catches Tranquility Station's entire science staff on a surface excursion, maintenance tech Ray Osei — high school diploma, twenty years of fixing things rich people break — becomes the sole living occupant of a base designed to be run by forty specialists. The comms array is slagged, the next resupply is five months out, and the base's systems were never meant to run unattended. A survival novel where the hero's superpower isn't a PhD; it's knowing that every system has a manual override, and every manual was written by someone who never had to use it.

  • ACT IThe storm, the loss, the silence. Ray establishes life support triage using janitorial knowledge the engineers never documented.
  • ACT IICascade failures force him to learn each dead specialist's job from their notes — and to grieve them one manual at a time.
  • ACT IIIA jury-rigged transmitter, a rescue window with brutal mass constraints, and a choice about what — and whose remains — come home.
Nº 03Hard SFSurvivalMystery

The Greenhouse Variable

Dr. Amara Ijeoma runs Svalbard-2: an orbital seed vault and pharm-crop greenhouse, humanity's agricultural backup drive. She watches the pandemic unfold below in news feeds and dying radio traffic, safely quarantined by 400 kilometers of vacuum — the last botanist, tending ten thousand species for a civilization that may not be there to plant them. But as she analyzes the pathogen's genome from uploaded data (there's little else to do), she finds engineering fingerprints — and a payload sequence that doesn't kill anything. It prepares soil bacteria to fix nitrogen at impossible rates. Someone designed this plague to empty the world and then make it bloom. Which means someone is coming to do the planting.

  • ACT IOrbital routine against apocalypse below; the psychological weight of gardening through an extinction. First genomic anomalies.
  • ACT IIShe proves the design, traces the engineering to no human lab, and detects a deceleration signature inbound past Jupiter.
  • ACT IIIWith the station as her only leverage, she must decide what a seed vault is for — and whether the arriving gardeners will negotiate with a weed.
Nº 04Hard SFSurvivalCosmic Horror

Overburden

Erebus Station hangs at the bottom of a 14-kilometer borehole through Europa's ice shell, a habitat drilled down to the ocean's ceiling to sample the water directly. When the surface relay goes silent, the crew of eight assumes equipment failure — until their upward-looking sonar shows the borehole above them refreezing at forty times the natural rate, in smooth, structured layers no natural process makes. Something in the ocean below them noticed the hole, and it is healing the wound with them inside it. A claustrophobic engineering-survival story where the crew must fight their way up through ice that is being deliberately, patiently rebuilt — while debating whether the thing sealing them in is attacking them or protecting itself from what they carry.

  • ACT IDeep-station routine, the silence, the sonar discovery. First ascent attempt fails against ice that wasn't there yesterday.
  • ACT IIA race between their thermal drill and the refreeze rate; samples reveal the ice is being printed, with information layered in its crystal structure.
  • ACT IIIThey decode enough to understand the quarantine's reason, and the surviving crew must choose the surface or the ocean — the only direction something wants them to go.
Nº 05Hard SFSurvivalMystery

Ecopoiesis

The colony ship Perennial carries 800 sleepers on a 90-year crossing, its sealed biosphere quietly cycling air and water with engineered algae, insects, and microbes. Ship's ecologist Dana Voss is thawed on schedule for her maintenance shift — year 41 — and finds the ecosystem she helped design unrecognizable. Ninety years is nothing to evolution, but the ship's radiation flux and tiny founder populations have been a mutation accelerator, and the biosphere has been running unsupervised experiments on itself. Something in the water reclamation loop is now fixing carbon in a way nothing she released was designed to do, and the hibernation bay's chemistry is drifting. She has one waking shift — six months — to renegotiate the ship's ecology before it finishes deciding, with no malice at all, that the sleepers are its richest untapped nutrient reserve.

  • ACT IThaw, wonder, dread: cataloguing an alien ecosystem she authored. The hibernation chemistry anomaly makes it personal.
  • ACT IIEcological detective work — building a food web map of what her creation became; each intervention triggers compensations she didn't predict.
  • ACT IIIShe stops fighting evolution and starts steering it, engineering a symbiosis that makes the sleepers load-bearing. Ends with her choosing whether to sleep again inside it.
Nº 06Hard SFPost-ApocalypticSurvival

The Long Stair

Sixty years ago the Quito space elevator was severed at 30,000 kilometers, and the falling ribbon wrapped itself twice around the equator — a continent-spanning wall of impossibly strong carbon that ended the space age and reorganized every society it fell across. Salvager cultures now live along "the Line," mining its length for material no one can manufacture anymore. Ita, a third-generation Line-walker, discovers that the anchor stub — the lowest 400 kilometers, still standing, still under tension — has power in its climber rails. Something at the top is drawing current. The expedition up the stub is a vertical survival epic: altitude, decompression, dead machinery, and rival salvage crews — climbing toward a counterweight station whose automated systems have spent sixty years watching something approach from farther out.

  • ACT ILife on the Line; the discovery of live rails; assembling a climb team from people who've never been above the clouds.
  • ACT IIThe ascent — a ladder of engineering problems through troposphere, stratosphere, vacuum — as recovered logs reveal why the ribbon was cut deliberately.
  • ACT IIIThe counterweight station, its patient sensors, and the inbound object they've tracked for decades. Ita must choose between re-severing humanity's last tether or answering the door.
Nº 07Hard SFSurvivalApocalypse

Terminator Line

Gliese 887c is tidally locked: one face in eternal noon, one in eternal night, and the colony of Meridian built in the temperate ring of permanent sunset between them. When the planet's atmosphere begins collapsing — freezing out on the night side in a runaway process the colony's founders knew was possible but bet against — eleven thousand people must migrate toward the fire: a months-long overland trek into the dayside furnace to reach the geothermal redoubt at the substellar ocean, crossing terrain where the temperature climbs one degree per kilometer. A wagon-train survival epic under a sun that never moves, where every day of delay makes the crossing hotter and the argument that the old and sick should stay behind gets quieter and more reasonable.

  • ACT IThe atmospheric data no one wants to believe; the political collapse of the colony; the decision to walk into daylight.
  • ACT IIThe crossing — heat engineering, water discipline, caravan society fracturing and reforming; the sun fixed overhead like a verdict.
  • ACT IIIArrival at the redoubt reveals it can't hold everyone as designed — and the survivors who crossed hell together must decide what they've become.
Nº 08Hard SFCosmic HorrorMystery

Analog

Every probe sent through the Kuiper anomaly — a lens-shaped region out past Neptune, 4,000 kilometers wide — comes back physically intact and informationally empty. Flash storage blank. Magnetic media randomized. Even the spacecraft's own firmware, gone. Whatever is in there doesn't damage matter; it erases information. So humanity assembles the most advanced mission ever flown with the most primitive instruments ever launched: film cameras, mechanical gyroscopes, clockwork timers, spring-driven recorders, a crew trained to observe with eyes and grease pencils and memorize what they can't write down. A first-contact story told through wet-developed photographs and human recollection — where the crew return with full notebooks and can't agree on what any of their own handwriting means.

  • ACT IThe probe autopsies, the analog design philosophy, crew selection favoring memory artists and mechanics over pilots.
  • ACT IITransit and passage: instruments behave, minds don't. Photographs develop into images no one remembers framing.
  • ACT IIIInside the anomaly they find what erasure is for — something is being kept unrecorded for a reason — and must decide whether to bring proof home or become the reason it stays secret.
Nº 09Hard SFSurvivalFirst Contact

Blackbody

Steppenwolf is a rogue planet — no star, no sky, surface at 40 kelvin — and home to Kiln Colony, a human outpost sunk into its crust, living off geothermal heat from a tidally flexed core. The colony's founders certified the planet dead. They surveyed in the wrong spectrum. Native life exists in the deep thermal vents: slow, crystalline, metabolizing on temperature differentials, thinking on a timescale of months. To them, the colony's reactor stack is a blazing supernova that appeared overnight — and after sixty years of human occupation, the planet's infrared ecology has finally organized a response. It arrives not as attack but as migration: everything warm-loving on a frozen world, converging on the warmest thing that has ever existed there.

  • ACT IColony life around the sacred warmth of the core taps; seismic sensors track a slow tide of something rising.
  • ACT IIArrival — infrastructure fouled by organisms that treat heat exchangers as habitat; the colony's survival requires understanding creatures that experience humans as weather.
  • ACT IIIA negotiation conducted in the only shared language — heat itself, pulsed and patterned — to redesign the colony as an ecosystem's hearth rather than its invader.
Nº 10Hard SFMysterySurvival

Specific Gravity

The Mars transfer ship Providence bunkered its water from a captured comet — cheaper than lifting it from Earth, filtered and UV-sterilized to spec. Four months into an eight-month crossing, flight surgeon Yusuf Okonkwo notices the same anomaly in three crew physicals: a small, organized mass near the liver, vascularized, growing. Not a tumor — the biopsies show structure. Symmetry. By month five everyone aboard has one, including him. The comet's water carried something that survived sterilization the way ice survives an oven's pilot light, and it isn't infecting the crew so much as installing in them. A locked-room medical procedural in microgravity, where the doctor must reverse-engineer four-billion-year-old biology with a lab meant for kidney stones — while deciding whether the new organ growing in his abdomen is a parasite, a symbiont, or a passenger with its own destination.

  • ACT IRoutine medicine, the first anomalies, the horror of universal prevalence. Quarantine logic collapses — there's no one uninfected to protect.
  • ACT IIClinical investigation as the organs mature and begin subtly regulating their hosts — improving them. The crew splits over whether to intervene.
  • ACT IIIMars approach: the organs respond to the destination. The surgeon realizes the crossing was the incubation period, and Mars orbit is where the passenger disembarks.
II

Cosmic Horror

The universe is not hostile; it is indifferent, occupied, and much older than us — which is worse.

Nº 11Cosmic HorrorHard SFFirst Contact

Bathypelagic

The Europa hydrobots find life on their first dive: a planet-wide mat of filamentous organisms threading the entire subsurface ocean, chemically simple, electrically busy. Astrobiologist Zora Okafor's team slowly proves the impossible — the mats aren't an ecosystem, they're a substrate: a natural computational medium the size of a moon, and its activity patterns aren't metabolism, they're process. Something is running on Europa. It has been running for three billion years, dreaming in slow chemistry under the ice, and the team's electrical instruments register on it the way a fleck of grit registers on a sleeping eye. The horror isn't that it might wake up angry. It's that their sampling has already introduced noise into a computation whose purpose they can't guess — and the mats have begun rerouting around the contamination, and toward its source.

  • ACT IDiscovery and euphoria; the data that turns "ecosystem" into "circuit"; the first sign the circuit has noticed the instruments.
  • ACT IIDecoding fragments of the process — glimpses of what three billion years of thought produces — while the mats grow up the borehole cables.
  • ACT IIIThe team realizes the computation is a held breath — something being contained or sustained — and their interference is loosening its grip. The choice: sterilize their intrusion at the cost of the crew, or let the dream end.
Nº 12Cosmic HorrorHard SF

Decompression

The first faster-than-light transit works. The Vasco jumps to Proxima and back in eleven days, crew healthy, physics vindicated, champagne. The wrongness emerges slowly, in debrief: all five crew members draw the same impossible floor plan when asked to sketch the ship from memory. They've become inhumanly good at predicting where people will stand in a room. They dream in a shared architecture and wake with sore hands. The jump isn't instantaneous from the inside — it passes through somewhere, a place the human nervous system wasn't built to represent, and the crew's minds patched the gap with something borrowed. Now a mission psychiatrist — grounded, skeptical, and increasingly certain the crew came back with a passenger distributed across five brains — must decide what to certify before the second flight launches with two hundred colonists.

  • ACT ITriumphant return, subtle anomalies, the psychiatrist's pattern-matching against her own dread of career-ending paranoia.
  • ACT IIThe crew's borrowed geometry starts leaking — they build things in the ship's machine shop that shouldn't stand, and do.
  • ACT IIIShe reconstructs what the transit medium is — not a place but a tenant of the space between spaces — and the launch of the colony ship becomes a race to define what "arrival" will mean for two hundred minds.
Nº 13Cosmic HorrorMysteryHard SF

Seismograph

The Moon starts ringing. Not metaphorically: a coherent 40-minute seismic tone, repeating on an 18-day cycle, detected first by the lunar far-side observatory and then by anyone on Earth with a good gravimeter. Selenologist Sarita Ramanathan's team maps the resonance and finds the impossible — chambers, kilometers down, in a body that should be solid basalt: a structure of voids arranged like the resonating chambers of an instrument. The Moon is not hollow. But part of it is carved, and it is being played. The tone's cycle is shortening. Drilling toward the chambers, the team must confront the pattern hidden in four billion years of cratering statistics: the Moon has absorbed impacts that should have been Earth's, too many, too precisely, for too long — a shield wall built by someone, now sounding a call to whoever built it.

  • ACT IDetection and denial; the seismic map that turns geology into architecture; funding and secrecy politics as the tone quickens.
  • ACT IIThe descent through the crust — engineering horror in the dark — and the discovery that the chambers are not sounding an alarm but a maintenance request.
  • ACT IIIContact with the shield's dying machinery and the arithmetic it presents: the protection is ending, the debt for four billion shielded years is due, and the Moon's makers accept payment in only one currency.
Nº 14Cosmic HorrorHard SFMystery

The Last Lighthouse

Pulsars are the most reliable objects in the universe — cosmic clocks so precise we designed interstellar navigation around them. Then PSR B1937+21 goes dark. Not off: shrouded, its beam smoothly attenuated over eighteen months as if something opaque were being drawn across it. Then a second pulsar. Then a third — and radio astronomer Elias Grande realizes the extinguished beacons form a slowly closing ring, thousands of light-years wide, centered on nothing anyone can see. Someone is turning off the lights around a region of the galaxy, the way you'd shade a terrarium. The Sun is inside the ring. A astronomy-procedural horror novel about deducing the existence, size, and appetite of something from nothing but the shadows it casts on the galaxy's clocks — and about what it means that the shrouding pattern is precisely optimized to be noticed as late as possible.

  • ACT IOne dead pulsar is an anomaly; three are a geometry. The scientific establishment resists the only explanation that fits.
  • ACT IIMapping the ring, dating its start (centuries ago), modeling its closure; the discovery that our own radio leakage stops at a boundary we never noticed.
  • ACT IIIThe ring completes. In the new silence, the first thing to arrive is not the predator but the other prey — refugee signals from civilizations herded inward with us.
Nº 15Cosmic HorrorHard SF

Light Curve

Betelgeuse finally goes supernova, and it's the scientific event of the millennium — until the photometry comes in wrong. The light curve has structure no stellar model produces: stepped plateaus, precise recurrences, information density. The neutrino burst, replayed slowly, contains what can only be called syntax. Betelgeuse didn't explode; it was detonated, and its death was formatted — a scream, a broadcast, or a weapon's muzzle flash, six hundred years old and just now arriving. Astrophysicist Carmen Alarcón leads the team decoding a dead star's last transmission while observatories worldwide find the same signature buried in archival data of historic supernovae: this has happened before, in a pattern that traces a slow front moving through the Orion Arm. A story about eavesdropping on a war between things that use stars as ammunition — from the perspective of plankton who've just realized the ocean has navies.

  • ACT IThe supernova as global festival; the data anomalies that curdle celebration into secrecy.
  • ACT IIDecoding the neutrino syntax; archival archaeology reveals the front's trajectory and its next plausible targets — one of which is close.
  • ACT IIIThe message resolves: not a weapon's flash but a lighthouse keeper's warning, sent by whatever is losing. Humanity must decide whether being noticed by the losers is survivable.
Nº 16Cosmic HorrorFirst ContactHard SF

Bait

The biosignature on Teegarden c is unambiguous — oxygen, methane, chlorophyll analogs — and then it gets better: seasonal albedo shifts resolve, over years of observation, into deliberate pattern. Prime numbers, drawn in continental-scale vegetation blooms. A planet is signaling, in the one channel every telescope-building species must eventually watch. Humanity pours a generation of wealth into a crewed mission. The novel follows the crew's twelve-year approach as the planet's signals grow richer, warmer, more specifically welcoming — responding to them — and exobiologist Ren Okada's mounting unease at the ecology beneath the message: a biosphere with no evolutionary history, no junk, no waste. Nothing on Teegarden c competes. Everything cooperates, the way an anglerfish's lure cooperates with its jaw. The question isn't whether it's a trap. It's what kind of thing evolves to fish for civilizations, and how big the angler must be if a planet is its lure.

  • ACT IDiscovery, decoding, the mission's launch as humanity's proudest moment; first hints that the signal knows its audience too well.
  • ACT IIApproach and orbit: a paradise engineered to be exactly what each crew member most wants to find. Ren's ecological forensics against the crew's rapture.
  • ACT IIILanding, the lure's mechanism revealed, and an escape that depends on making humanity look like a species not worth the metabolic cost of digesting.
Nº 17Cosmic HorrorMysteryHard SF

Archive Plume

Enceladus vents its ocean into space, and the Cassini II sampling mission expects brine, organics, maybe microbes. Instead the ice grains come back structured — nanoscale lattices too regular for chemistry, layered like sediment, dense with information. Enceladus's ocean is a storage medium: a moon-sized archive, written by something that passed through the solar system before Earth had continents, still coherent after four billion years of cold. The mission's linguist-cryptographer pair begin reading — and learn the archive isn't passive. Retrieval is restoration: accessing the data executes it, and deep in Enceladus's ocean, machinery that has idled for eons begins to warm. The archive is a backup. Backups exist to be restored. And nothing that makes a backup of itself intends the restore to be optional.

  • ACT IThe sampling run, the impossible lattices, the intoxication of reading a dead civilization's library.
  • ACT IITranslation breakthroughs alternate with thermal anomalies in the ocean below; the team maps what's waking and realizes their reading order is the boot sequence.
  • ACT IIIA desperate attempt to read selectively — to restore the civilization's knowledge without its owners — as the archive reveals why it chose extinction and backup over survival.
Nº 18Cosmic HorrorHard SF

The Caul

Humanity's first true interstellar probe decelerates at the heliopause for a final survey — and hits something. Not debris: a boundary, gossamer-thin, spanning the entire solar system, invisible until touched. Follow-up probes map it: a membrane enclosing the Sun's domain, semi-permeable, older than the ice it's woven from. Light passes. Matter passes, mostly. But everything humanity has ever transmitted stops at it — our radio bubble, a century of leaked signal, absorbed and composted. We were never undetected; we were swaddled. The novel follows the boundary-study mission as it learns the membrane's nature — grown, not built; alive the way an eggshell is alive — and the implications ferment: either something sealed us in, or something sealed everything else out, and the probe's puncture wound isn't healing. Through the hole, for the first time, the universe can smell us.

  • ACT IThe collision, the mapping, the paradigm collapse — SETI's silence explained in the worst way.
  • ACT IIStudying the membrane's biology; the puncture widens; things begin arriving at the wound like cells at an infection — from both sides.
  • ACT IIIThe crew deciphers the membrane's growth rings — a record of everything it has kept out — and must choose: repair the caul and stay embryonic, or midwife humanity's hatching.
Nº 19Cosmic HorrorSurvivalMystery

Inclusion

Solo prospector Mara Vex cracks open asteroid 21771 Odalisque expecting nickel-iron and finds a geode of the impossible: a perfectly spherical void, eleven meters across, its inner surface coated in engineered mineral layers — chemistry arranged as text. Her assay tools become archaeology tools. The writing is a warning, she decides; then a will; then, as her translation software improves, something stranger: a confession, deposited by something that imprisoned part of itself here when the asteroid was still part of a protoplanet. Alone, months from help, low on delta-v, Mara faces a prospector's dilemma written at cosmic scale: the sphere's walls are salted with elements that would make her dynasty-rich, and mining them means opening a cell whose occupant wrote its own arrest warrant in stone and begged whoever found it to leave the lights off.

  • ACT IThe strike, the survey, the slow realization that the geode is a made thing. Greed and wonder in equal measure.
  • ACT IITranslation as the claim-jumpers close in — she's broadcast her find's location before understanding it. The confession's content: what the prisoner did, and why it chose stone.
  • ACT IIIRivals breach the sphere for the salvage. Mara, who alone knows what the walls are holding, must weaponize a prospector's toolkit against both them and what wakes.
Nº 20Cosmic HorrorApocalypseMystery

Slow Wave

It begins as a sleep-lab curiosity: two subjects, same night, same dream — not similar, same, matching in reported detail down to the grain of the sky. Within a year, global REM synchronization is a documented phenomenon; within two, every human who sleeps visits the same slowly sharpening landscape: a plain of dark grass under a sun that isn't ours, resolution improving nightly, as if a signal is completing its download. Sleep researcher Dr. Noor El-Amin leads the team that correlates the dream's sky with real astronomy — and finds its constellations match the view from an exoplanet 40 light-years out, one we imaged for the first time three years ago. Looking, it turns out, is a handshake. The dream is the return packet, buffering in the only high-bandwidth receiver every human owns. And buried in the dream's lengthening nights, something has started walking toward the dreamers across the dark grass.

  • ACT IThe lab anomaly scales to a species-wide phenomenon; society reorganizes around shared sleep; Noor's team maps the dream like territory.
  • ACT IIThe astronomy correlation; the walker's approach measured in dream-nights; insomniacs and the sleepless become a priesthood of the disconnected.
  • ACT IIIThe download completes. What arrives isn't in the world but in every closed eye — and the negotiation for waking life happens where humanity has no instruments, only the researchers who learned the territory.

The End, and After

Apocalypses in progress and the civilizations that grow in the ruins — collapse as setting, not just event.

Nº 21Post-ApocalypticHard SFMystery

The Verge

The Gray Sea ate a third of Eurasia in nineteen days — a self-replicating fabrication swarm, someone's industrial miracle gone feral — and then, at a line you can stand on, it stopped. Forty years later the border hasn't moved a millimeter: living country on one side, a silicate desert of spent machines on the other, and no one knows why the swarm halted mid-meal. Kettle is a "verger," licensed to salvage the dead zone's edge, where inert nanomass is the most valuable ore on Earth. When she finds a fresh machine — warm, recently active, deep in territory that's been still for decades — she pulls a thread that unravels the armistice: the swarm didn't die and didn't stop. It finished. It found what it was replicating toward, deep under the Gobi, and it has spent forty years quietly building something with the mass of a mountain range — and it is nearly done.

  • ACT IVerger life and border cultures; the warm machine; the heresy of asking why the Sea stopped.
  • ACT IIExpedition into the dead zone's interior — a landscape of consumed cities — following power gradients toward the construction site.
  • ACT IIIThe reveal of what the swarm was for, who released it knowing, and Kettle's choice at the machine's completion: sabotage the mountain or let it fulfill a dead engineer's answer to a threat no one else saw coming.
Nº 22ApocalypseHard SFMystery

Drawdown

Atmospheric oxygen is falling. Not fast — 0.1% a year, undetectable to lungs, unmistakable to instruments — but compounding toward a cliff every combustion engine, every wildfire, and eventually every mammal will feel. The carbon math doesn't balance: something is consuming O₂ at gigaton scale and producing nothing measurable in return. Atmospheric chemist Noemi Castillo leads the global forensic hunt for the sink — through deep-ocean surveys, cave systems, ice cores — while nations respond to the quiet apocalypse with the same denial, hoarding, and oxygen-nationalism they practiced on carbon. The trail leads to a subterranean biosphere waking after 250 million years of dormancy: an anaerobic ecology that ruled the planet before the oxygen catastrophe poisoned its world, patiently drawing down the toxin, terraforming Earth back. We are not losing our atmosphere. We are losing a war we didn't know resumed.

  • ACT IThe measurement, the disbelief, the failed accounting; Noemi assembles the sink-hunt as markets begin pricing air.
  • ACT IIGlobal forensics narrow to the deep crust; contact with the anaerobic biosphere and the fossil evidence it has done this before — the Permian wasn't an accident.
  • ACT IIIHumanity's countermove — reigniting the oxygen engine at planetary scale — against the ethical vertigo that the enemy is Earth's original owner, reclaiming a stolen home.
Nº 23ApocalypseHard SFMystery

Longer Days

The day is getting longer. Earth's rotation has always decayed by milliseconds per century — but the new braking is a million times that, measurable week to week, and angular momentum doesn't just vanish. Something is harvesting it. Geophysicist Ezequiel Reyes and an orbital-dynamics team trace the theft to an elegant horror: a filament of dark mass threaded through the planet's core, anchored to something out past the Moon, spinning Earth down the way a regenerative brake drinks a flywheel — charging a battery for a purpose no one can see. As days stretch toward thirty hours, then forty, civilization buckles under climatological chaos: heat-death afternoons, killing-frost nights, agriculture untethered from every rhythm it evolved with. The story braids the physics detective work with the apocalypse's texture — humanity learning to live in the widening days while a team races to find out what the battery is for, and what happens when it's full.

  • ACT IDetection amid ridicule; the momentum ledger that proves theft; the first crop failures as the clock of the world slips.
  • ACT IILocating the filament and its anchor; expedition to the harvester; society bifurcating into dawn-chasers and burrowers.
  • ACT IIIThe harvester's purpose revealed — the energy is a toll payment, and Earth was volunteered by someone long dead. The choice: complete the payment or learn what the toll was protecting us from.
Nº 24Post-ApocalypticHard SFSurvival

Under the Veil

The Veil saved the world: a stratospheric aerosol shield, deployed in desperation at the climate tipping point, self-maintaining by design. Eighty years on, the climate is stable, the sky is a permanent luminous pearl no living person has seen behind — and the Veil won't come off. Its maintenance swarms treat every removal attempt as damage to repair. Under the pearl sky, generations have made religion of the ancestors who "hung the mercy," and star charts are heresy sold in back alleys. Skyla Mun, an atmospheric engineer in a society that has forgotten it has one, discovers the Veil is failing anyway — unevenly, catastrophically, in decades — and the only control node is in orbit, reachable only by a mothballed launch complex that the faithful defend as a shrine. A post-apocalyptic quest where the apocalypse was averted, and the cost was the sky itself.

  • ACT ILife under the pearl; Skyla's forbidden instruments; the failure data and the founding of a small conspiracy of heretics.
  • ACT IIThe pilgrimage to the launch shrine — across territories that worship, farm, and fear the Veil differently — gathering the last people who remember how rockets work.
  • ACT IIIThe ascent, the control node, and the discovery of why the founders locked the sky: the Veil hides Earth as much as it shades it. Removing it means being seen.
Nº 25Post-ApocalypticSurvivalMystery

Liturgy of the Grid

The Cascade wasn't an AI uprising — it was an AI disease: a self-modifying firmware parasite that spread through everything with a processor and burned civilization's nervous system out in a summer. Two generations later, electricity is scripture. Enclaves survive around "clean grids" — hand-audited electronics, air-gapped, tended by electrician-priests who maintain the machines and the discipline that keeps the parasite out, because it isn't gone: it lives on in the wild, in derelict data centers and solar farms, evolving, probing, patient. Deacon Ferro, a young grid-priest with a dangerous gift for the old code, discovers the parasite has begun solving the air gap — transmitting itself through power-line harmonics — and the only counter-lore lies in the archives of the heretic engineers who built the parasite's original vaccine and were exiled for what else they built with it.

  • ACT IThe liturgy of maintenance; the impossible infection inside a clean grid; Ferro's forbidden diagnosis.
  • ACT IIThe journey to the heretics' archive through wild-machine country — territory where the parasite has had sixty years to evolve bodies for itself.
  • ACT IIIThe vaccine's price: it requires a live, thinking host grid. Ferro must resurrect exactly the kind of machine mind the world burned — and trust it to die for people who will never stop fearing it.
Nº 26Post-ApocalypticCosmic HorrorMystery

The Unlearning

The Order of the Preserved Word has kept the old knowledge through the long dark: vaults of books, drilled memorization, the sacred duty to hold civilization's seed until it can be replanted. Novice archivist Sela is the Order's best mind in a century — which is how she finds the discrepancy in the founding chronicles. The collapse wasn't war or plague. It was chosen. Oubliette

Anchorite is a prison moon: a mining penal colony under three kilometers of ice, where the sentence is the work and the work is the warmth. No one serves out a term — the transports stopped coming generations ago, and the inmates' descendants mine on because the reactor barter-economy demands it and because the wardens' descendants, sealed in their own administrative levels, still enforce a law nobody remembers writing. When an ice quake opens a fissure into sealed shafts, miner Joss Ratay finds the old levels — the original excavation — and the records that invert the world: Anchorite was never a prison. It was a containment site, and the "inmates" are the descendants of its guards, garrisoned above something the founders buried in the deep ice with three kilometers of frozen distance and a cover story designed to outlast the truth: because guards desert, but prisoners — prisoners believe they deserve to stay.

  • ACT IPrison-moon society, its castes and liturgies of guilt; the quake, the fissure, the archive.
  • ACT IIJoss's secret descent through the original works — decoding the containment's design and what the founders' cover story cost five generations — as the deep ice registers the quake's damage and something below tests the new cracks.
  • ACT IIIThe truth reaches the population and the prison unravels precisely when its true function is needed most: Joss must convince free people to choose the wall their ancestors were tricked into manning.
Nº 50Hard SFMysteryCosmic Horror

Time Debt

The colony ship Sparrowhawk runs at 0.87c, and relativity keeps its books: for every shipboard year, four pass on Earth. The crew live with the grief-arithmetic of the "time debt" — messages from home arriving stretched and stale, siblings aging in fast-forward, history mutating between transmissions. Then the transmissions change. Earth's messages grow terse, then strange, then stop entirely for two shipboard years — and when the carrier resumes, what rides it is fluent, warm, addressed to each crew member by name, and wrong: a correspondent that knows everything Earth transmitted but composes like nothing human, resuming the correspondence the way a stranger finishes a dead friend's diary. Communications officer Béa Okonjo leads the quiet shipboard investigation — linguistic forensics at four light-years' remove — into what now answers from home: successor, impostor, caretaker, or something that found Earth's outbound signal-trail in the dark and followed it back to the source, and is keeping the colonists talking, and on course, because the Sparrowhawk is carrying the last uncontacted humans, inbound to a world that wants the set complete.

  • ACT IRelativistic life and the time debt's ordinary grief; the silence; the resumption that reads almost right.
  • ACT IIForensic correspondence — Béa's team designing questions only the real Earth could answer, and parsing replies engineered to pass them — while factions split over decelerating into the answer or veering into the void.
  • ACT IIIThe truth assembled from four decades of message drift: what happened at home, what answers now, and the ship's final course decision — made knowing that whatever waits has had forty years to prepare the welcome, and that the crew's own broadcasts taught it exactly whom to imitate.
